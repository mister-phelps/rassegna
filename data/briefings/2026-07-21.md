# Daily Briefing — Tuesday, 21 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**Parliament's China report stalls as MEPs head to Beijing amid pressure controversy**
An eight-member European Parliament delegation from the Foreign Affairs Committee arrived in China on Tuesday, hoping to break a months-long deadlock over a critical report on EU-China relations. As [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/parliaments-china-conundrum/) reports, Beijing warned MEPs ahead of the vote that adopting the draft in its current form would have "consequences" — a warning that MEPs from other groups say the Socialists and Democrats (S&D) have heeded: the group wants to delete language describing China as a "strategic rival" and delete the assessment that China "offers few, if any, benefits to European companies." The S&D denied being leaned on by Beijing, saying they were "not aware of any warning from any Chinese official." Finnish EPP MEP Mika Aaltola warned: "When Beijing warns of 'consequences' before a report is even adopted, the warning itself is the operation." The delegation will meet Foreign Minister Wang Yi and senior CCP officials; their findings are expected to shape any autumn compromise.

**The EU's regulatory ambitions are crumbling under pressure from all sides**
Writing in [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/20/ue-un-blocco-in-frantumi/), Mario Seminerio offers a bleak diagnosis of the EU's capacity to act as a unified bloc. On the methane regulation — which from 2027 will require importers to certify the emissions provenance of all gas — the US, Qatar, Algeria and Nigeria have sent a joint letter demanding delays, arguing that the traceability requirements are technically impossible given the fragmented structure of American gas production. Several EU member states including Germany, Italy, the Netherlands and the Czech Republic have also called for postponement. The European Commission, Seminerio argues, is "trying to save the principle by sacrificing the substance," preparing soft guidelines rather than formal revision. Meanwhile, the latest round of Russia sanctions has stalled: Greece is blocking adoption over exemptions for shipping giant Dynagas, which has transported 30 million tonnes of Russian LNG since the invasion; Austria, Portugal, Germany, Italy and France have all advanced separate carve-out demands. The US has also opened a Section 301 investigation into German pharmaceutical pricing, with similar pressure being applied to France. Seminerio concludes: "I continue to be strongly pessimistic about Europe's future, and I hope to be wrong."

**EU hits AliExpress with record €550 million fine under Digital Services Act**
The European Commission on Monday imposed a record €550 million fine on AliExpress — the platform owned by China's Alibaba — for failing to prevent the sale of illegal and harmful products, according to [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/burnham-launches-labours-last-stand/). This is the largest penalty ever levied under the Digital Services Act, which requires very large platforms to assess and mitigate risks associated with illegal content and goods. The Reuters Technology Roundup placed the fine at $629 million.

**Solar power supplies a quarter of EU electricity for the first time**
Solar energy provided 25% of the EU's electricity in June, the first time that threshold has been reached in a single month, [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/burnham-launches-labours-last-stand/) reports, citing data from Ember Energy. Solar generated a record 52 TWh, surpassing the previous monthly high of 47 TWh set in May.

## UK Politics

**Andy Burnham sworn in as Prime Minister, pledges to "rewire" Britain**
Andy Burnham, the former Mayor of Greater Manchester, was formally sworn in as the United Kingdom's new Prime Minister on Monday, becoming the country's seventh leader in a decade, as [Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.thomsonreuters.com) and [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/burnham-launches-labours-last-stand/) report. (Forecast describes him as the sixth leader in a decade; the difference reflects whether Burnham himself counts.) In his first speech outside Downing Street, Burnham promised: "We will make this moment a circuit breaker for Britain, bringing forward the biggest changes in the last forty years." Within hours he had sacked several ministers close to his predecessor Keir Starmer — including Chancellor Rachel Reeves and Deputy PM David Lammy — and made his first calls to world leaders, inviting US President Trump to visit Manchester and reaffirming solidarity with President Zelenskyy of Ukraine. [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/burnham-launches-labours-last-stand/) frames Burnham's challenge as existential for Labour: "If Labour fails, British politics are in danger of following the United States and electing the most right-wing government it has ever seen," with Reform UK, led by Nigel Farage, having swept recent elections across the country. Writing today in [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/21/quest-ce-que-le-manchesterisme-la-doctrine-andy-burnham/), Karel Williams analyses what he calls "Manchesterisme" — Burnham's doctrine combining decentralisation of power, rejection of neoliberalism, and a "business-friendly" socialism — but questions whether such ambitions can survive the realities of central government.

**Hamish Falconer named UK's new Europe minister after Thomas-Symonds sacking**
As part of Burnham's reshuffle, Hamish Falconer — a former civil service diplomat and Middle East minister — has been appointed minister for European and intergovernmental relations, jointly spanning the Cabinet Office and the Foreign Office, according to [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/parliaments-china-conundrum/). His predecessor Nick Thomas-Symonds, who had overseen the Starmer government's EU reset, was dismissed.

## Foreign Policy

**US-Iran war enters tenth consecutive day of strikes; nuclear sites targeted**
The US–Iran conflict escalated sharply over the weekend and into Monday, with American forces striking Iranian targets for a ninth or tenth consecutive day — different sources count from different reference points — while Iran's Revolutionary Guards claimed retaliatory strikes on US military assets across the Middle East, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com), [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/parliaments-china-conundrum/), and [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/20/les-etats-unis-se-preparent-ils-a-une-nouvelle-escalade-en-iran/) all report. Le Grand Continent provides the most granular account: Iran accused the US of striking the Darkhovin nuclear construction site on Sunday, and the city of Bushehr — home to Iran's only operating nuclear power plant — on Monday morning; the IAEA announced an investigation. The US has been moving additional military assets from European bases — F-16s from Germany and F-35s from the UK — to the Middle East, and has sent dozens of additional refuelling aircraft to Israel. On Sunday, Trump declared: "Iran has suffered significant damage. Militarily, they've lost almost everything." Meanwhile Yemen's Houthis announced a naval blockade on Saudi Arabia, per [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/parliaments-china-conundrum/), opening a potential further front. Bloomberg, cited by Playbook, reported that US forces struck Iranian targets overnight on Sunday, marking the tenth consecutive day. Total US military deaths since the start of the conflict have reached 18.

**Russian troops surviving an average of 20 to 30 minutes on the Ukrainian front**
CIA Director John Ratcliffe confirmed on 15 July that the average life expectancy of a Russian recruit arriving at the front in Ukraine is currently "estimated between 20 and 30 minutes," attributing the figure to AI-enabled drone warfare, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/20/un-soldat-russe-vit-en-moyenne-20-a-30-minutes-sur-le-front-ukrainien/). Russian losses in the first half of 2026 were eight times higher than Ukrainian losses — compared to a ratio of two to three times over 2022–2025 — per a CSIS study. Russia faces mounting recruitment difficulties: only 8,000 drone operators were trained in the first half of 2026, against a target of 34,400. Le Grand Continent reports growing speculation in Russia about a new mobilisation wave after September's legislative elections, with the Kremlin having digitised military registration to tighten enforcement on draft-eligible men.

**Syria's rapid normalisation is outpacing consolidation, analysts warn**
[Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/burnham-launches-labours-last-stand/) carries analysis from former CIA officer William Usher arguing that Syria's rehabilitation — sanctions lifted, President Ahmed al-Sharaa received at the White House, full diplomatic relations restored after Macron's 7 July Damascus visit — is "running well ahead of its consolidation." While Macron was still in Damascus, explosions wounded at least eighteen people in the capital. Usher writes: "The world is treating al-Sharaa as the settled ruler of a functioning state, but he isn't. The further recognition gets out ahead of reality, the more leverage the West throws away at exactly the moment it could still buy something with it."
