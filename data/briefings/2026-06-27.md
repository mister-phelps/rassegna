# Daily Briefing — Saturday, 27 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**Hitler-salute photograph rocks the Bundestag**
A photograph appearing to show AfD parliamentary group leader Martin Reichardt performing a Hitler salute has dominated debate in the Bundestag this week, per the Politico Berlin Playbook newsletter. AfD figures, including Stephan Brandner, have disputed that interpretation of the image, while rivals have seized on the episode as fresh evidence of the party's extremist fringe. The row comes as Germany's mainstream parties continue to wrestle with how to respond to the AfD's rising support without appearing to legitimise it.

**Merz's pension commission proposes a 33-point overhaul**
Chancellor Friedrich Merz's Rentenkommission has put forward a sweeping 33-point reform of Germany's state pension system, according to phastidio.net's detailed analysis of the proposals. The plan includes a gradually rising retirement age, a Swedish-style funded "capitalisation" pillar layered onto the existing pay-as-you-go system, and an overhaul of the minijob regime, all driven by Germany's worsening ratio of workers to retirees. Phastidio frames the reform as Germany now following a road Italy has already travelled, while the Politico Berlin Playbook newsletter notes the proposals are already provoking pushback within Merz's own coalition, including from Bundestag president Bärbel Bas. The debate underlines how pension sustainability is becoming as live an issue in Berlin as it has long been in Rome.

**A heat dome grips Europe, exposing political fault lines**
A prolonged "heat dome" has settled over much of the continent this week, with the Thomson Reuters Weekend Briefing newsletter reporting dozens of deaths, melting roads in Germany and warped train tracks in Sweden, in a region where most homes lack air conditioning. The Euractiv newsletter notes that Brussels' Schuman roundabout has become something of a symbol of the crisis, dubbed a literal urban heat island, after Belgian prime minister Bart De Wever dismissed an EU funding request for a cooling redesign of the junction as a "national humiliation". Separately, per the Euractiv newsletter, the EU, Canada and China held their tenth such climate summit since the United States withdrew from the 1.5°C commitment under Donald Trump, as Brussels seeks to keep multilateral climate diplomacy alive without Washington.

## UK Politics

**Rachel Reeves expected to be demoted as Chancellor**
Rachel Reeves is expected to be moved out of the Treasury after two turbulent years as Chancellor, The Times reports — what would be the first such demotion of a sitting Chancellor in more than 40 years. The Times says allies of Reeves have been approached for comment as part of its reporting, while separately suggesting Number 10 is also planning a "rose garden moment" with the Liberal Democrats, hinting at a broader political realignment alongside the reshuffle. No formal reshuffle has yet been confirmed.

**Burnham seen as favourite to succeed Starmer**
Manchester mayor Andy Burnham is increasingly seen in Westminster as the favourite to succeed Sir Keir Starmer as Labour leader, according to commentator Anne McElvoy as cited in the Politico Berlin Playbook newsletter — a view echoed by The Times, which quotes Burnham arguing that the south of England is "paying the price" for the north's economic underperformance. The Times also reports growing Treasury support for bringing the Olympics to the north of England, a move that could further bolster Burnham's standing. Separately, The Times reports that Britain risks losing its senior NATO role without a further increase in defence spending.

### Also noted
- *The Times*: Donald Trump has threatened a 100% tariff on countries, including Britain, that pursue a digital tax on major US technology firms.

## Economy & Markets

**Poland's economy stages a remarkable comeback**
Poland's economy grew 3.5% in 2025 and is on course to overtake Italy's within roughly three decades, with per-capita GDP set to surpass that of France and Spain by 2035, according to the Euractiv newsletter. The Visegrad Four — Poland, Czechia, Slovakia and Hungary — now account for around 10% of EU GDP and 14% of its population, the newsletter notes, even as Poland's own fiscal position has deteriorated sharply: spending now runs at 51% of GDP against revenue of 44%, up from a roughly balanced 41%/41% split in 2019. The same newsletter flags a darker underside to the growth story — a fertility rate of around one child per woman, alongside housing costs up 117% and rents up 74% in recent years — that could complicate Poland's long-term trajectory.

**Eurozone outlook improves as digital euro advances**
The eurozone growth outlook has brightened as US-Iran tensions ease, with the IMF projecting euro-area growth of 1.1% in 2026 despite an energy-driven supply shock stemming from the Middle East conflict, according to the Euractiv newsletter. The European Parliament has backed the digital euro project, the same newsletter reports, while EU economy commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis has warned that agreeing the bloc's next long-term budget "is not going to be easy". Brussels has also proposed a sweeping simplification of EU tax rules, per Euractiv.

**Chinese electric vehicles reach one in ten European car sales**
Roughly one in ten new electric cars sold in Europe is now Chinese-made, according to [Le Grand Continent's](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/26/une-voiture-electrique-sur-dix-vendue-en-europe-est-desormais-chinoise/) analysis of Bloomberg, ACEA and OECD MAGIC database figures. The piece traces the roughly 11% market share to aggressive pricing from manufacturers such as BYD, even as EU tariffs on Chinese-made EVs remain in place. The trend underscores the limits of trade defences against fast-moving Chinese competition, and suggests Chinese brands are increasingly competing on cost and product range rather than being priced out by the tariff regime.

## Foreign Policy

**US strikes Iran after Hormuz tanker attack; fragile Israel-Lebanon deal signed**
The United States struck targets inside Iran after a cargo ship was attacked in the Strait of Hormuz, with Iran's Revolutionary Guards claiming retaliatory strikes on US-linked positions, according to the Reuters Iran Briefing newsletter. Israel and Lebanon have separately signed an initial US-mediated agreement, though the Thomson Reuters Weekend Briefing newsletter notes its precise terms — and its relationship to an earlier April agreement — remain unclear, with both Tehran and Washington trading what the newsletter calls "familiar accusations" over who broke last week's ceasefire first. The UAE has held a rare direct call with Iran stressing the importance of Hormuz security, per Reuters, while a UN agency is working to restart evacuations through the strait.

**Netanyahu faces a hawkish challenger as Eisenkot surges**
Israeli prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu faces a growing electoral challenge from Gadi Eisenkot, a former IDF chief of staff who served in Netanyahu's wartime cabinet before resigning over policy disagreements, according to the Thomson Reuters Weekend Briefing newsletter, which reports Eisenkot is surging in the polls. The Reuters Iran Briefing newsletter separately confirms Netanyahu is facing an election challenge from an ex-general, without naming him. Civil-rights groups have also warned, per Reuters, that a proposed Israeli bill on West Bank "ancient sites" amounts to de facto annexation, adding domestic legal controversy to the political pressure on Netanyahu's coalition.

**Merz rallies NATO's European Five as Trump renews "disloyalty" claims**
Chancellor Friedrich Merz has convened NATO's European Five partners — Germany, France, the UK, Italy and Poland — at the German Chancellery, according to the Politico Berlin Playbook newsletter, after President Trump renewed accusations that European allies are not pulling their weight within the alliance. The Times separately reports that Britain risks losing its senior NATO role without further increases in defence spending, suggesting London's standing within the alliance is itself becoming a live concern. The Berlin Playbook newsletter also notes doubts that Lithuania's NATO brigade can reach its target strength of 5,000 troops by 2027, a further sign of the gap between alliance ambitions and delivery.

**Venezuela earthquake death toll could exceed 10,000**
An earthquake in Venezuela has killed more than 900 people with hundreds still missing, according to the Thomson Reuters Weekend Briefing newsletter, which reports that US data models suggest the eventual toll could exceed 10,000. The scale of the disaster is emerging as a mounting political challenge for the government of Delcy Rodríguez, the newsletter notes, with rescue efforts complicated by the scale of destruction. The earthquake is shaping up to be one of the deadliest natural disasters in the region in recent memory, though official figures remain provisional.
