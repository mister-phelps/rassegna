## EU Affairs

**Russia sanctions stalled by Athens as summer deadline looms**

EU ambassadors gathered in Coreper II today for what could be a final chance to seal the 21st Russia sanctions package before the summer recess. According to the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/sanctions-season-finale/), diplomats were despondent going in — "Very difficult to predict," sighed one; "Better call Athens," offered another. Greece remains the sole holdout, seeking an open-ended exemption for Dynagas, a Greek shipping firm specialising in Russian LNG transport, against proposed restrictions on LNG transshipment services to third countries. The [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/europe-s-east/news/settlements-showdown/) notes that governments have until Thursday to strike a deal, having agreed to freeze the bloc's oil price cap to create space for a breakthrough; the Commission was expected to present an economic impact assessment today, focused on the proposed LNG ban. Austria, which also held out last week, dropped its veto despite capitals declining to compensate Raiffeisen Bank for €2.1 billion in assets seized by Russia — with a promise to revisit the question later. The package covers roughly 250 new listings, additional banks, and shadow-fleet vessels. "If the issues haven't been resolved now, when will they?" one EU diplomat wondered, as quoted by the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/sanctions-season-finale/).

**Kallas presses for Israeli settlement trade ban as Politico and Euractiv frame the deadlock differently**

At the same Coreper meeting, High Representative Kaja Kallas asked ambassadors for their positions on trade restrictions against products from illegal Israeli settlements — an attempt to gauge whether a qualified majority exists before calling an extraordinary ministers' meeting, as the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/europe-s-east/news/settlements-showdown/) reports. Three options are on the table; Kallas has said the full ban is the most popular. Belgium and the Netherlands have already announced unilateral measures. The Rapporteur notes that in unusually pointed remarks last week, Kallas appeared to accuse Commission President Ursula von der Leyen of representing Germany's position rather than the EU's — saying the Commission "now operates more like the Council." Von der Leyen has argued unanimity is required and has delayed producing a formal proposal. The [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/sanctions-season-finale/) frames the risk bluntly: an extraordinary summer session could backfire if it confirms there is still no QMV for any of the three options.

**Merz uses Spahn's resignation as an opening for a political reset**

The departure of Jens Spahn — parliamentary leader of the CDU and one of Chancellor Friedrich Merz's most important allies — is turning into an unexpected political gift, as the [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/the-blast-radius-of-the-middle-powers-theory/) reports. Spahn quit on Saturday after he and his husband acknowledged using a surrogate mother in the United States, a practice banned in Germany. Merz moved swiftly to float a cabinet reshuffle, which would let him appoint a new CDU parliamentary leader to settle the coalition and reset politically before crucial autumn regional elections.

## Italian Politics

**Italy's income tax has become a de facto wage policy — and the Parliamentary Budget Office says so**

A working paper published today by Italy's Parliamentary Budget Office (UPB), analysed at length by [Mario Seminerio at Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/22/il-phastidioso-upb-e-i-kulaki-spremuti-e-drenati/), confirms a structural anomaly with significant fiscal implications. Italian real wages per full-time equivalent fell 1.6% between 1990 and 2024 — against gains of over 30% in France and Germany and 50.5% in the United States. The UPB's central finding is that the IRPEF income tax has drifted from a redistributive instrument into an implicit wage subsidy: some 84% of employees now fall below the €35,100 annual income threshold above which today's tax system is more burdensome than in 1990, following three decades of bonuses, deductions, and fiscal credits. Seminerio argues the paper confirms that IRPEF has come to substitute for collective bargaining, compensating for wage stagnation driven by structural factors — notably an explosion of involuntary part-time work (now over 30% of employment contracts). The UPB finds that Italy's growing wage dispersion is almost entirely a composition effect of part-time employment rather than a rising skill premium, setting it apart from other advanced economies. The paper's conclusion — that this fiscal model may be approaching its limits — is pointed: using the tax system as a substitute for labour market and productivity reform carries mounting risks, including eroded incentives for skilled workers, widening horizontal inequity between different types of earners, and a narrowing fiscal base.

## Economy & Markets

**Gas prices spike to six-month high as Iran war enters second month; World Bank warns on global growth**

European benchmark natural gas prices briefly surpassed €60 per megawatt-hour on Monday — their highest level since mid-March — before settling at €59 on Tuesday, a 50% increase from the previous month, according to the [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/the-blast-radius-of-the-middle-powers-theory/). The move followed the resumption of intensive US strikes against Iran, which reached their eleventh consecutive night by Wednesday. The World Bank's chief economist Indermit Gill told [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) that escalating US-Iran hostilities could reignite inflation, push interest rates higher, and cut global growth to as low as 1.3% — down from 2.9% last year.

**Trump's tariffs have cost America 75,000 manufacturing jobs — the opposite of the promised "golden age"**

Despite sweeping tariffs across most of the world, the United States has shed 75,000 manufacturing jobs since Trump's inauguration in January 2025, according to Department of Labor data cited by [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/21/malgre-limposition-de-tarifs-sur-la-majeure-partie-du-monde-les-etats-unis-ont-perdu-75-000-emplois-manufacturiers-depuis-linvestiture-de-trump/). By the equivalent point in the Biden administration, 625,000 such jobs had been created. Factory construction spending collapsed 26.4% between January 2025 and May 2026. With a 10% stopgap tariff due to expire on Friday and Trump reportedly preparing to impose fresh levies on dozens of countries, Le Grand Continent notes that 57% of Americans now disapprove of the administration's trade policy, and 66% are dissatisfied with its impact on inflation.

## Foreign Policy

**Zelenskyy sacks top general after street protests, installs younger reform-minded commander**

President Volodymyr Zelenskyy dismissed Oleksandr Syrskyi as commander-in-chief of Ukraine's armed forces — the third such change since Russia's full-scale invasion of 2022 — naming 43-year-old Mykhailo Drapatyi in his place. The move follows Zelenskyy's earlier removal of Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, which had triggered substantial street protests across Ukraine, with one of their two main demands being Syrskyi's removal. As [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/22/qui-est-mykhailo-drapatiy-le-nouveau-commandant-en-chef-des-armees-ukrainiennes/) reports in detail, Drapatyi became a national hero in 2014 for breaking through separatist barricades in Mariupol with an infantry fighting vehicle, later contributing to the liberation of the Kherson right bank and the stabilisation of the Kharkiv front. His appointment signals a generational shift: unlike Syrskyi, whose tactical approach was shaped by Soviet-era military education, Drapatyi belongs to the generation formed by the 2014 war, is a champion of drone and unmanned systems, and has said that "an army in which commanders are personally accountable for the lives of their men is a living army." The [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/europe-s-east/news/settlements-showdown/) notes that protesters who called for Syrskyi's removal had often named Drapatyi as their preferred replacement. He faces three immediate challenges: securing the loyalty of officers aligned with Syrskyi; reforming Ukraine's mobilisation system (the average frontline soldier is now well over 40); and maintaining battlefield momentum as Russian advances slow.

**US-Iran war: Rubio leaves door open to talks as costs mount and Houthis threaten Red Sea blockade**

The conflict between the United States and Iran showed no signs of resolution on Wednesday, with Secretary of State Marco Rubio declaring that Tehran "is not serious about talks to end their war" even as he insisted the US remains willing to negotiate. According to [Thomson Reuters Gulf Currents](https://www.reuters.com/newsletters/gulf-currents/), US military casualties have risen to 18 killed and approximately 430 wounded, while thousands have died — mostly in Iran and Lebanon — according to health ministry and rights group tallies. Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth told a Senate committee the conflict has cost $37.5 billion over five months, with Trump seeking nearly $90 billion more, prompting Democratic Senator Kirsten Gillibrand to challenge him directly: "The reason why people are so upset with you is because the words you have used in the past don't add up." The [Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com) separately reports that Iran's interior minister visited Pakistan — which has been acting as mediator — and that Pakistan has privately asked Washington for a $10 billion exchange stabilisation facility, apparently linked to its mediating role. Houthi threats to blockade Saudi Arabia's Red Sea ports are threatening to open a second front, with multiple tankers already rerouting away from Yemen's coast; [Gulf Currents](https://www.reuters.com/newsletters/gulf-currents/) warns that taking on the Houthis simultaneously would "bifurcate admittedly fairly robust resources" already stretched across the Iran theatre.

**Trump moves to sign US-Saudi civil nuclear deal without standard non-proliferation safeguards**

The White House was expected to sign today a 30-year agreement authorising American firms to develop civil nuclear power in Saudi Arabia, potentially including, after a two-year study period, a uranium enrichment facility on Saudi soil. [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/22/en-signant-un-accord-sur-le-nucleaire-civil-avec-larabie-saoudite-trump-ouvre-t-il-la-voie-a-une-proliferation-nucleaire-au-moyen-orient/) reports that unlike the 2009 Bush-era deal with the UAE, Saudi Arabia would not be bound by equivalent non-proliferation conditions — notably, it would not be required to sign an additional IAEA protocol authorising intrusive inspections, nor to commit to forgoing domestic enrichment. Congress has 90 days to object. The deal raises proliferation concerns: Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman stated in 2018 that if Iran obtained a nuclear weapon, Saudi Arabia would "follow suit as soon as possible." The White House sees it as a commercial opportunity worth tens of billions to US firms, a means of crowding out Rosatom and Chinese rivals, and a way to shore up a Saudi relationship strained by the Iran war — though notably, [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/22/en-signant-un-accord-sur-le-nucleaire-civil-avec-larabie-saoudite-trump-ouvre-t-il-la-voie-a-une-proliferation-nucleaire-au-moyen-orient/) notes that unlike under Biden, the deal is not contingent on Saudi normalisation with Israel.
