# Daily Briefing — Saturday, 11 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**NATO Ankara summit projects unity despite months of internal tension**

NATO leaders concluded their two-day summit in Ankara on Wednesday by endorsing a joint declaration affirming "ironclad commitment to collective defence under Article 5," hailed by Secretary General Mark Rutte as evidence that the alliance is "so much stronger" thanks to sustained pressure on defence spending. According to the [Euractiv newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/the-brief/), the six-paragraph statement also formally recognised Ukraine as "a key contributor to transatlantic security," backed by a further €70 billion in military assistance and agreements drawing on Ukrainian drone and missile expertise. Alliance-wide defence spending rose by €121.5 billion over the past year. US President Trump, who hailed "a spirit of respect" after talks, has spent the intervening twelve months rolling out the red carpet for Vladimir Putin and threatening European partners — making the declaration's Article 5 language more symbolically freighted than usual. Euractiv frames the summit's real achievement not as keeping Trump placated but as forging "a more cohesive, more European alliance."

**EU Russia sanctions package hollowed out at French, Italian, and Greek insistence**

EU member states approved a new round of sanctions against Russia on Friday, but only after the proposal was significantly weakened, according to the [Euractiv newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/the-brief/). France, Italy, and Greece pushed successfully to strip out a Baltic-led ban on Russian soldiers entering the bloc, while Greece secured a carve-out allowing it to continue shipping Russian liquefied natural gas to non-EU states. Euractiv characterises the resulting package as "hollowed out."

**Poland-Ukraine memory war flares on Volhynia anniversary; Tusk walks a tightrope**

Today, 11 July, marks the 83rd anniversary of the Volhynia "Bloody Sunday," when Ukrainian nationalist UPA forces massacred an estimated 100,000 Polish civilians in 1943. Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk used the occasion to declare the killings a genocide and announce plans for a "Wall of Memory" in Warsaw bearing the name of every identified victim — while simultaneously urging Poles not to let remembrance "serve hatred." According to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/11/346025/), the speech comes after a sharp deterioration in bilateral relations: President Zelensky renamed an elite unit after "heroes of the UPA" in May, prompting Polish President Karol Nawrocki to strip him of the Order of the White Eagle — Poland's highest decoration — in June. At the Ankara summit, a bilateral meeting ended with Nawrocki declaring the UPA question "non-negotiable" and Zelensky calling the discussion "constructive." On 8 July, the European Parliament adopted an amendment to Ukraine's accession report regretting Zelensky's decision as "not in conformity with European values," while reaffirming support for Ukrainian membership. New exhumations of Polish victims are scheduled to begin at Ostrówki and Wola Ostrowiecka on 13 July.

**EPP manoeuvre forces through CSAM-scanning extension despite majority opposition**

A controversial extension of EU rules permitting tech companies to scan their platforms for child sexual abuse material (CSAM) has passed the European Parliament, according to the [Euractiv newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/the-brief/). The law — which had expired in April after the Council refused to include privacy safeguards — was revived at the request of Parliament President Roberta Metsola (EPP) and timed deliberately to coincide with July's lower parliamentary attendance, raising the bar for rejection to an absolute majority. On Thursday, a simple majority voted against the extension, but fell short of the threshold required — handing the EPP its goal despite majority MEP opposition.

**Farage pays himself £50,000 rent from Reform funds — one penny below declaration threshold**

Nigel Farage is paying himself £50,000 in rent for a studio space at one of his homes — precisely 1p below the amount that would trigger a mandatory declaration to the Register of Members' Interests, according to reporting by [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk). Farage this week resigned his Clacton seat to force a by-election, which he will contest against the satirical candidate Count Binface, who has received a formal endorsement from Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch. The Times describes the contest as "surely one of the strangest polls in history."

**Burnham's succession looms alongside an unsustainable welfare bill**

Andy Burnham's emergence as Prime Minister-in-waiting frames a difficult inheritance on public spending. According to [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/10/regno-unito-la-sindrome-del-welfare/), a Department for Work and Pensions review published this week concluded that Britain's Personal Independence Payment (PIP) disability benefit is "not fit for purpose" and fiscally unsustainable. PIP — a non-means-tested, tax-free benefit for working-age people with long-term physical or mental health conditions — has ballooned since Covid to 4 million recipients in England and Wales, costing £32 billion this financial year and projected to reach £41.5 billion by 2031. Mental health conditions — anxiety, ADHD, stress — now account for 39% of claims, double the 2021 figure. Burnham's core electoral base lies in precisely the de-industrialised northern constituencies most affected, with one in ten adults receiving PIP in towns such as Wigan. Burnham has indicated a preference for therapy and treatment over cutting cash transfers, while also ruling out scrapping the state pension triple lock. Phastidio notes that past attempts to compress welfare costs have consistently seen them re-emerge elsewhere in the system.

## Economy & Markets

**Is Europe a failing football team? Euractiv on the continent's economic predicament**

A long-form essay in the [Euractiv newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/the-brief/) uses the analogy of a struggling football team to characterise the EU's economic condition: "its star performers, Germany and France, are seriously underperforming," its managers "have lost the dressing room of public opinion," and neither copying the US model (federal budget at 23% of GDP) nor China's export-oriented approach (dependent on welfare-suppressing policies) is feasible. The piece argues that Europe's response — deregulation, militarism, and punitive tariffs — amounts to "firm tackling" against opponents with superior technical mastery, echoing England's catastrophic misjudgement before the 6–3 defeat to Hungary in 1953. The essay is pessimistic about the available options, framing any credible path as "kicking the ball backwards" — necessary, but unlikely to produce a goal and capable of producing own goals.

**SK Hynix shares rise 14% in Nasdaq debut; CEO warns of 2027 memory shortage**

South Korean chipmaker SK Hynix's US-listed shares rose 14% on their Nasdaq debut following a $26.5 billion share sale, according to the [Reuters Weekend Briefing](https://www.reuters.com), signalling sustained investor appetite for AI-related semiconductor stocks despite recent volatility. SK Hynix's chief executive told Reuters he expects the global memory market to experience "its worst-ever supply shortage" in 2027 as AI-driven capital expenditure accelerates.

## Foreign Policy

**Trump declares Iran ceasefire over; demands open Strait of Hormuz as Khamenei heir remains unseen**

US President Trump declared this week's ceasefire with Iran over while simultaneously stating the two sides had agreed to continue negotiations — demanding that Tehran guarantee safe passage for shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. According to the [Reuters Iran Briefing](https://www.reuters.com), the United States is formally seeking an Iranian pledge on the Strait, while a UN agency document calls on countries to "reject Iran's efforts to control Hormuz." Renewed fighting has already pushed US petrol prices higher. Compounding Tehran's difficulties, Mojtaba Khamenei — son and successor to the assassinated Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — has not been seen publicly since reportedly being wounded in US-Israeli airstrikes, a situation the [Reuters Weekend Briefing](https://www.reuters.com) describes as "becoming a liability for the Islamic Republic," with ordinary Iranians increasingly noticing his absence.

**Russia consolidates its Syrian footprint despite Assad's fall**

Syria's new authorities are expected to allow Russia to open a commercial logistics hub at its Tartous naval base within days, through a Syrian firm in partnership with Russian companies, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/10/la-russie-consolide-sa-presence-en-syrie/). The hub would initially handle 250,000 tonnes monthly of Russian grain, steel, oil, and other goods — and could serve as a platform for exports to Iraq and Jordan. Satellite imagery from May had already confirmed Russian military cargo deliveries to the Hmeimim air base, indicating Moscow retained a military presence after Assad's fall. The development sits in apparent tension with Damascus's simultaneous Western rapprochement: French President Macron visited Damas on 6–7 July — the first Western head of state to do so since the change of government — and Syrian leader Ahmed al-Sharaa attended the Ankara NATO summit on 8 July, where Trump pledged to remove Syria from the US terrorism-sponsor list by 22 August. Syria remains dependent on Russia for 85% of its wheat imports.
