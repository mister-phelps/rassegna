# Daily Briefing — Thursday, 4 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**Weber eyes Bavaria as EPP expands its dominion**
Manfred Weber, the European People's Party chief, has set off fresh speculation about his political future after sending an open letter to around 800 senior CSU figures criticising — without naming — Bavarian Minister-President Markus Söder, a fellow CSU member since their days in its youth wing. According to the Politico Playbook, seven party officials told the newsletter that the letter was a deliberate political signal: insiders believe Weber is not preparing a bid for the Bavarian premiership but is instead angling for the CSU party leadership itself, potentially controlling German politics while remaining in Brussels. Meanwhile, Weber continues to expand the EPP family: Montenegro's Prime Minister Milojko Spajić and his Europe Now Movement are expected to join the EPP at its assembly in Vienna on 30 June, and Hungary's Péter Magyar and his Tisza party have begun the formal process of joining, per the same source. Belgian Prime Minister Bart De Wever's N-VA is described as the EPP's "next target," which would bring the party's prime minister tally in the European Council to 13.

**Hungary clears path for Ukraine's EU accession — with conditions**
Péter Magyar announced late Wednesday that Budapest had struck a deal with Kyiv on protecting the rights of the Hungarian minority in Transcarpathia, allowing the EU to open the first formal accession negotiating cluster with Ukraine in mid-June. As the [Politico Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/) reports, the development was widely celebrated in Brussels and EU capitals — but Magyar's statement buried a condition that should temper enthusiasm: Hungary will support Ukraine's accession only if Kyiv closes all 33 accession chapters within 10–15 years, subject to a legally binding referendum. [Euractiv's Rapporteur newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/) also noted that Magyar met French President Emmanuel Macron in Paris on Wednesday, with both leaders agreeing to modernise a bilateral partnership dating back to 1991. A formal agreement is due to be signed in Budapest on 23 October.

**EU launches tech sovereignty package to reduce US and Chinese dependencies**
The European Commission on Wednesday unveiled a four-pronged technology sovereignty package aimed at reducing Europe's reliance on US and Chinese technology. As the [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/what-trumps-next-tariffs-might-look-like/) details, the package would require public administrations to stress-test their dependence on foreign cloud services, revise the EU Chips Act to scale up industrial capacity, leverage open-source technology to avoid vendor lock-in, and mobilise an estimated €200 billion in private and public investment for data centre capacity by 2036. A senior Commission official was quoted by Euractiv saying of the US and Chinese reactions: "Are the Chinese going to be happy? No. Are the Americans going to love it? No. Do we care? Also no — this is about bolstering European sovereignty." The [Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) noted the package was described by one Commission official as "Tech Liberation Day," though the briefing cautioned that true technological independence from US Big Tech "will take longer to attain."

**France pushes to make Channel crossings an EU matter**
With small-boat crossings into the UK down 37% so far in 2026 compared to the same period last year, France is nonetheless pushing for the Channel route to become formally recognised as an EU–UK migration challenge rather than a bilateral French responsibility, [Euractiv's Rapporteur newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/) reports. The European Commission is preparing an action plan on the Channel route, expected later this month, likely to focus on tighter border controls and action against smuggling networks. Many EU capitals remain unconvinced. Complicating matters, migrants are increasingly departing from the Belgian coast — a shift Paris hopes will persuade other capitals that the problem has truly become European in scope. Mediterranean countries including Italy, Spain, Cyprus and Greece remain opposed to any "one-in, one-out" return arrangement with the UK, fearing it would ultimately burden them with migrants returned from Britain to Europe.

**EU to sanction Chinese companies for aiding Russia; China hardball analysed**
The EU's diplomatic service is set to sanction four Chinese companies accused of supporting Russia's war effort in Ukraine, the Politico Playbook reports exclusively. The move comes as EU–China trade tensions intensify. [Euractiv's Red Thread newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/section/china/) this week mapped Beijing's "economic coercion playbook" — targeting asymmetric dependencies across wine (Australia), rare earths (Japan), tourism (South Korea), and supply chains (Lithuania) — and warned Brussels that Europe's own exposure in batteries, critical minerals, pharmaceuticals, semiconductors and drones makes it vulnerable to similar pressure. Meanwhile, France fined Shein €22 million and the Commission fined Temu €200 million under the Digital Services Act; a €3 levy on small parcels from Chinese online retailers is due from 1 July. EU Trade Commissioner Maroš Šefčovič meets Chinese trade envoy Li Chenggang in Paris today ahead of Commerce Minister Wang Wentao's planned Brussels visit on 28–29 June.

**Brantner calls for Germany to abandon strategic denial**
Franziska Brantner, leader of Germany's Greens, has published a doctrine piece in [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/04/allemagne-deni-brantner/) arguing that "Europe is alone" for the first time since 1945 and that European strategic autonomy — long a French obsession and a source of British irritation — has become an unavoidable necessity. Brantner argues Europe has money but no doctrine, and that Germany's emergence as a military power is "one of the most significant developments" in European security since the end of the Cold War — but that its value depends entirely on how firmly it is anchored in a European framework. The piece, adapted from an Oxford lecture delivered on 14 May, is framed around the concept of a *Nie wieder allein* ("never again alone") strategy and calls on Berlin to develop a coherent roadmap for the new era.

## Italian Politics

**Meloni as Europe's far-right interlocutor: the "dividend" grows**
The Politico Playbook's Brussels edition devoted significant space to what it calls the "Meloni dividend" — the Italian Prime Minister's growing role as a bridge between the mainstream European centre-right and the far right. The Commission's decision to allow some green energy investments to be excluded from EU fiscal calculations, which Meloni had requested in a letter to Commission President von der Leyen, is seen as a tangible reward. Two diplomats and an EU official told the Playbook that Meloni could serve as a model for France's National Rally and its presumptive presidential candidate Jordan Bardella, demonstrating that there is more to be gained from working with Brussels than against it. Sweden and the other "frugals" are furious, arguing the concession undermines EU fiscal rules.

**Police arrest two over Calabria farm workers' killing**
Italian police have arrested two Pakistani nationals over the killing of four farm workers whose burned bodies were found inside a minivan at a petrol station in Calabria, according to [Euractiv's Rapporteur newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/). The suspects face multiple aggravated murder charges. An Afghan survivor told investigators the victims had been threatened at gunpoint and forced to work without pay; prosecutors are examining whether Italy's *caporalato* labour exploitation system was involved.

## Economy & Markets

**OECD warns of potential recession if Iran conflict drags into 2027**
The OECD published its Economic Outlook on Wednesday, sharply downgrading its global growth forecasts in light of the US–Israeli war on Iran and its disruption to the Strait of Hormuz. [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/03/plusieurs-pays-pourraient-entrer-en-recession-en-2027-si-la-guerre-en-iran-se-prolonge/) reports that even in the optimistic scenario — a conflict resolved by mid-2026 — global growth is projected at 2.8% this year, down from 3.4% in 2025, recovering to 3.1% in 2027. If the war continues into next year, growth falls to 2.1% in 2026 and 1.8% in 2027, with several major economies at risk of recession. Gas prices in Asia have risen 81% since the start of the conflict in late February; European gas is up 43%, compared to only 6.3% in the US. OECD Chief Economist Stefano Scarpetta called the war "the defining factor for the global economic outlook," describing a prolonged Hormuz blockade as potentially the worst growth shock in 40 years, outside COVID and the 2008 financial crisis.

**Trump's next tariff wave: high headlines, important fine print**
The Trump administration has released two successive late-night reports setting out its plans to reimpose tariffs after the Supreme Court struck down the legal basis for much of its original regime. The [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/what-trumps-next-tariffs-might-look-like/) reports that one document proposes a 25% tariff on Brazilian goods, while a second would impose up to 12.5% duties on 60 countries accused of failing to block goods made with forced labour — though the EU, UK, Mexico and Canada face lower rates under these proposals. Countries that have already struck deals — including Japan ($550bn investment commitment), the EU (mutual tariff reduction under the "Turnberry deal"), and nine others — may still face additional measures from a separate investigation into excess manufacturing capacity. Italian MEP Brando Benifei described the process as "another attempt to artificially manufacture a new justification for tariffs, undermining the rules-based trading system."

**Strategy's bitcoin model shows signs of structural strain**
[Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/04/strategy-lo-schema-ponzi-che-divoro-se-stesso/) publishes a detailed analysis of the fragility of Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy), Michael Saylor's bitcoin accumulation vehicle, which sold a mere $2.5 million worth of bitcoin — its first sale since 2022 — causing a disproportionate market reaction. Strategy has been responsible for approximately 70% of net bitcoin purchases in 2026, buying 171,238 bitcoin since January, more than the entire global mining network produced in the same period. The company's share price has fallen over 70% from its peak, and analyst Mario Seminerio argues that the model functions only when bitcoin is rising: a prolonged bear market would force dividend suspensions or a disorderly liquidation of the firm's 843,000-bitcoin reserve. Bitcoin itself has lost roughly 40% over the past year while the Nasdaq 100 gained 40%, as investors rotated from crypto into AI-linked equities.

## Foreign Policy & Geopolitics

**Lebanon ceasefire agreed; Iran deal prospects improve as US House moves to end war**
Israel and Lebanon have agreed to implement a ceasefire following US-mediated talks, the Trump administration announced on Wednesday, raising hopes for broader progress on ending the three-month-old US–Israeli war on Iran. [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) and the [Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) both lead with the development, noting that the Republican-led US House of Representatives simultaneously approved a resolution to block President Trump from continuing the Iran war — reflecting growing unease within his own party about the conflict. Trump confirmed in a New York Post interview that he had told Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu during a Monday phone call: "Bibi, we gotta stop this. You gotta stop it." Israeli strikes continued in Lebanon despite the ceasefire announcement, and nine people were reported killed in Gaza. Gulf markets rose on peace deal hopes; a UNIFIL peacekeeper was killed in south Lebanon.

**Poland formally requests permanent US military base**
Poland has formally proposed the establishment of a new permanent US military base on its territory, Defence Minister Władysław Kosiniak-Kamysz announced after discussions with US Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth. Both [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/) and the [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/what-trumps-next-tariffs-might-look-like/) report the move, which comes after Trump's decision to send an additional 5,000 US troops to Poland and amid broader concerns about Washington's waning commitment to European security. Warsaw is preparing to offer financial incentives; talks remain at an early stage.

**Putin hosts SPIEF amid rival visions of Russia's future**
President Vladimir Putin opened Russia's flagship St Petersburg International Economic Forum against a backdrop of unresolved war in Ukraine and competing visions of the country's future, the [Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) reports. [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/03/qui-participe-au-forum-economique-de-saint-petersbourg/) published analysis of who is attending the forum.
