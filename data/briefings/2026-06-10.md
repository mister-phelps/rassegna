# Daily Briefing — Wednesday, 10 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**Von der Leyen consolidates control over EU security strategy, sidelining the EEAS**
As the EU prepares its European Security Strategy ahead of the NATO summit in Ankara in early July, the Berlaymont is firmly in the driver's seat — at the expense of the European External Action Service. According to the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/), the document is being drafted primarily by the Commission, with von der Leyen's chief diplomatic adviser Simon Mordue and Arnout Molenaar leading the effort. Two previous strategic documents — the EU Global Strategy and the Strategic Compass — were largely managed by the EEAS, which this time is expected to be limited mainly to Article 42.7 and issues explicitly requested by member states. Even Defence Commissioner Andrius Kubilius told Politico he was not party to the drafting: "It's high level. I don't know what they are writing." The Playbook characterises this as part of a broader pattern in which von der Leyen places trusted loyalists at the centre of sensitive dossiers — including the recent appointment of Siemens Chair Jim Hagemann Snabe as the Commission's AI envoy, reporting directly to the president.

**EU Parliament demands answers on Hungary funds deal; €16 billion at stake**
The European Parliament's budget and budgetary control committees will hold a joint hearing on 14 July to scrutinise the Commission's plan to unlock up to €16 billion in EU funds for Hungary, following a political understanding reached last month between von der Leyen and Hungarian PM Péter Magyar, as the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/) reports. Budget Commissioner Piotr Serafin, EVP Raffaele Fitto, and Justice Commissioner Michael McGrath will appear before MEPs. Lawmakers from across groups have voiced concern that conditionality may have been diluted. "Unfreezing EU funds is never a political favour," said Andreas Schwab, chair of the budgetary control committee. Renew's Lucia Yar warned that "if conditionality is seen as flexible depending on circumstances, we risk turning the rule of law from a principle into a political instrument." A separate legal challenge over the 2023 release of €10 billion to the Orbán government adds to the pressure, with an advocate general having recommended that decision be annulled. Magyar confirmed his government had submitted a "historically significant legislative package" to the Hungarian parliament, but the Commission said a formal revised recovery plan had not yet been received.

**Trust in US security guarantees collapses across Europe, ECFR poll finds**
A major new poll of more than 19,000 people across 15 European countries by the European Council on Foreign Relations, previewed by [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/), shows that only 11 per cent of Europeans now view the United States as an ally — down from 16 per cent six months ago and 22 per cent in November 2024. One in four now sees America as a rival or adversary. Majorities in every country surveyed doubt the US would defend them if attacked, while most trust other European countries to do so. Support for European-made weapons, higher defence spending, and joint borrowing for security investment is rising. Interestingly, most respondents expect transatlantic ties to improve once Trump leaves office.

**Kosovo elections: Kurti wins again, but governing majority remains uncertain**
Kosovars voted on 7 June in their third legislative election since February 2025, with Albin Kurti's Vetëvendosje (LVV) again coming first at around 43 per cent of counted votes — though down from its December peak of 51 per cent — according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/09/elections-au-kosovo-une-majorite-relative-pour-kurti-dans-un-contexte-de-crise-institutionnelle/). Turnout was low at 37 per cent. The PDK held at 21 per cent and the LDK gained ground at 17.6 per cent. LVV remains the dominant force but falls short of an outright majority needed to elect a president or govern alone; over 100,000 diaspora votes — expected to favour LVV — have not yet been counted. EU enlargement commissioner Marta Kos and HR/VP Kaja Kallas both congratulated Kurti while urging him to pursue dialogue with opposition parties and resume the Belgrade–Pristina normalisation process, a condition for further EU integration. Kosovo has so far received only €62 million of the €980 million available under the Western Balkans growth plan.

**Five EU capitals propose restricting voting rights of future member states**
A document seen by [Euractiv's Rapporteur newsletter](https://newsletter.euractiv.com) reveals that the Netherlands, France, Germany, Belgium and Luxembourg have proposed temporarily barring new EU members from voting on budgetary, security and foreign policy decisions — a measure they frame as "transitional." Dutch Foreign Minister Tom Berendsen said: "Enlargement is a strategic tool, but only if it makes the EU stronger, not weaker." The paper also proposes a new safeguard clause to protect against democratic backsliding by new members, with a veiled reference to Hungary's months-long veto of EU support for Ukraine. The proposal would affect front-runners Montenegro and Albania, as well as hopefuls Ukraine and Moldova.

**S&D in survival mode as socialist leaders seek route out of European torpor**
At the party's Red Gala in Brussels on Tuesday, Socialist MEPs and leaders grappled with the centre-left's slide across the continent, as [Euractiv's Rapporteur](https://newsletter.euractiv.com) reports. Group president Iratxe García vowed her 135 MEPs would aim to become Parliament's largest group — a position last held in 1999. The group unveiled six "flagship" initiatives focused on housing, jobs, clean energy and healthy food, deliberately sidestepping divisive issues like migration and Trump. European Council President António Costa, who will chair next week's European Council, delivered the punchline: "We are all here to say that progressives are going to fight back." The S&D will be represented by just three leaders — from Malta, Denmark and Spain — at next week's summit.

**Commission set to debate ETS reform; islands exemption package expected**
The College of Commissioners held an orientation debate Wednesday on the future of the EU Emissions Trading System, ahead of a broader reform proposal, as the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/) notes. A separate package on islands and coastal communities is expected to include targeted exemptions to ETS and maritime fuel rules for certain islands, citing the need for "a more gradual transition to decarbonisation." The UN's aviation body ICAO has written to von der Leyen urging her not to extend the ETS further.

EU ambassadors signed off Wednesday on opening Cluster 1 of Ukraine's accession negotiations, after Kyiv delivered fresh reforms including parliamentary votes on judicial governance and railway safety, with further votes expected on state aid, district heating and renewable energy permitting, per the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/).

## Economy & Markets

**Iran war to hit Europe and China harder than the US, PIIE finds**
A report published Monday by the Peterson Institute for International Economics, covered in detail by [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/09/la-guerre-en-iran-devrait-provoquer-plus-de-pertes-economiques-pour-leurope-et-la-chine-que-pour-les-etats-unis/), projects that in a scenario of temporarily elevated energy prices lasting one year, US GDP would be 1.2 per cent below its 2023 baseline in 2026, against -1.6 per cent for Western Europe and -1.8 per cent for China. Asian economies outside the Gulf are the most exposed after the MENA region, with India facing up to -3.1 per cent and Malaysia -2 per cent. The asymmetry reflects America's domestic energy production: the US imports less than half its oil consumption and just 0.1 per cent of its LNG, against 80.4 per cent and 60.5 per cent respectively for France. European countries import up to 92 per cent of their oil (Italy), 81 per cent (Germany) and 73 per cent (France).

**USMCA at risk as Canada finds itself outside the renegotiation table**
Analyst Mario Seminerio at [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/09/usmca-trappola-per-carney/) offers a detailed dissection of Canada's position ahead of the USMCA's mandatory review on 1 July 2026. Formal bilateral talks between Washington and Mexico City launched on 18 March without Ottawa, after Ontario premier Doug Ford's anti-tariff campaign antagonised Trump last autumn. While Canadian PM Mark Carney is now pitching a "Fortress North America" vision of continental integration, this runs directly counter to Trump's narrower "Fortress America" preference for domestic US production. Seminerio argues the two visions are fundamentally incompatible: Carney wants continental integration; Trump wants American production with Canada as a subordinate raw materials supplier. Canada's earlier rapprochement with China — including a deal cutting EV tariffs from 100% to 6.1% — has been seized upon in Washington as evidence of bad faith, and Seminerio suggests Trump may use the USMCA review to exact retribution for Canada's resistance to US annexation rhetoric.

## Foreign Policy

**US strikes Iran overnight after Apache helicopter downing; Iran retaliates**
In the early hours of Wednesday, the United States launched strikes against Iranian air defence systems and radars near the Strait of Hormuz, in response to the destruction of a US Army Apache helicopter, as [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/10/trump-a-annonce-a-38-reprises-un-accord-avec-liran-depuis-le-debut-de-la-guerre/) and the [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://reuters.com) report. Iranian forces retaliated with drones and at least four ballistic missiles. The exchange came despite a fragile ceasefire in place since April and ongoing negotiations over a memorandum that would defer nuclear talks and the status of the Strait of Hormuz for 30 to 60 days. Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said his country would "leave no attack or threat unanswered." President Trump confirmed the two Apache crew members were "fine" after being rescued. Le Grand Continent notes that before these strikes, Trump had announced an imminent deal with Iran on 38 separate occasions since 23 March — approximately once every two days — while Iranian chief negotiator Mohammad Baqer Ghalibaf stated on Monday that Trump's latest claims about a memorandum "contradicted what had been agreed," and that Tehran had "no confidence in the opposing party."

**Israel defied Trump to secure a seat at the peace table**
As the [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://reuters.com) reports, Israel's renewed strikes on Iran — launched in apparent defiance of Trump — were aimed at ensuring Jerusalem has a seat at the peace negotiating table, from which it has so far been kept at arm's length by the US president. Both Iran and Israel have warned of further retaliation if provoked. Reuters frames this as placing the Trump administration in a bind, with the Houthis' latest Red Sea threats adding further pressure on an already fragile process.

**Franco-German next-generation fighter project formally collapsed**

**Ukraine war reaches same length as World War One**
As of Wednesday 11 June, Russia's full-scale war against Ukraine will have lasted as long as the First World War — 1,567 days — [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/09/a-partir-de-demain-la-guerre-en-ukraine-sera-aussi-longue-que-la-premiere-guerre-mondiale/) notes. Counting from Russia's annexation of Crimea in 2014, Ukraine has been under Russian aggression for 4,492 days — twice as long as the Second World War. Russian drone and missile attacks overnight on Tuesday struck Ukraine's Kharkiv region, killing a pregnant woman and two others, per the [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://reuters.com).
