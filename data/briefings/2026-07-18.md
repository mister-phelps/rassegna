# Daily Briefing — Saturday, 18 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**Zelenskyy cabinet shake-up sparks protests and raises EU concerns**

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy's decision to dismiss his popular defence minister, Mykhailo Fedorov, triggered days of protests across Ukraine and alarm in Brussels. EU Defence Commissioner Andrius Kubilius described the move as "a big surprise", telling [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/): "We were working with him in a very good way. I don't know who will be next." The answer emerged quickly: Yevhenii Khmara, a former spy chief, was named as Fedorov's replacement. Fedorov's explosive final press conference — in which he declared that commander-in-chief Oleksandr Syrsky was "dividing the country" — heightened the sense of political crisis. Zelenskyy also replaced Ukraine's Brussels ambassador, Vsevolod Chentsov (in post since 2021), with European Integration Minister Taras Kachka, a move [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports was framed as a push for "more sectoral, meticulous work" on EU accession. The reshuffle coincides with a moment of EU-Ukraine momentum: Commission President Ursula von der Leyen had been in Kyiv earlier this week to announce a new "defence industrial partnership" centred on an EU-Ukraine drone deal worth up to €10 billion from unspent SAFE funds, alongside an additional €10 billion from the Ukraine Support loan for drones, missiles and fighter jets, as [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports. As the [Reuters Weekend Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) notes, Zelenskyy subsequently chose Ukraine's former top police officer to lead the national security and defence council in a bid to contain the political fallout.

**EU energy package: electrification target, ETS reform, and aviation carbon pricing**

After late-night talks on Thursday and last-minute delays on Friday, the European Commission unveiled a major energy policy package ahead of the summer recess, [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports. The centrepiece is an economy-wide target to raise Europe's electricity consumption share from approximately 24% today to 46% by 2040. The package also includes a reform of the Emissions Trading System that would extend industry's permitted timeline for polluting by nine years, to 2048. A measure to apply the EU's carbon price to some aviation routes was also included, though routes to American and Chinese airports and all incoming flights are exempted.

**NextGenerationEU: Hungary joins as debt repayment debate intensifies**

The EU's €637 billion NextGenerationEU recovery fund remains in the spotlight this week, with [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) publishing a substantial analysis of its legacy. Hungary's recovery plan — the last outstanding — was finally approved by EU finance ministers last week, half a decade after the fund entered into force. The analysis, by Thomas Moller-Nielsen, challenges the fund's "performance-based" credentials: Bruegel senior fellow Zsolt Darvas is quoted as noting that monitoring focuses largely on "input" and "output" indicators — money spent, roads built — with little attention to whether funds were well spent. Separately, EU ambassadors this week failed to agree on updated Russia sanctions: the bloc's oil price cap, currently set at $44 per barrel, has been frozen until 23 July to allow a deal to be reached; without agreement, it would have risen to $58 per barrel, potentially replenishing Russia's war chest. Bulgaria's deputy premier Atanas Pekanov also made the case to [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) for rolling over the NextGenerationEU debt — due for repayment from 2028 — citing strong market appetite for Eurobonds and growing concern about the dollar's reserve status.

## UK Politics

**Andy Burnham's nationalisation agenda takes shape**

Writing in [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/18/la-nationalisation-qui-vient/), historian Nicholas Mulder places the UK firmly within a global wave of nationalisation. Under Keir Starmer, Britain has already renationalised its railways and taken effective control of the last active steelworks in England, at Scunthorpe — a plant currently being forcibly acquired from its Chinese owner, the Jingye group. His likely successor, Andy Burnham, is expected to take control of the financially troubled Thames Water and extend the nationalisation drive to other water companies as well as electricity and gas utilities, according to a report in the *Guardian* cited by Mulder.

## Economy & Markets

**Russia's economy in sharp decline; oligarchs move billions abroad**

The Moscow stock exchange's MOEX index has fallen 27% since the start of the year, returning to the lows last seen in 2022 immediately after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/18/economie-russe-lindice-de-la-bourse-de-moscou-a-chute-de-27-depuis-le-debut-de-lannee/) reports. The index had recovered strongly through 2023–24, reaching 3,500 points in May 2024 — a period widely cited as evidence of the supposed "failure" of Western sanctions. That narrative has since been overtaken: cumulative sanctions pressure and Ukrainian drone strikes on Russian oil infrastructure have progressively paralysed the economy. According to *Bloomberg* (cited by Le Grand Continent), several of Russia's wealthiest individuals — including some with close ties to Vladimir Putin — have transferred billions of dollars abroad since the start of the year, shifting into cryptocurrencies, gold, foreign real estate and private equity funds, particularly in Gulf states. The article also notes that Communist Party leader Guennadi Ziuganov's suggestion last month that 30 trillion roubles of private savings could be "mobilised" by the state rattled investors, even though he subsequently walked back the remark.

**Apple overtakes Nvidia as world's most valuable company**

Apple displaced Nvidia at the top of the global market capitalisation ranking on Friday, [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) reports, as investors reassessed the outlook for artificial intelligence and rotated away from chip-related stocks. Chipmakers and other high-flying AI-adjacent equities fell in tandem.

**Oil rises on US-Iran hostilities**

Oil prices settled higher on Friday on renewed US-Iran hostilities and the threat of Red Sea closure, [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) reports. The US has conducted multiple nights of strikes on Iranian military sites including logistics infrastructure, escalating the conflict one week after a fragile ceasefire fell apart. Iran has in turn renewed missile and drone attacks on Gulf states.

## Foreign Policy

**US-Iran war intensifies one week after ceasefire collapse**

The [Reuters Iran Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) reports that the United States has stepped up strikes on Iranian military sites, including logistics infrastructure, one week after a fragile ceasefire agreement fell apart. Iran has responded by renewing attacks on Gulf states. Other developments include Israeli strikes killing Palestinians attending a funeral in Gaza for victims of an earlier strike; a sanctioned tanker leaking oil near Oman; Pakistan and Kuwait reportedly discussing an expanded defence pact; and a suspected pirate seizure of a tanker in the Gulf of Aden. The [Reuters Weekend Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) notes that Ukraine's drone strikes on Russian oil refineries and its military escalation in the Sea of Azov are pushing up diesel prices and hurting Russian grain exports.

**For the first time in 20 years, China is viewed more favourably than the US worldwide**

A Pew Research Center survey conducted across 36 countries between February and May finds that 27 of them hold a more favourable view of China than of the United States — including France, Germany, Italy and Spain, [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/18/effet-trump-pour-la-premiere-fois-depuis-20-ans-la-chine-est-percue-plus-positivement-dans-le-monde-que-les-etats-unis/) reports. Globally, 46% of respondents view China favourably (+14 points since 2023), against just 36% for the US (−22 points). The only European countries where the US retains an edge are Poland and Hungary — although even there China's approval is rising (up 13 and 9 points respectively). The survey finds that 75% of respondents consider the US likely to interfere in other countries' affairs, against 45% for China, and that fewer than half regard the US as a reliable partner (49%), compared to more than two-thirds for China (69%). Among Italians, twice as many now trust Xi Jinping (33%) as trust Donald Trump (17%). Le Grand Continent notes that this contradicts Trump's own Friday-night speech, in which he declared: "Two years ago our country was dying. Today we are the most prominent country in the world."

**The new wave of nationalisation: a global paradigm shift**

[Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/18/la-nationalisation-qui-vient/) publishes a major analytical piece by Cornell historian Nicholas Mulder arguing that a coherent global wave of nationalisation is under way — ideologically heterogeneous and spanning rich and poor countries alike. Examples cited include Belgium's planned takeover of ENGIE's seven nuclear reactors (which supply up to 45% of Belgian electricity), the UK's renationalisation of railways and steel, France's repeated parliamentary votes to nationalise ArcelorMittal's French operations, the Netherlands' consideration of a stake in shipbuilder Damen, the Trump administration's majority stake in rare earths miner MP Materials, and Indonesia's asset confiscations in palm oil and nickel. Mulder situates the current wave within a broader historical typology — comparable to the nationalisations of the 1930s in its political heterodoxy and its coincidence with the collapse of a liberal international economic order — while noting its novelty: for the first time, generalised state property expansion is occurring simultaneously with historically high levels of cross-border capital mobility. The piece was accompanied by the Phastidio.net [weekly digest](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/18/la-settimana-phastidiosa-18-luglio-2026/) (Mario Seminerio), which also highlighted the nationalisation theme alongside commentary on Trump's increasingly tangled posture on the Strait of Hormuz and the accelerating collapse of US soft power.
