# Daily Briefing — Sunday, 12 July 2026

## Foreign Policy

**US strikes Iran; Strait of Hormuz declared closed as new supreme leader pledges revenge**

The United States has continued its military campaign against Iran, with Tehran retaliating by striking Gulf states and declaring the Strait of Hormuz closed, according to the Reuters Iran Briefing newsletter (12 July). In a development of extraordinary geopolitical magnitude, Iran's new supreme leader pledged revenge "for his slain father and predecessor," indicating that the previous supreme leader was killed in the escalation. The crisis escalated following a series of attacks on commercial shipping: one Indian national was reported missing after an attack on a vessel off Oman, and the UK Maritime Trade Operations authority (UKMTO) reported that a crew had abandoned a container ship near Oman after a fire on board. [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/11/la-settimana-phastidiosa-11-luglio-2026/) notes that Trump — described as an increasingly "grotesque mask" — declared a prior ceasefire over, at least until market pressures compel him to reverse course.

**Russia consolidates military and commercial presence in Syria despite Western rapprochement**

Despite Syria's simultaneous movement towards the West and the Gulf, Russia is expected to retain its military bases at Tartous and Hmeimim, as [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/10/la-russie-consolide-sa-presence-en-syrie/) reports in detail. A commercial logistics hub is expected to begin operations at Tartous in mid-July, handling an initial cargo volume of 250,000 tonnes per month — blé, vegetable oils, steel and coal — developed through a partnership between Syrian firm Rus Line and Russian companies in the Russo-Syrian Business Council. The development occurs in the wake of French President Macron's visit to Damascus on 6–7 July — the first visit by a Western head of state since Ahmed al-Sharaa came to power — and of Syrian President al-Sharaa's attendance at the NATO summit in Ankara on 8 July, where President Trump indicated his intention to remove Syria from the list of state sponsors of terrorism (effective 22 August). Le Grand Continent notes that Syria remains dependent on Russia for 85 per cent of its wheat imports and for most of its oil, a structural dependency that limits how far Damas can pivot westward. The question of whether Russia's renewed presence will complicate Syria's Western rapprochement remains unresolved.

**Former Qatar ruler Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani dies at 74**

Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani, the former Emir of Qatar who seized power from his father in 1995 and is credited with modernising the country — transforming it into a regional diplomatic and media power — died aged 74, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) reports. He stepped down in 2013 in favour of his son, the current Emir Sheikh Tamim.

## EU Affairs

**Tusk invokes Volhynia anniversary to call for Polish-Ukrainian historical reconciliation**

11 July marked the 83rd anniversary of the "Bloody Sunday" of Volhynia — the peak of a 1943 massacre in which UPA (Ukrainian Insurgent Army) units killed approximately 100,000 Poles in Volhynia and eastern Galicia, a campaign Poland classifies as genocide but Ukraine refuses to recognise as such. In a near three-minute video message published on social media, Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk declared: "It was a genocide committed by Ukrainian nationalists against Poles... Our duty, towards the victims but also as a means of overcoming this painful past, is the truth." He also announced the construction of a "Wall of Memory" in Warsaw with the names of every identified victim, per [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/11/346025/). Tusk simultaneously appealed for reconciliation with Kyiv, warning that "memory must not be put at the service of hatred." The statement comes after one of the sharpest deteriorations in Polish-Ukrainian relations since the war began: Zelensky renamed an elite military unit after "UPA heroes" in late May, prompting Polish President Nawrocki — who had already made Ukrainian EU and NATO membership conditional on settling memorial disputes throughout his 2025 presidential campaign — to publicly withdraw the Order of the White Eagle from Zelensky. At the NATO summit in Ankara, Nawrocki told Zelensky the UPA question was "non-negotiable," with Zelensky describing the meeting as "constructive." A European Parliament amendment last week, backed by MEPs from Tusk's own party, expressed regret at what it called Zelensky's "unnecessary and unprovoked escalation." New exhumations at Ostrówki and Wola Ostrowiecka are scheduled to begin 13 July. Le Grand Continent notes that opposition leader Czarnek (PiS) called for a Sejm resolution blocking Ukrainian EU accession so long as glorification of UPA perpetrators continues.

**EU threatens Meta with fines over addictive features on Instagram and Facebook**

The European Union has ordered Instagram and Facebook to change their addictive algorithmic design features or risk financial penalties, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) reports. The move represents a further escalation of Digital Services Act enforcement against Meta, following earlier investigations into political advertising, disinformation, and content moderation practices.

## UK Politics

**Andy Burnham set to enter Downing Street within days as Rachel Reeves fights for chancellorship**

Britain will have a new prime minister in "fewer than ten days," [The Sunday Times](https://www.thetimes.com) reports this morning, with Andy Burnham set to succeed as Labour leader and take office at Downing Street. The paper's Jason Cowley profiles Burnham and assesses the risks he faces on entering office, while Josh Glancy reports that Chancellor Rachel Reeves has not abandoned her efforts to retain her position in the new cabinet. Burnham has proposed a land value tax, which tax specialist Dan Neidle describes in the paper as "the right idea." The transition adds political urgency to an existing fiscal challenge: as [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/10/regno-unito-la-sindrome-del-welfare/) analyses, working-age disability welfare spending has grown without restraint since the Covid pandemic, constituting what the site terms a "ticking bomb in the public accounts," with successive governments paralysed by the electoral cost of reform.

**Farage's relationship with convicted criminal Cottrell deepens into legal scandal**

[The Sunday Times](https://www.thetimes.com) reports new revelations from its Insight team — which last week broke the story of Nigel Farage's secret financial reliance on "Posh George" Cottrell, a convicted criminal — showing that Cottrell made undisclosed donations to Reform UK in breach of electoral law. The revelations from last week's original story triggered a by-election in Clacton. The paper asks whether Farage will ultimately distance himself from his "troublesome friend."

**Prince Harry and co-claimants lose Daily Mail lawsuit on every point**

Prince Harry, Baroness Lawrence, and a number of celebrities who sued Associated Newspapers, alleging the *Daily Mail* and *Mail on Sunday* had used illegal methods to invade their privacy, lost their case on every single point and now face costs running into millions of pounds, [The Sunday Times](https://www.thetimes.com) reports. Jim Armitage's investigation describes the case as "arguably flimsy" and examines why it was ever brought to court; Camilla Long attended proceedings and offered her verdict on the evidence and the judgment.
