# Daily Briefing — Monday, 6 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**NATO Summit in Ankara: Rutte's Trump diplomacy faces its sternest test yet**

European leaders arrive in Ankara on Tuesday for the NATO summit having, for once, a positive message on defence spending: military expenditure by European NATO members rose last year at its fastest pace since 1953, with Germany and Spain — two countries Trump has repeatedly criticised — among those recording sharp increases. "There is a good story to tell," one NATO ambassador told *Politico's* Brussels Playbook. "The numbers don't lie." Yet the summit's outcome may still hinge on the US president's mood. In the days before Ankara, Trump again publicly criticised European allies, questioned NATO solidarity following the Iran conflict, and singled out Germany for its "MUCH LOWER" defence spending over the past decade; one diplomat described him as the alliance's "wildcard", per the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com). NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte — whose "whatever it takes" approach has attracted both admiration and mockery — has meanwhile spent weeks highlighting rising European and Canadian defence spending against the new 5% benchmark. European lawmakers gave him broadly upbeat marks, with MEP David McAllister saying "Mark Rutte is doing his job", while Latvian Green Mārtiņš Stakis offered a more sardonic reading: "It looks like what we used in kindergarten: big pictures, giant letters."

**Trump's FIFA intervention: Europe fumes over World Cup red-card reversal**

The run-up to Monday night's round-of-16 match between the United States and Belgium was overshadowed by an extraordinary intervention by Donald Trump, who personally lobbied FIFA chief Gianni Infantino to lift a one-match ban on US forward Folarin Balogun — imposed for a foul against Bosnia-Herzegovina — thereby allowing him to play against Belgium. Trump later admitted he had been entirely ignorant of the rules when he intervened: "He gave him a red card. I didn't know what that meant," he said, adding the ban seemed unfair to him (it is a rule that applies universally), as [Euractiv reports](https://www.euractiv.com). FIFA reversed the ban, drawing immediate condemnation from UEFA, which said the decision "crossed a red line" and issued a statement: "We express our disbelief at such an unprecedented, incomprehensible and unjustifiable decision." [Politico's Forecast newsletter](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/) noted the clash carries a deeper ideological charge: Belgium — headquarters of both NATO and the EU, a country that prides itself on consensus politics and rules-based diplomacy — is, in many respects, Trump's ideological antithesis. "The kind of politics that Trump practices, with so much power concentrated in the hands of one man, is unthinkable" for Belgians, said political scientist Carl Devos of Ghent University.

**Germany plans to surge defence spending by a third in 2027**

Germany will ramp up defence spending by a third next year, according to a draft budget allocating €109.7 billion to the military in 2027 — putting Berlin on track to meet NATO's target by 2029, six years ahead of the alliance's agreed deadline, according to the [Euractiv newsletter](https://www.euractiv.com). Overall, the federal budget is expected to reach €555.4 billion, financed with around €200 billion in debt.

**Le Pen awaits Tuesday court ruling that could reshape France's 2027 presidential race**

Marine Le Pen and Jordan Bardella spent the weekend projecting unity ahead of a Tuesday court ruling that will determine whether Le Pen is eligible to contest France's 2027 presidential election. The Euractiv Rapporteur reports that judges could acquit her, confirm last year's conviction, or uphold it while reducing her period of ineligibility — the last option potentially restoring her eligibility in time for the race. Le Pen has already ruled out campaigning while wearing an electronic monitoring bracelet, and has framed the case throughout as politically motivated.

**AfD congress: Weidel consolidates power in Erfurt amid counter-protests**

Germany's far-right Alternative für Deutschland held its annual congress in Erfurt at the weekend, electing Alice Weidel as party co-leader with 81% of the vote — a stronger result than her 2024 Essen score — while co-president Tino Chrupalla was re-elected more narrowly with 70%, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/06/a-erfurt-alice-weidel-renforce-son-pouvoir-lors-du-congres-de-lafd/). The congress was less an occasion for substantive debate than a staging of the AfD as a credible party of government: "Wir wollen unser Land retten," Weidel told Phoenix television. The gathering took place despite counter-demonstrations of up to 10,000 people in Erfurt, in a congress notable for the consolidation of a tripartite informal leadership — Weidel, Björn Höcke (Thuringia), and Sebastian Münzenmaier (North Rhine-Westphalia) — and the election of figures with close ties to far-right extremist networks. The AfD is currently leading in German national polls and is targeting two eastern Länder in September's regional elections.

## Economy & Markets

**Renewables are unavoidable — but fixed costs are rising faster than bills are falling**

A new analysis by Mario Seminerio on [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/06/rinnovabili-una-strada-lastricata-di-costi-fissi/) uses the United Kingdom as a case study for the structural tensions in Europe's energy transition. The UK now generates over a third of its electricity from wind and solar, and the government has long promised that the decarbonisation push will reduce household bills. But UK electricity bills have risen 54% in their variable component and 120% in their fixed component since 2019 — and the fixed costs are set to keep climbing, driven by grid upgrades, capacity payments, subsidised contracts-for-difference locked in before the 2021–23 energy crisis, and the Sizewell C nuclear programme. Ofgem estimates that grid investment alone will add around £60 to annual bills. Even the CEO of E.On UK has suggested that bills could keep rising even if wholesale electricity prices fell to zero. The structural risk, Seminerio argues, applies equally to Italy: a race to renewables that destroys industrial demand through high fixed costs could end up hollowing out the very consumer base that makes cost sharing viable.

## Foreign Policy

**Iran stages its largest-ever state funeral as successor stays invisible and negotiations resume**

Tehran staged what the regime hopes will be the most attended funeral in the 21st century for slain Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei — killed by the United States on 28 February — with authorities targeting 15–20 million participants across a six-day ceremony spanning five cities in Iran and Iraq, according to a detailed [Le Grand Continent analysis](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/06/ce-que-revele-la-mise-en-scene-des-funerailles-du-khamenei/). The choreography is designed to invoke the Shia theology of martyrdom rooted in the Battle of Kerbala, with red flags (signifying vengeance), chest-beating, and giant portraits of the late Guide: every image, Le Grand Continent notes, functions as a coded signal in a regime that is simultaneously staging a theatrical demand for revenge and pursuing indirect ceasefire negotiations with Washington. Notably absent from the ceremony was Mojtaba Khamenei, 56, who succeeded his father as Supreme Leader but has not appeared in public since the 28 February attacks. As [Reuters reports](https://www.reuters.com), crowds marched through Tehran calling for vengeance on Trump on Monday. Indirect negotiations, mediated by Qatar and Pakistan, resumed on 1 July in Doha around the Strait of Hormuz and ceasefire implementation; a further meeting is expected immediately after the funeral concludes Thursday in Mashhad. Russia was represented at the ceremony by former president Dmitri Medvedev; China by a senior parliamentary official. The [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com) notes that Iran's nuclear negotiator Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf — who has also been photographed sobbing before the coffin — has been conspicuously prominent in the funeral's staging, embodying the regime's internal tension between those who want to avenge the martyrs and those who want to reconstruct the country.

**Russia strikes Kyiv with missiles and drones, killing at least 12**

Russia hammered Kyiv with missiles and drones in the early hours of Monday, killing at least 12 people, according to the [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com). The attack came amid reported shortages of US-made interceptors, leaving the Ukrainian capital struggling to defend itself. The Euractiv Rapporteur notes that Ukraine has meanwhile been winning the "petrol war", with Russia scrambling to protect its oil export routes.
