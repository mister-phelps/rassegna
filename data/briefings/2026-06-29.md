# Daily Briefing — Monday, 29 June 2026

## EU Affairs

### Main stories

**A record-breaking heatwave kills hundreds across Europe, but draws little political response.** France has recorded around 1,000 excess deaths since last Wednesday, according to preliminary Santé publique France data reported by [Politico's Dimanchissime](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/dimanchissime/) and corroborated by Euractiv's Paris dispatch, with the toll expected to rise as only 60% of deaths have been counted so far and the elderly hit hardest. In Poland, temperatures hit a new national record of 40.5°C on 28 June, beating the previous 1921 record, yet, as [Le Grand Continent reports](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/29/la-vague-de-chaleur-la-plus-intense-jamais-mesuree-en-pologne-ne-provoque-aucun-debat-politique/), the only official reaction was a low-key social media post from PM Donald Tusk, with opposition figures largely ignoring the heatwave in favour of other issues. Euractiv's own reporting flagged a "heat dome" pushing record temperatures across the continent more broadly, including the Balkans, per Reuters' Thomson Daily Briefing. The framing differs sharply: Le Grand Continent treats Poland's political silence as the story itself, while French outlets (cited via Politico) focused on the death toll and pleas for citizens to check on vulnerable neighbours.

**The EU's diplomatic service is locked in a quiet power struggle with the Commission.** According to [Politico's Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/article/european-external-action-service-eeas-fight-future-kaja-kallas-ursula-von-der-leyen/), the EEAS, led by Kaja Kallas, is fighting for survival amid squeezed budgets and what ten current and former officials describe as the Commission steadily poaching staff and duplicating functions to boost Ursula von der Leyen's global profile. France's former EU ambassador Pierre Sellal told Politico that "a foreign policy defined by statements on one hand and sanctions on the other is not a foreign policy," tracing the EEAS's structural weakness back to a "messy compromise at 3 in the morning" in 2011. Kallas is pushing back with a new leadership structure, including new Secretary-General Kajsa Ollongren, though many officials remain sceptical that staffing changes alone can fix the underlying mismatch in policy tools. Politico's Paris and Berlin playbooks both flagged the story as one of the day's defining EU narratives.

**Macron is hunting for new EU-wide taxes to plug a €2 trillion budget.** French President Emmanuel Macron has instructed his administration to find new EU taxes — on Big Tech, crypto firms, foreign polluters and potentially airlines — to finance the bloc's next roughly €2 trillion budget, according to five officials cited by [Politico's Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/article/france-hunts-for-new-eu-taxes-to-fund-e2t-budget/). The goal, also reported in Berlin Playbook, is to avoid higher French contributions to Brussels ahead of the 2027 presidential election, with Paris working alongside Spain to back European Parliament proposals along these lines.

**Trump-era transatlantic tensions were on display at a lavish July 4th party in Brussels with thin EU attendance.** US Ambassador to Belgium Bill White privatised Cinquantenaire Park for an "Independence Day" bash attended by 8,850 people and funded by $5.5 million in corporate sponsorship, per [Politico's Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/eeas-fights-for-survival/) and Euractiv's Rapporteur. Only three of the EU's 27 commissioners attended (Andrius Kubilius, Maroš Šefčovič and Oliver Várhelyi), with no António Costa, Kaja Kallas or Ursula von der Leyen — who sent her chief of staff Björn Seibert instead. European Parliament President Roberta Metsola was the most senior EU figure present and the only speaker to publicly acknowledge "disagreements" with Washington, using a marriage metaphor: "Like in any marriage, we have disagreements... and sometimes there's also real criticism or reproach." Euractiv noted the sparse turnout came just days after Trump's latest tariff threat against the bloc.

**Russia has shifted its disinformation tactics from social media to search engines and AI platforms.** Per [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/29/projet-2026-les-nouvelles-methodes-de-la-desinformation-russe/), a Bloomberg investigation (Stephanie Baker and Priyanjana Bengani, 23 June) leaked 73 files from Russia's Social Design Agency, headed by Sergei Kiriyenko, showing a project targeting Germany generated 200,000 web pages and trained six AI platforms on pro-Russian content. The campaign also used fake think tanks to launder doctored versions of legitimate research, including reports from France's IFRI, and identified Armenia as a major 2025–2026 target, including a planned fake Wikipedia clone — though Russia's efforts in Armenia and Hungary reportedly failed to achieve their aims.

**France's 2027 presidential field continues to take shape amid unresolved primary disputes.** The Socialist Party and Greens remain unable to agree on terms for a 9 July primary vote, with the sticking point being how open or restricted the electorate should be, according to [Politico's Playbook Paris](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook-paris/les-colles-primaires/). Separately, the Rassemblement National faces a defining moment on 7 July, when a Paris appeals court ruling will determine whether Marine Le Pen or Jordan Bardella leads the party's presidential bid, per [Politico's Dimanchissime](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/dimanchissime/attal-bat-le-fer-tant-quil-fait-chaud/). Renaissance's Gabriel Attal has meanwhile pledged a "permanent campaign" against what he calls a "nationalo-Insoumis pact" between the RN and LFI, while centre-left figures François Hollande, Bernard Cazeneuve and Raphaël Glucksmann drew over 500 people to a joint appearance in Le Mans.

## UK Politics

### Main stories

**Andy Burnham is steering Britain's leadership transition while Keir Starmer remains technically in post.** Reuters' Daily Briefing describes Burnham as "the Labour lawmaker expected to replace Keir Starmer as British prime minister," and his office said he will outline his vision for Britain, promising to shift power from London to the regions. *Additional context via WebSearch*: Starmer announced his resignation on 22 June, the same day Burnham was sworn in as MP for Makerfield; nominations for the Labour leadership open 9 July and close 16 July, with Burnham — the only declared candidate — set to be announced PM-designate on 17 July if uncontested. This is consistent with, not contradicted by, Politico's Brussels Playbook agenda item noting NATO Secretary-General Mark Rutte's visit to the UK today to meet "Prime Minister Keir Starmer" — Starmer remains the technical caretaker PM until the leadership process concludes.

**Burnham is preparing policy plans on youth employment and rail.** Per the Times' Daily Briefing (forwarded), Burnham will give mayors and councils responsibility for helping a "lost generation" of nearly one million young people into work, and has endorsed reinstating the cancelled HS2 leg between Birmingham and Manchester — albeit one that critics say will run slower trains than originally planned. Westminster is meanwhile watching closely to see who Burnham picks as chancellor, "the person who'll take the blame" if his government fails to turn the economy around, according to Politico's Brussels Playbook.

**Reform UK's biggest donor has registered to vote in Britain amid a deepening party funding row.** Christopher Harborne, a British-Thai cryptocurrency billionaire who gave Nigel Farage a £5 million gift before he became an MP and has donated £15 million to Reform since 2025, has switched his voter registration from overseas to a Hampshire address, the Times reports. Farage faces a parliamentary standards investigation over whether the original gift breached MPs' disclosure rules, while the government has separately moved to cap overseas donations at £100,000 a year — a restriction Harborne disputes the government's right to impose.

### Also noted

- NATO Secretary-General Mark Rutte visited the UK today, meeting Starmer, Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper and Defence Secretary Dan Jarvis (Politico's Brussels Playbook).

## Economy & Markets

### Main stories

**Volkswagen is reportedly considering cutting 100,000 jobs and closing four German plants.** Citing a Manager Magazin report, [Politico's Berlin Playbook](https://www.manager-magazin.de/unternehmen/mobilitaet/volkswagen-ceo-oliver-blume-will-100-000-jobs-streichen-a-77381bad-0897-4a63-bf6c-26ed1fe2129f) says VW is weighing cuts to roughly 100,000 of its 600,000 global jobs amid fierce competition from Chinese carmakers, a move IG Metall and the state of Lower Saxony — which together hold a majority on VW's supervisory board — have vowed to resist. Lower Saxony's economy minister Olaf Lies warned against seeking "the easy way out" through layoffs and plant closures alone.

**Spain's rapid defence spending increase is outpacing its industrial capacity.** Spain nearly doubled military spending to about €33.9 billion in 2025 to meet NATO's 2% target, but experts warn the domestic defence sector lacks the workforce, capacity and long-term planning to absorb the investment efficiently, Euractiv's Rapporteur reports, calling for stronger SME support and a clearer industrial strategy.

**A leading central banking authority has warned of a Liz Truss-style bond "fire sale" risk.** Global governments face a sharp rise in borrowing costs from bond fire sales by hedge funds and skittish investors, echoing the dynamics that followed the UK's 2022 mini-budget, the Times' Daily Briefing reports.

**France's public finances are "sitting on a powder keg," its budget minister warns.** David Amiel, France's minister for public accounts, told France Inter that a fiscal slippage this autumn would "throw all the [presidential] candidates' programmes in the bin," per [Politico's Dimanchissime](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/dimanchissime/attal-bat-le-fer-tant-quil-fait-chaud/), while warning that keeping the 2026 deficit under 5% of GDP is "far from guaranteed" per a Cour des comptes report. A public finance alert committee will review the situation on 7 July; new Banque de France governor Emmanuel Moulin has separately cautioned against "too pessimistic a view" of the French economy.

## Foreign Policy

### Main stories

**The US and Iran have agreed to halt attacks and resume talks in Doha on Tuesday.** A senior US official said the move raises hopes of saving an interim peace deal that came under pressure from days of tit-for-tat strikes, according to both Reuters' Iran Briefing and Thomson Reuters' Daily Briefing. Politico's Berlin Playbook adds that Iran's foreign minister Abbas Araghchi said in Baghdad that any new regional security arrangement must "involve all countries of the region" with no outside "presence or interference" — a more assertive framing of Tehran's aims in the Strait of Hormuz than the US-side framing of simply restoring free shipping, illustrating a divergence in emphasis between the German and Anglo-American newsletter coverage of the same de-escalation.

**Poland and the US have entered a new phase of talks on a permanent American military base.** Poland's Defence Minister Władysław Kosiniak-Kamysz said the Pentagon's global force review will shape the final decision, with Poznań and Wrocław among the leading candidate sites, beyond the roughly 10,000 US troops already stationed in Poland, Euractiv's Rapporteur reports.

**Serbian President Aleksandar Vučić says he will resign in the coming weeks to campaign for his party.** Vučić announced at a rally in Belgrade that he would step down and campaign for the Serbian Progressive Party under the banner "United Serbia," in what he called his last address to such a large crowd as president — a move that comes amid continuing student-led anti-government protests, per Euractiv's Rapporteur.
