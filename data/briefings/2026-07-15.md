# Daily Briefing — Wednesday, 15 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**EU Russia Sanctions 21st Package Reaches Make-or-Break Morning**

EU ambassadors reconvened Wednesday in a last-ditch attempt to agree on the bloc's 21st sanctions package against Russia after late-night talks on Tuesday collapsed without a deal. According to the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/11th-hour-bid-to-curb-russian-rockets/), the central dispute concerns the oil price cap — currently frozen at $44.10 per barrel — which is scheduled to automatically rise to $58 today in the absence of an agreement, a development that would substantially replenish Moscow's war revenues. The new package would also target banks and financial intermediaries that finance Russian fossil-fuel purchases. [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com) confirms the Irish Council presidency tabled compromise proposals Tuesday night before ambassadors suspended talks. Two bloc members are the main holdouts: Greece, which is concerned about the impact on its dominant shipping sector, and Austria, which is pushing for compensation for Raiffeisen Bank — the Austrian lender that chose to remain in Russia and would be adversely affected by proposed restrictions on money flows. "It's very hard," one senior EU diplomat told Politico. "But we are still hoping for a result."

**Von der Leyen Signs €10bn Ukraine Drone Deal, European Leaders Gather in Kyiv**

Commission President Ursula von der Leyen led a high-level European delegation to Ukraine on Wednesday to sign a landmark drone partnership with President Volodymyr Zelenskyy. As [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com) reports, with a correspondent on the ground, the deal commits approximately €10 billion in leftover funds from the EU's SAFE defence loan scheme to building drone factories in eastern EU member states — a dual arrangement designed to ramp up EU production capacity while giving European manufacturers access to Ukraine's battle-tested expertise. [Politico Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/11th-hour-bid-to-curb-russian-rockets/) reports that leaders of Greece, Romania, Croatia, Slovenia and others are also expected to sign a "Kyiv Declaration" committing to mobilise funding and logistics for Ukrainian air defences, in a major effort to reduce Kyiv's dependence on American support. Politico has seen a draft of the joint statement. France separately agreed this week to sell Rafale fighter jets to Ukraine.

**Biggest EU Enlargement Push Since 2004 as Four Candidates Advance**

Tuesday saw what [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com) describes as "the most significant enlargement push in a single day since 2004," as Ukraine and Moldova opened accession negotiations on the foreign, security and defence policy chapter. Ireland's Europe minister, presiding over the talks, called it "a moment of reality to show people that the enlargement process is real." Montenegro and Albania also closed negotiating chapters on the same day. EU Enlargement Commissioner Marta Kos hailed Ukraine's military expertise, calling its inclusion in the continent's future security architecture "unimaginable," but cautioned that Kyiv has yet to complete key rule-of-law reforms. The day's progress came just as EU ambassadors failed, hours later, to agree on the latest Russia sanctions package — a reminder of the contradictions running through Europe's approach to the war.

**EXCLUSIVE: Brussels Draws Up Turnberry Tariff Wishlist**

The European Commission has drafted a list of hundreds of EU exports it wants Washington to exempt from the blanket 15% tariff applied under the EU-US Turnberry deal, according to a proposal seen by [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com). Brussels argues it has already fulfilled its side of the bargain — scrapping tariffs on hundreds of US industrial and agri-food products from 1 July — and is now urging Washington to apply standard tariff rates rather than the 15% Turnberry levy to EU goods it deems vital to US reindustrialisation, farming, or strategic supply chains. The exemption list spans food products (wine, spirits including gin, whisky and vodka, Roquefort and Pecorino cheeses, tuna, smoked salmon, olive oil, pasta), as well as industrial robots, electrical equipment, surgical sutures and diagnostic kits. The move sets up a fresh round of transatlantic trade diplomacy.

**Estonian PM: "Russia Is in Difficulty Because Our Strategy Is Working"**

In a wide-ranging interview with [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/15/la-russie-de-poutine-est-en-difficulte-parce-que-notre-strategie-fonctionne-entretien-avec-le-premier-ministre-estonien/), Estonian Prime Minister Kristen Michal pushed back forcefully against European voices dismissing the Russian threat, arguing that Moscow is struggling precisely because sanctions, asset freezes, and the shadow-fleet crackdown are working. Michal warned that Russian cyber attacks against Estonia have tripled over the past three years even as the Ukraine war continues, and predicted that Moscow will weaponise AI to breach European digital infrastructure within six to eighteen months. He argued against any EU mediator role in ceasefire negotiations, saying it would erode European leverage without serving Ukrainian interests: "A mediator places itself between the two parties, and ends up making concessions." Estonia now spends 7% of GDP on defence. On the Bastille Day parade — from which the US was absent — Michal said Europe is transitioning from "a peace project without weapons" to "a peace project with weapons," and that within three to five years the continent will have the defence industry capacity it needs.

## Italian Politics

**Coalition Fracture Over Electoral Reform**

The Meloni government suffered a notable setback on Tuesday when Italy's lower house rejected, by a single vote, a key amendment to its flagship electoral reform. As [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com) reports, the measure — which would have allowed voters to choose individual candidates — was defeated by coalition partners the League and Forza Italia, in a public display of friction within the ruling alliance. The wider reform, which grants a seat bonus to coalitions winning more than 42% of the vote, continues through parliament, but the episode exposes the limits of Meloni's grip on her coalition ahead of next year's election.

**The State Shareholdings Debate Returns**

Italian commentator Mario Seminerio at [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/15/il-compagno-trump-e-le-partecipazioni-statali/) offers a sceptical analysis of the European moment of state interventionism, running from Trump's equity stakes in AI companies to the Italian centre-left's plans to recreate public industrial policy bodies. The PD is reportedly drafting a white paper under former minister Andrea Orlando that envisages a new public participation agency, an expanded role for Invitalia, and a technology transfer agency. Seminerio argues that without fiscal space or a credible mechanism for identifying winning industries, Italy risks repeating the history of state shareholdings as a drain on public resources — and notes that France and Germany, often cited as models, are themselves in deep economic difficulty.

## Economy & Markets

**Iran-US Escalation Pushes Oil to Four-Week Highs**

The renewed military confrontation between Iran and the United States — with Iran firing ballistic missiles at a US air base in Jordan and the US striking Iranian targets for five hours in a battle over control of the Strait of Hormuz — pushed oil prices to four-week highs, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) and the [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com) report. The UAE says Iranian missiles struck oil tankers in the Strait, killing one sailor. Traffic through the Strait has slowed to a two-month low amid the heightened safety risk. EU ambassadors face particular urgency on the Russia oil price cap today: the Middle East flare-up risks pushing global energy prices higher, which would increase Moscow's oil revenues even before the cap automatically rises to $58.

**EU-China Deficit at Record; Trade Instruments to Remain**

The EU confirmed it will maintain its trade defence instruments against China as the bilateral trade deficit rose to a record $32.87 billion in June, according to [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com). Beijing recently released data showing the month-on-month rise from $30.66 billion in May.

## Foreign Policy

**Iran-US War Resumes: Battle for the Strait of Hormuz**

Iran fired ballistic missiles at a US military base in Jordan on Tuesday and the United States responded with five hours of air strikes on Iranian targets in a renewed clash over control of the Strait of Hormuz, according to [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) and the [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com). President Trump reinstated a blockade of Iranian shipping in the Gulf and initially proposed charging a 20% fee on cargo transiting the Strait, though [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/11th-hour-bid-to-curb-russian-rockets/) reported he appeared to walk back the fee threat. A Reuters/Ipsos poll conducted during the escalation found four in five Americans expect the conflict to drag on for an extended period. The UAE says Iranian missiles struck oil tankers in the Strait, killing one sailor, and the Houthis fired missiles at Saudi Arabia, breaking years of relative calm on that front.

**Death of Senator Graham Clouds Ukraine Support in Washington**

The sudden death of US Senator Lindsey Graham has raised a significant question for Ukraine and its European allies: without one of Trump's closest confidants — and the most vocal Congressional advocate for Kyiv — will the administration's recent tilt towards Ukraine endure? The [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com) reports that Graham's death leaves a gap at the intersection of Republican loyalism and Ukraine support that will be difficult to fill.
