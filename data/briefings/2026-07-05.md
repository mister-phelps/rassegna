# Daily Briefing — Sunday, 5 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**Germany's Merz government unveils €10bn income tax reform**
The Merz-Klingbeil coalition has presented its long-awaited income tax package, as [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/02/riforma-fiscale-tedesca-italien-wir-kommen/) reports in detail. The plan — worth €10bn, significantly below the €17–27bn floated by Finance Minister Lars Klingbeil earlier — reduces the burden on lower- and middle-income families, with a two-earner household on €60,000 saving around €600 annually from 2028. The top *Reichensteuer* threshold is lowered from €280,000 to €250,000 for singles, and a new 47% band introduced above that ceiling; the mini-job flat-rate contribution rises from 2% to 5%, offset by unlimited-purpose fixed-term contracts of up to four years until end-2030. Phastidio notes the reform partly returns fiscal drag generated by recent inflation rather than offering genuine structural relief, and draws an unflattering parallel with Italy's redistribution logic — taxing the upper-middle class to fund modest cuts lower down — while leaving public spending untouched. First economists' reactions describe the package as "small steps" that may add up to something positive, though opposition parties from both the far left and far right have already dismissed it as either too timid (AfD) or needlessly punitive (Greens).

**The end of the Atlantic narrative is a historical opportunity for Europe**
In a major essay published on [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/04/la-fin-du-recit-atlantique-et-nouveau-recit-europeen/), historian Ludovic Tournès argues that the United States is in the grip of what he calls a "grand estrangement" — a progressive severance from the transatlantic narrative born in 1945. Trump's second term, Tournès contends, is not an aberration but a return to the pre-1941 American normal: an "originalist syndrome" that looks to Jefferson, Jackson, and the Manifest Destiny rather than Roosevelt and the Marshall Plan. Those who expect a post-Trump return to normality are mistaken, he writes: "Il n'y aura pas de retour à la normale." Far from catastrophe, however, the retreat opens a historic window for Europe to articulate its own narrative — grounded in the rule of law, multilateral commitment, and cultural plurality — with the war in Ukraine already erasing the old east–west divide within the continent. Tournès calls on artists, writers, and cultural institutions across Europe to construct "des récits pluriels" that make a shared European destiny visible and felt.

**Marine Le Pen's ban appeal: ruling expected Tuesday**
An appeals court will decide on Tuesday whether to uphold the five-year ban from public office handed to National Rally leader Marine Le Pen following her 2025 conviction over misused EU parliamentary funds, as the [Politico Sunday Crunch](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/sunday-crunch/) notes. The Observer this week published a profile of Le Pen ahead of the verdict.

## UK Politics

**Farage's finances: convicted criminal funded his political comeback, investigation demanded**
The dominant story across the Sunday papers — led by the Sunday Times Insight team and picked up by the [Politico Sunday Crunch](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/sunday-crunch/) — is a detailed investigation into George Cottrell, known as "Posh George," a longstanding Farage associate who has served time in a US jail for fraud. According to [the Sunday Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk), Cottrell funded substantial elements of Farage's political comeback in autumn-winter 2023–24: social media rebranding, personal security, three recruited staff, and the use of a five-storey central London house he rents near Buckingham Palace. None of this was declared to Parliament. The revelation compounds an existing investigation by the parliamentary standards watchdog into Farage's failure to declare a £5m donation from crypto-billionaire Christopher Harborne. Lib Dem President Josh Babarinde has written to Standards Commissioner Daniel Greenberg calling for a new inquiry, and Labour's Lucy Powell said Farage "misunderstood the rules." Reform UK dismissed the story as "baseless and contrived," alleging the Sunday Times is motivated by partisan bias given its backing for Labour at the last election. [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/sunday-crunch/) frames this as a "knotty tale" for Farage, noting that the Sunday Times has also launched a podcast series on Cottrell, and that he is separately lobbying for a presidential pardon from Donald Trump.

**Burnham's economic inheritance: property taxes, social reform, and the pace of change**
With Andy Burnham all but certain to become Prime Minister imminently, the Sunday papers are heavily preoccupied with the shape of his economic agenda, as [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/sunday-crunch/) and the [Sunday Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk) report extensively. Burnham has said there is "some room" for movement on taxes, without raising income tax, VAT, or national insurance. Options reportedly under discussion include lowering the mansion tax threshold from £2m to £1.5m — potentially affecting over 150,000 families and raising ~£250m per year — and replacing council tax and stamp duty with a proportional property levy. Burnham is also said to plan retaining the social media ban for under-16s, rolling out the Greater Manchester baccalaureate nationally, rejecting a new Scottish independence referendum, and revisiting Starmer's jury reform and early prisoner release scheme. [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/sunday-crunch/) notes that former minister Miatta Fahnbulleh's policy work has been compressed by the speed of Starmer's departure. The [Sunday Telegraph](https://www.telegraph.co.uk) splashes with a Tony Blair Institute warning against equalising capital gains tax and income tax.

**Powell pushes for broadcaster-style social media regulation ahead of elections**
Labour Deputy Leader Lucy Powell has backed tighter controls on social media companies during election campaigns, modelled on existing broadcaster rules, as [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/sunday-crunch/) reports. Speaking to Times Radio, Powell criticised platforms for "trying to distort debate and feed misinformation" and backed stronger Ofcom powers to regulate promoted content. The Representation of the People Bill, returning to parliament later this month, would impose legal duties on Meta and X, with eligibility for receiving donations linked to company profits rather than revenue.

## Economy & Markets

**USMCA lapses as Trump opts for turbulent annual renegotiations**
[Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/04/la-settimana-phastidiosa-4-luglio-2026/) flags that Donald Trump has declined to renew the United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement, replacing it with a framework of annual renegotiations. Analysts warn the arrangement will inject chronic uncertainty into North American supply chains and corporate investment decisions, as trade terms between the three countries become effectively unpredictable on a rolling basis.

**Chinese logistics firms build US infrastructure to sidestep tariffs**
Despite the US-China trade war, Chinese e-commerce companies are building out domestic logistics infrastructure inside the United States — warehousing, last-mile delivery, local fulfilment — in a significant strategic adaptation documented by [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/01/la-logistica-cinese-tira-il-pacco-a-trump/). By reducing their tariff exposure through local operations, firms are maintaining competitive advantage in the American market even as Washington tightens trade restrictions. Phastidio frames this as a structural shift that partially nullifies the intended effect of tariffs.

## Foreign Policy

**Khamenei's state funeral: a political test for the Islamic Republic**
State funeral ceremonies for Ali Khamenei — Supreme Leader from 1989 until his death in US strikes on 28 February — began in Tehran on Saturday 4 July, with between 12 and 15 million mourners expected across a week of national commemoration, as [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/04/les-funerailles-dali-khamenei-un-moment-de-verite-pour-lavenir-du-regime-iranien/) and [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) both report. The procession will move from Tehran's Mosalla mosque through the city, on to Qom, then to Najaf in Iraq (home to most Shia imams' tombs, illustrating Iran's ambitions for Shia regional influence) and finally to Mashhad, Khamenei's birthplace. Le Grand Continent characterises the event as a "moment of truth" for the regime: a sufficiently large turnout could either harden domestic opposition to ongoing US negotiations — ultraconservative figure Hassan Rahimpour-Azghadi declared at a Tehran rally, "I spit on this era where they kill our leader and then talk of peace" — or consolidate national unity behind negotiators tasked with rebuilding the country. As [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) notes, three of Khamenei's sons appeared at the funeral, but his designated successor Mojtaba Khamenei has still not been seen in public since his nomination in March — a significant and unexplained absence that Le Grand Continent highlights as particularly telling. Crowds on Saturday chanted "Death to America" and "May God's curse fall on Israel."

**JD Vance: "something very broken" about British politics**
US Vice-President JD Vance told the Sunday Times in an interview — flagged by both [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/sunday-crunch/) and the [Sunday Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk) as a must-read — that Britain has "been failed by its leadership for a long time" and that voters are "crying out for significant structural change," pointing to Westminster's recent churn of prime ministers. Vance acknowledged knowing little about Andy Burnham but assured that Washington will work with whoever leads Britain "as successfully as we can." He insisted his frequent criticisms of Europe come from "a perspective of love and admiration," and separately discussed Ukraine, where he confirmed the US posture of disengagement from European security guarantees.
