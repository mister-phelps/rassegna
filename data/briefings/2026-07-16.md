# Daily Briefing — Thursday, 16 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**Centre-left alliance fractures ahead of Metsola midterm vote**

The informal pact between the Socialists and Democrats, Renew Europe and the Greens/EFA — forged to check the EPP's rightward drift — is fracturing ahead of the European Parliament's midterm reshuffle, as the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/center-left-strains-over-metsola-iii-support/) reports. Parliament President Roberta Metsola is widely expected to seek a third term despite the S&D's claim to the role. Some Green and Renew MEPs are already gaming out early deals with the EPP rather than holding the line with the Socialists, whom one Green MEP told Politico have "zero chances." S&D leader Iratxe García insists that negotiations must cover "political commitments" as well as posts. The likeliest outcome, per several senior MEPs cited anonymously, is a grand bargain between the EPP, S&D and Renew: extra jobs for the Socialists in exchange for backing Metsola, plus policy pledges. A residual fear runs through all three groups: even with a deal, the Patriots for Europe could capture a vice-presidency. EPP leader Manfred Weber calls this a red line — but, as Playbook notes, the vote is secret, and recent history suggests red lines fade. The week brought an illustration: on Tuesday the EPP voted with the Patriots to block scrutiny of the far-right group's spending, and a day later Patriots votes helped the EPP shield a member from Slovenian prosecutors.

**Russia sanctions pushed to 22 July; oil price cap frozen for a week**

EU member states failed to agree on a new Russia sanctions package on Wednesday, delaying another attempt to tighten pressure on the Kremlin. As a stopgap, capitals agreed to extend the existing oil price cap until 23 July while negotiators seek a compromise, according to four EU diplomats cited by the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/). The Commission had proposed freezing the cap for six months, but the plan ran into resistance from major shipping states, including Greece. The [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-russia-oil-price-cap-sanctions-delayed/) confirmed the next attempt is set for 22 July. Zelenskyy said in Kyiv that "of course some people get tired," but that what mattered was for Ukraine to tire one day later than Russia.

**ETS review lands Friday; Italy and Poland lead "pragmatic" coalition**

The European Commission is due to unveil its long-awaited review of the EU Emissions Trading System on Friday, alongside a new energy package, as reported by both the [Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-ets-carbon-market-reform-proposal/) and the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/). Italy and Poland are leading a ten-country coalition calling for a "pragmatic" approach, while the traditional agenda-setters France and Germany are pursuing divergent lines, and a non-aligned group is keeping its powder dry. The EPP has already secured the lead Parliamentary role: German MEP Peter Liese will steer the main file, with Poland's Adam Jarubas in charge of fallback benchmarks, per Playbook. One thing is certain, according to Euractiv: the Green Deal's ambitions will be scaled back, with free pollution permits expected to remain available well into the late 2030s.

**Von der Leyen in Kyiv; Zelenskyy reshuffles government amid protests**

European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen visited Kyiv on Wednesday, announcing a new drone scale-up scheme and encouraging EU member states to open the next cluster of accession negotiations with Ukraine, according to the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/). The visit was [interrupted by air raid sirens](https://www.politico.eu/article/russia-attacks-odesa-ursula-von-der-leyen-ukraine-trip/), sending officials and journalists into a shelter. The bigger story in the Ukrainian capital, however, was Zelenskyy's controversial government reshuffle: his reported decision to remove 35-year-old tech-savvy Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov prompted protests and consternation, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) reports. The new government is expected to be led by Naftogaz CEO Serhii Koretskyi. Deputy Prime Minister Taras Kachka, who leads Ukraine's EU accession talks, is also expected to change roles — EU officials speculate he may swap positions with the current Ukrainian ambassador to the EU, Vsevolod Chentsov.

**EU budget: Germany blocks new levies, eyes €400bn cut; Macron and Merz meet**

Germany is resisting new EU-wide levies for the 2028–2034 budget, arguing in an internal note seen by the [Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/center-left-strains-over-metsola-iii-support/) that proposed revenues are "nowhere near enough to offset the proposed sharp increase in the budget volume." Berlin backed a €400 billion overall cut — roughly a fifth of the Commission's proposal — and suggested the five Commission-proposed levies would yield €24 billion per year rather than the €65 billion forecast. The note sets up a direct confrontation with France, which backs own resources as a precondition for any budget deal. French President Emmanuel Macron and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz met on Thursday in North Rhine-Westphalia for the 26th Franco-German Ministerial Council, as the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports. Ireland, holding the Council presidency, is conducting one-to-one ambassador talks and hopes to have a negotiating box ready for an October summit; Europe Minister Thomas Byrne acknowledged the task is "very, very difficult."

**EU electrification rate stagnant at 23%; Commission package due Friday**

The EU's electrification rate stands at only 23% of final energy consumption — comparable to the United States and far behind China, Japan and South Korea, where it exceeds 30% — according to an analysis published Thursday by [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/16/depuis-2022-et-le-debut-de-la-guerre-en-ukraine-lelectrification-de-lunion-na-que-tres-peu-evolue/). The bloc had set a 32% target for 2030. Despite massive renewable deployment since 2022, fossil fuels still represent 58% of EU energy needs, and some 600 GW of renewable capacity is awaiting grid connection. IEA Director Fatih Birol called Europe's slow electrification a "major mistake." The Commission is due to present its electrification package on Friday, expected to include reduced electricity taxes, subsidies for heat pumps and EVs, and a cap ensuring electricity costs do not exceed 2.5 times the gas price for households by end of decade.

**European defence architecture takes shape after Bastille Day**

Ten European countries — Denmark, France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Spain, Sweden, Ukraine and the UK — announced the creation of an Integrated Ballistic Missile Defence Coalition on 13 July, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/16/le-pentagone-sen-prend-aux-puissances-moyennes/). The day after, troops from 35 nations marched on the Champs-Élysées before 25 heads of state — including Zelenskyy — in a formation explicitly parallel to NATO and without the United States. France and Ukraine simultaneously signed a roadmap for delivery of 16 Rafales (2027–28), SAMP/T NG air defence systems, SCALP cruise missiles and Ukrainian licensed production of Aster 30 missiles. US Under Secretary of Defense Elbridge Colby pushed back on 14 July, dismissing middle-power coordination as a "misunderstanding of international relations" and warning that "access to the American defence industrial base is a privilege, not a right." Le Grand Continent offered a detailed rebuttal, noting the US itself orders warships from Korean and Italian yards and turned to Ukraine for drone expertise. Conservative US commentator David Frum invoked the Delian League: "Under Donald Trump, the United States is perceived by its former allies as predatory, untrustworthy and deeply corrupt." Estonian Prime Minister Kristen Michal told Le Grand Continent simply: "Europe was a peace project without weapons. It is becoming a peace project with weapons."

## Italian Politics

**PD proposes revived state shareholdings; Seminerio sceptical**

Italy's Democratic Party is elaborating a white paper on industrial policy that would create a new agency for public shareholdings and convert Invitalia and Cassa Depositi e Prestiti into real industrial-policy vehicles, according to [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/15/il-compagno-trump-e-le-partecipazioni-statali/). The proposal comes amid a broader wave of state interventionism — from Trump's equity stakes in AI labs to UK Labour's reversal of privatisations. Phastidio's Mario Seminerio is unsparing: "the first phase of Italian state holdings produced national champions; it ended as a radioactive convalescent home for lost cases." There is, he notes, no money to fund the ambition, while France and Germany — the supposed models — are, respectively, in "a comatose state" and full manufacturing deindustrialisation.

## UK Politics

**Starmer's World Cup final dashed; Falklands row flares around Argentina match**

Argentina's semi-final victory over England has spoiled what the European Commission had hoped would be an all-European final, and deprived outgoing Prime Minister Keir Starmer of a trip to the final, as the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/center-left-strains-over-metsola-iii-support/) notes. A [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/sport-is-not-war-except-when-argentina-plays-england/) investigation found that Milei's shift reflects his close alliance with Trump — noting that a leaked Pentagon memo had suggested Washington could review its support for the British position on the Falklands in retaliation for UK foot-dragging on Iran. A Downing Street spokesperson said Starmer was "solely focused on the semi-final and securing a spot in the final." Milei faces a dilemma, however: 79% of Argentines support sovereignty claims "without concessions," but overplaying the issue risks straining his relationship with the White House. Argentinian veterans' groups urged restraint: "Sport is not war."

Oil eased slightly on Thursday as traders weighed conflicting signals, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) reports, but analysts warn that simultaneous threats to the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — from Houthi missiles targeting Saudi Arabia — could send prices toward $200 a barrel if Saudi Arabia's Red Sea pipeline route is closed (see Foreign Policy below). The [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) notes that investors and industry executives see mounting signs that Europe's financially weaker airlines may be headed for a shakeout as fuel costs climb.

**Uber bids $14.8bn for Delivery Hero**

Uber has launched a public takeover offer for Delivery Hero valuing the German food delivery company at $14.8 billion, which would create the world's largest food delivery firm outside China, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) reports. The deal would mark a significant consolidation in European tech and media.

**France faces "alarming" long-term pension trajectory**

France's pension monitoring committee (CSR) issued its annual report warning that financial prospects are "worrying in the medium term and alarming in the long term," as analysed by [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/13/francia-pensioni-lattine-e-comitati/). The pension system deficit is projected to reach 1.2% of GDP by 2050 and 2.4% by 2070, as INSEE has sharply lowered its long-term fertility assumption to 1.45 children per woman. The CSR warned that the 2026 suspension of the 2023 pension reform — which had gradually raised the retirement age — will cost €13 billion in additional accumulated debt by 2033. The only available lever, per the committee, is raising the employment rate of older workers; higher contributions are excluded as France's social charges are already among the OECD's highest. Seminerio on Phastidio warns French markets face a stress test this autumn, with presidential elections approaching.

## Foreign Policy

**Iran-US conflict: fifth night of strikes, Hormuz "red line," Houthi threat to Saudi lifeline**

The United States launched a fifth consecutive night of strikes against Iran, with Iran warning that the Strait of Hormuz is an inviolable "red line" and threatening to "resist until the end," as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) reports. A US naval blockade of Iranian ports took effect on Wednesday. The June ceasefire is unravelling: Iran's Foreign Ministry accused Washington of driving the memorandum of understanding "into crisis," while Trump declared the initial agreement "over," saying Iranian officials had not honoured their commitments. Hopes for resumed talks in Switzerland have "all but fizzled," per [Thomson Reuters Gulf Currents](https://www.reuters.com/). The conflict has opened a potentially more dangerous front: Yemen's Houthi movement fired missiles at Saudi Arabia for the first time in the war on 13 July, threatening the kingdom's Red Sea pipeline route — Aramco's workaround to the Hormuz closure that drove a 25% rise in first-quarter profit. A Houthi official warned that "the Bab el-Mandeb Strait and the Strait of Hormuz will be closed in an operational alliance," which Middle East scholar Fawaz Gerges described to Reuters as a signal that "not only Hormuz, but Bab al-Mandab, is at risk." Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps has threatened to close "all other export corridors that benefit the US and its allies," declaring: "regional energy exports are either shared by all, or denied to all." EU Foreign Policy Chief Kaja Kallas travelled to Djibouti on Thursday to visit the EU's Aspides and Atalanta naval missions; seven EU diplomats told the [Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/center-left-strains-over-metsola-iii-support/) that Brussels has no Plan B — "at least not until energy prices really take off." Trump separately posted that Iran had released detained US citizen Dena Karari, calling it "a gesture of goodwill."

**Estonia warns Russia is weaponising AI; Baltic states on high alert**

Estonian Prime Minister Kristen Michal, interviewed by [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/15/la-russie-de-poutine-est-en-difficulte-parce-que-notre-strategie-fonctionne-entretien-avec-le-premier-ministre-estonien/) at the Bastille Day coalition summit in Paris, warned that Russian cyberattacks against Estonia have tripled over the past three years, and that Moscow will deploy AI to "analyse, corrupt, alter and steal data at scale within six to eighteen months." He pushed back on those who cite Russia's difficulties as evidence that the threat is overstated: "Russia is in difficulty only because the European strategy is working — those who invoke Russia's difficulties to deny the threat are confusing cause and effect." Michal argued that any peace process must be between Ukraine and Russia alone, on Kyiv's terms; EU mediation, in his view, would cost Europe its leverage without yielding anything in return. Lithuania and Latvia separately issued public warnings, citing intelligence signals pointing to Russian plans to attack NATO critical infrastructure including energy and transport networks.
