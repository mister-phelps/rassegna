## EU Affairs

**Ireland's Presidency Faces €2 Trillion Budget Battle as Germany Demands Deep Cuts**
Ireland has kicked off its six-month presidency of the Council of the EU with an immediate reckoning: brokering agreement on the bloc's next seven-year budget by December. As [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com) reports, the €2 trillion multiannual financial framework is set to be Brussels' hardest in years, not least because a leaked German government position is calling for a €400 billion cut, describing a deal on the current draft as "impossible." A loose coalition of 16 cohesion-friendly states is pushing back against that framing, while EU budget commissioner Piotr Serafin has warned that a smaller budget would come at the cost of modernisation. Irish PM Micheál Martin acknowledged the scale of the task, saying it would mean "reconciling the irreconcilable." The current timetable foresees updated figures in October, a possible extraordinary summit in November, and a decisive European Council in December — with major elections in France and Italy next year adding urgency to the calendar.

**EU's 21st Russia Sanctions Package Exposes the Limits of Economic Pressure**
The European Commission has formally proposed its 21st sanctions package on Russia, and according to [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com), the exercise is becoming a monument to strategic exhaustion. The package adds 30 shadow fleet listings, transaction bans on 31 Russian banks and 20 third-country firms, and tightens export restrictions on metals. Its genuinely novel element is a six-month freeze on the EU's existing oil price cap — a technical necessity, since the cap is set to jump to above $60 per barrel on 15 July, above the current Urals crude price of around $56, which would render it meaningless. Germany and major importers are blocking a proposed fish import ban; France and Italy object to a travel ban on Russian soldiers on legal grounds; Bulgaria has called the proposed sanctioning of Patriarch Kirill a throwback to the "era of the Crusades." Despite the divisions, diplomats expect the package to be approved at the Foreign Affairs Council on 13 July. The Euractiv newsletter argues the deeper problem is structural: quoting RUSI senior fellow Richard Connolly, "wars are rarely abandoned because they become expensive," and Russia's economy, while strained, shows signs of adaptation — inflation has halved to 5.3% and real wages are at record highs.

**The End of the Atlantic Narrative as a European Opportunity**
Writing in [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/04/la-fin-du-recit-atlantique-et-nouveau-recit-europeen/), historian Ludovic Tournès argues that the American "grand estrangement" from Europe — a process decades in the making but accelerated sharply since Trump's return — represents a historic opening for the construction of a distinctly European narrative. Tournès traces the current US "originalism" back to an idealisation of the founding republic of the 18th century, manifest in Silicon Valley libertarianism, legal textualism on the Supreme Court, and Trump's explicit invocations of Andrew Jackson and William McKinley. He argues that Europeans who await a return to the post-1945 Atlantic order are mistaken: "there will be no return to normality" — that Atlanticist interlude was itself an exception in US history. Instead, Tournès calls on European artists, writers, and institutions to construct plural narratives of a shared continental identity, pointing to the vitality of festivals and cultural networks already operating across the EU.

## Economy & Markets

**Germany's Merz Government Unveils Tax Reform — Sceptics Question Whether It's More Than Fiscal Drag Restitution**
The Merz-Klingbeil government has presented a €10 billion fiscal package, as [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/02/riforma-fiscale-tedesca-italien-wir-kommen/) details in a thorough analysis. The centrepiece is relief for lower- and middle-income families — a dual-earner household with two children on €60,000 will save around €600 per year from 2028. The package is financed in part by lowering the threshold for the *Reichensteuer* top rate (45%) to €250,000, and by introducing an additional 47% bracket above €280,000. Mini-job contributions are also set to rise sharply. Mario Seminerio of Phastidio notes the package is far smaller than the €17–27 billion initially floated and questions whether it amounts to anything more than a partial rebate of inflation-driven fiscal drag — since Germany lacks a constitutional mechanism for automatic indexation. Productivity measures include a ban on phone-only sick notes (mandatory GP visit from day one), liberalised Sunday trading hours for bars and bakeries, and a simplified administrative reporting regime. The reform also includes a public housing construction company to address what Seminerio describes as a pan-European affordability crisis.

**Iran War Creates Largest Oil-Supply Disruption in Daily Terms; Peace Talks Advance**
As the Reuters Weekend Briefing reports, the war triggered by the US–Israeli strike that killed Supreme Leader Khamenei on 28 February has produced the largest single oil-supply disruption in history measured by daily production losses — though the 1979 Iranian Revolution remains the biggest crisis by cumulative loss. US–Iran talks concluded in Doha on 4 July, focused primarily on reopening the Strait of Hormuz; Reuters reports oil prices are "little changed" as peace efforts hold. Gulf oil exports rose to record levels in June on surging UAE flows.

## Foreign Policy

**Khamenei's Week-Long Funeral Rites Begin in Tehran, Testing Regime's Grip**
Iran has begun a week of national mourning for Ali Khamenei, who was killed on 28 February in the first US-Israeli airstrike of the Iran war. As [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/04/les-funerailles-dali-khamenei-un-moment-de-verite-pour-lavenir-du-regime-iranien/) reports in detail, and [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com) corroborates, the ceremonies are being framed by the Islamic Republic as a demonstration of resilience: 12–15 million people are expected to attend, and heads of government from Pakistan, Iraq, and Tajikistan, as well as officials from roughly 100 countries including China and Russia, have been announced as participants. The coffin will travel from Tehran's Mosalla mosque through the city to Qom, then to Najaf in Iraq — underscoring Iran's claim to Shia authority beyond its borders — and finally to Mashhad. The ceremonies carry acute political risk. The regime has loosened some social restrictions (mandatory hijab enforcement, restrictions on female singers) to pre-empt unrest, while simultaneously arresting dissidents: two prominent environmental activists were detained on 1 July. Crowds in Tehran chanted "Death to America" and "May God's curse fall upon Israel" throughout the morning. Le Grand Continent notes a sharp internal divide: ultraconservative figures are attacking the negotiators holding US–Iran peace talks, with one prominent voice declaring "I spit on this era where our leader is killed and we then speak of peace with America." New supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei has not been seen in public since his appointment in March.

**Putin Claims Victories on Territories That Do Not Exist; Russia Has Lost 1.4 Million Men**
[Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/03/vladimir-poutine-vante-des-conquetes-sur-des-territoires-qui-nexistent-pas-reellement/) documents a significant episode in the Kremlin's information war: in a 28 June interview with state media, Putin claimed Ukrainian forces were "about to be encircled" near the "Stary Oskol river" — a river that does not exist (Stary Oskol is a Russian city; the actual Oskol river runs through Ukrainian-controlled Kharkiv oblast). The Institute for the Study of War found no evidence of any encirclement in the area. Russia's actual territorial gains in the first half of 2026 amount to 770 km² — 2.5 times less than in the same period of 2025 — with June's uptick to 84 km² still well below Kremlin targets. In parallel, the Centre for Strategic and International Studies published a study on 1 July estimating Russia's total casualties since the invasion at 1.4 million (killed, wounded, or missing), equal to roughly 1% of the country's population. Russia currently controls around 19% of Ukrainian territory. Kiev, meanwhile, suffered its most massive missile and drone attack of the full-scale war in the night of 1–2 July.
