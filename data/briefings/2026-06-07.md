# Daily Briefing — Sunday, 7 June 2026

## EU Affairs

### Main stories

**Germany fails to win a UN Security Council seat for the first time ever.** In a 3 June General Assembly vote for two non-permanent Western European seats, Germany secured only 104 of the 127 votes needed for a two-thirds majority, while Portugal won 134 and Austria 131. It is the first time Berlin has lost such a vote, having previously sat on the Council for twelve years across five terms since 1977. Chancellor Friedrich Merz had personally dispatched Foreign Minister Johann Wadephul to lobby dozens of UN missions in New York; most observers attribute the defeat to Germany's pronounced pro-Israel stance since October 2023 and its refusal to condemn Israeli conduct in Gaza and the West Bank, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/05/pourquoi-lallemagne-a-perdu-le-vote-pour-sieger-au-conseil-de-securite-de-lonu/).

**Paris and Brussels clash over who should run Europe's power grids.** President Macron's newly unveiled "Plan d'électrification des usages" — targeting 600,000 new jobs and cutting fossil fuels below 30% of the energy mix by 2035, from 58% in 2023 — requires grid operator RTE to find some €100 billion over fifteen years and lay 45,000km of new lines, a programme already straining RTE's finances and prompting government cash injections. This collides with the EU's Energy Grids Package, part of the AccelerateEU plan agreed in late April and costed at €1,200 billion, which would centralise grid financing and planning at EU level and override national regulators' authorisation timetables. France — Europe's largest electricity exporter, with record export volumes in 2024-25 — is leading a coalition with Germany's backing, plus Bulgaria, Finland, Poland and Sweden, to keep grid management national or regional rather than centralised, [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/06/une-gestion-europeenne-des-reseaux-est-elle-compatible-avec-le-plan-francais-dacceleration-de-lelectrification/) reports.

**An FT investigation flags Morocco as a route for Chinese industry into the EU market.** Mario Seminerio, writing on [phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/01/marocco-cavallo-di-troia-cinese-in-ue/), relays Financial Times reporting on the Mohammed VI Tanger Tech City — a 500-hectare zone hosting around a dozen Chinese auto-component makers, including a $1.3 billion Gotion High-Tech gigafactory partly owned by Volkswagen. Rhodium Group estimates some $6 billion in Chinese investment has flowed into Morocco since the pandemic, and the kingdom is now the EU's top automotive supplier by value (€15.1 billion in 2023, ahead of China's €13.6 billion, and over €26 billion in total exports to the EU in 2025). Trade Commissioner Maroš Šefčovič has warned of "transshipment" risk, and the EU has twice penalised Moroccan exports for circumventing Chinese tariffs (fibreglass in 2022, aluminium wheels in March 2025, with duties of 5.6%-31.4%). Brussels' draft Industrial Accelerator Act — meant to lift EU manufacturing's share of GDP from 14.3% to 20% by 2035 — reportedly contains a loophole that would let Chinese-owned plants in Morocco qualify as "EU-origin," against which the auto-parts lobby Clepa is now pushing back.

## Italian Politics

### Main stories

**A row over Italy's youth-unemployment figures shows how a single statistic can flatter a government.** Economist Marco Fortis wrote in Il Messaggero that Italian youth unemployment fell from 20.3% in April 2025 to 16.9% in April 2026 — the largest such drop anywhere in the EU. Mario Seminerio checked the underlying Istat data on [phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/05/basta-qualche-ciliegia-e-la-disoccupazione-va-giu/) and found the apparent improvement is driven almost entirely by 134,000 more 15-24-year-olds becoming economically inactive (the inactivity rate up 1.9 percentage points) and 63,000 fewer of them actively seeking work — while actual employment in that age cohort fell by 40,000. In short, fewer young Italians are counted as unemployed chiefly because more of them have stopped looking for work altogether, not because more are finding it.

## UK Politics

### Main stories

**David Lammy publicly rebukes JD Vance over the Henry Nowak murder, despite their friendship.** The US vice-president posted on Friday that the murdered teenager — wrongly accused of a racist assault before he died — would still be alive "had Europe stood its ground...against the mass invasion of migrants." Lammy told the BBC's Laura Kuenssberg the claim has "got nothing to do with mass migration," noting the killer was "Brit born and raised," and said he had personally called Vance, a long-time friend, to make his disagreement known. The deputy prime minister also declined to rule out reviewing the religious exemption that lets Sikhs carry a ceremonial kirpan — something Nowak's family has called for — saying any "privilege" can be withdrawn if it "doesn't command the full confidence of the public," according to [POLITICO's Sunday Crunch](https://www.politico.eu/newsletters/sunday-crunch) (Noah Keate). Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch separately used a Sunday Times column to accuse police of "institutional incompetence" in their handling of the case.

**Keir Starmer hosts a Downing Street summit on Ukraine while fighting to keep his job.** The prime minister was due to welcome Emmanuel Macron, Friedrich Merz and Volodymyr Zelenskyy on Sunday afternoon for talks on Kyiv's fight against Russia, even as the Sun on Sunday reports he has spent the weekend telling key supporters he will "fight" any leadership challenge — widely expected to be fronted by Andy Burnham, with former transport secretary Louise Haigh emerging as a key campaign organiser and Wes Streeting insisting his outsider status "works in [his] favour." GMB leader Gary Smith, no natural Starmer ally, warned that the infighting "plays to the worst fears and prejudices of the electorate," POLITICO's Sunday Crunch reports. The Sunday Times adds that Starmer plans to fund the delayed defence investment plan partly by cutting net-zero, transport and capital spending.

**Ministers plan to compel tech firms to block under-age nude-image sharing.** The Observer's Rachel Sylvester reports the government will this week tell platforms to install software preventing children from taking or sharing explicit images, with legislation threatened if firms do not comply; a wider ban on social media for under-16s is also under consideration, though the Sun on Sunday hears YouTube Kids may be exempted owing to its educational content — coverage relayed by [POLITICO's Sunday Crunch](https://www.politico.eu/newsletters/sunday-crunch).

## Foreign Policy

### Main stories

**Armenians vote in an election that could determine the country's geopolitical course.** Sunday's parliamentary election pits Prime Minister Nikol Pashinyan's Civil Contract — in power since the 2018 "Velvet Revolution" but scarred by the 2020 defeat to Azerbaijan and the 2023 loss of Nagorno-Karabakh, which displaced more than 100,000 ethnic Armenians — against the pro-Moscow Armenian Alliance of ex-president Robert Kocharian and businessman Samvel Karapetian's Armenia Forte (Karapetian was arrested last June on charges of inciting a coup). Pre-election polling cited by [POLITICO's Sunday Crunch](https://www.politico.eu/newsletters/sunday-crunch) put Civil Contract on 32% to Strong Armenia's 11%; separately, Reuters had Pashinyan on roughly 30% and his rivals on 6-11% — additional context via Reuters/EVN Report. The European Commission pledged €50 million in aid on 4 June, shortly after Moscow suspended Armenian agricultural imports, and Donald Trump has publicly backed Pashinyan; per Bloomberg sources, a Kremlin team under deputy chief of staff Sergei Kiriyenko is working to counter Armenia's pivot West, while Vladimir Putin has called for a referendum on EU membership, [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/07/elections-en-armenie-vers-un-troisieme-mandat-pour-pachinian/) reports.

**Pope Leo XIV begins a week-long visit to Spain — the first papal trip there in fifteen years.** The pontiff was received in Madrid by King Felipe VI and Pedro Sánchez's government, with engagements including an open-air Corpus Christi Mass and an address to several hundred thousand expected at the Santiago Bernabéu stadium; on 8 June he addresses the Spanish Congress, a gesture impossible under France's secular system but permitted under Spain's concordat arrangements. The visit unfolds against tension between a Spanish episcopate accused of an ambivalent memory of the Franco era and Sánchez's left-leaning government, though the two sides recently reached a historic agreement on compensating victims of clerical child abuse. From 9 June the Pope travels to Catalonia to bless the newly completed Jesus Christ tower of the Sagrada Família — at 172.5 metres, now the world's tallest religious building — before ending his trip in the Canary Islands, where he is expected to call for compassion towards migrants on the perilous Atlantic route, on which the IOM estimates more than 2,300 people died in 2024-25 alone, [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/06/leon-xiv-se-rend-en-espagne-pour-la-premiere-visite-papale-depuis-15-ans/) reports.

**Starmer convenes Macron, Merz and Zelenskyy in London as the Ukraine war passes a grim milestone.** The Downing Street summit — called as the conflict surpassed the length of the First World War with no ceasefire in sight — comes after Ukraine struck deep into St Petersburg over the weekend, having endured renewed Russian drone attacks; the Observer reports that Kyiv's drone production has surged thanks to expanded domestic manufacturing, POLITICO's Sunday Crunch notes.
