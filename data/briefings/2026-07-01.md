# Daily Briefing — Wednesday, 1 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**Ireland takes the EU Council helm, with the MFF looming large**
Ireland assumed the rotating EU Council presidency from Cyprus today, with the opening ceremony held at Dublin Castle. Thomas Byrne, Ireland's European Affairs Minister, told the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/the-irish-go-for-broker/) that his country knows what it has signed up for: "Lots of people will be unhappy with a presidency when they prepare a negotiating box — we have to be ready for that." The dominant dossier will be the EU's next multiannual financial framework (MFF), with enlargement and defence also high on Dublin's agenda. Commissioner Michael McGrath, the Irishman in the College, said Ireland's reputation as an "honest broker" gives it "a real opportunity to help steer the union through a period of significant negotiations." Diplomatically, the handover from Cyprus drew mixed reviews: one EU diplomat cited by Playbook was blunt, saying Cyprus had been "lost and very dependent on the General Secretariat of the Council." The Commissioners will gather in Cork on Thursday for a special College meeting, arriving by chartered flight rather than, as Playbook noted with amusement, budget airline.

**EPP hardens migration stance before German regional elections**
The European People's Party adopted a resolution at its Vienna political assembly on Tuesday calling for a further tightening of EU migration policy, according to [Euractiv's Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/). The CDU secured an amendment demanding that the Commission propose "further legislative measures to prevent illegal migration," including an explicit right to reject asylum claims where migrants are being "instrumentalised" at the EU's borders. Most notably, the EPP called to "abolish the status of subsidiary protection" — granted to those who do not qualify as refugees but face serious harm if returned — to speed up returns after civil wars. The driving logic is the electoral calendar: two German regional elections in September have EPP partners worried, with the far-right AfD polling ahead of Merz's CDU. The signal from Vienna, Euractiv notes, is that the centre-right is no longer simply focused on faster returns but is now seeking to narrow access to protection itself.

**EPPO raids target former far-right ID group over €4.33 million in EU funds**
Investigators working with the European Public Prosecutor's Office conducted coordinated raids across France, Belgium, Italy, and Spain on Tuesday as part of a probe into the alleged misuse of €4.33 million in European Parliament funds by the former Identity and Democracy group, which included France's National Rally, according to [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/). French newspaper Le Canard Enchaîné also reported that prosecutors were considering opening an investigation into RN president Jordan Bardella over allegations of document forgery. Bardella denied wrongdoing, saying "we have nothing to be ashamed of." The [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/the-irish-go-for-broker/) also noted the raids, citing Le Monde.

**Montenegro accession treaty cleared for drafting**
EU governments approved the drafting of Montenegro's accession treaty on Tuesday, paving the way for the Balkan country to join the bloc in 2028. As [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports, the European Commission estimated membership would cost the EU approximately €3.2 billion between 2028 and 2034 — less than €1 per EU citizen per year — while boosting growth and integration. The move was framed in part as an attempt to reassure EU capitals as budget negotiations intensify that enlargement remains affordable.

**EU's €3 parcel levy on low-value e-commerce imports takes effect**
From today, the EU imposes a €3 customs charge on low-value parcels arriving from outside the bloc, ending the long-standing €150 de minimis exemption. As the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/the-irish-go-for-broker/) explains, the charge applies by goods category within a parcel — buying a T-shirt and a watch in the same consignment triggers two separate €3 fees. The measure is primarily aimed at Chinese platforms such as Temu, Shein, and AliExpress and is described as temporary pending a broader customs overhaul. MEP Anna Cavazzini called it "long overdue." Brussels says more than 5.9 billion low-value parcels entered the EU in 2025, up from 4.6 billion in 2024.

## UK Politics

**Starmer's Defence Investment Plan draws fire from his own side**
Prime Minister Keir Starmer on Tuesday announced a £15 billion increase in defence spending over the next four years — his Defence Investment Plan (DIP) — bringing the Ministry of Defence to 2.7% of national income by 2028, up from the current 2.6%, according to [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk/). The Times reports that Andy Burnham, Starmer's successor-in-waiting as Labour leader (due to take office 17 July) and then Prime Minister (19 July), faces a £5 billion funding gap as a result and will need to raise taxes or cut spending to comply with the government's fiscal rules. Defence Secretary Dan Jarvis conceded in the Commons that "we will need to do more." Former defence secretary John Healey, who resigned three weeks ago over insufficient funding, criticised the absence of a "clear, credible funding plan" towards 3% of GDP, warning that "European security is at stake." The biggest winner in the DIP is the Tempest sixth-generation fighter aircraft, built with Japan and Italy, which receives £8.6 billion over four years.

## Economy & Markets

**Russia's oil revenues collapse as export volumes hit a record high**
Russian seaborne crude oil exports reached 4.6 million barrels per day in the week of 21–28 June — the highest level since the start of the full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 — yet revenues have fallen sharply, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/01/economie-russe-malgre-une-hausse-des-volumes-dexportations-de-petrole-les-revenus-diminuent/). The Ural crude benchmark has collapsed by 62% from its April peak of $118 per barrel; by 26 June a barrel traded at $45, its lowest level since late February. The trigger is the US-Iran ceasefire, which has returned Iranian supply to markets. At $1.7 billion for the last week of June, Russia's weekly oil revenues have halved compared to May. Compounding the pressure, Ukrainian drone strikes on refineries have created domestic fuel shortages: restrictions on fuel purchases are now in place across 56 Russian regions, and Putin himself acknowledged the problem at a Kremlin meeting with energy executives on 28 June. Russian farmers have warned they may be unable to harvest their crops without financial assistance.

**Chinese logistics build a US beachhead as tariffs backfire**
Rather than curbing China's presence in the US market, American tariff barriers have pushed Chinese e-commerce companies to build integrated door-to-door logistics networks on US soil, [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/01/la-logistica-cinese-tira-il-pacco-a-trump/) reports in an analysis drawing on a Nikkei Asia investigation. International shipping, warehousing, and last-mile delivery for platforms such as Temu, Shein, and AliExpress are increasingly handled by Chinese-controlled operators, whose volume grew 13% last year and is putting pressure on UPS, FedEx, and even the US Postal Service. The shift was accelerated by the abolition of the de minimis exemption, prompting a switch to bulk imports and local stock. Phastidio also notes illicit practices — including deliberate mis-classification of goods at customs and use of shell-company importers of record — alongside legitimate cost-minimisation techniques. A Trump executive order seeks to tighten CBP scrutiny of importers of record, though Phastidio notes the agency was designed for counter-narcotics, not parcel inspection. The EU's own €3 parcel charge, entering into force today, is the European counterpart to the same problem.

## Foreign Policy

**US and Iran hold technical talks in Doha as ceasefire takes shape**
The United States and Iran held technical talks in Doha on Wednesday aimed at formalising the flow of shipping through the Strait of Hormuz and securing a lasting ceasefire, according to [the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/). The talks follow the US-Iran agreement that ended the recent conflict and contributed to the sharp fall in oil prices. Separately, Homeland Security Secretary Markwayne Mullin told reporters he had "danced a happy dance" when Iran was knocked out of the FIFA World Cup, according to [Politico Forecast](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/), illustrating the degree to which the Iran file remains politically charged within the Trump administration even as diplomacy advances.

**Trump suffers Supreme Court defeat on birthright citizenship**
The US Supreme Court on Tuesday rejected President Trump's executive order seeking to restrict birthright citizenship, ruling it unconstitutional, as [the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com/) and [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk/) both report. The court did, however, allow states to ban transgender student athletes from women's sports teams and struck down further campaign finance limits — outcomes described by Reuters as a mixed day for conservatives despite the birthright ruling representing the case that mattered most to Trump personally.

**Latin America's rightward turn accelerates: seven Trump-aligned presidents since 2025**
With Keiko Fujimori's confirmation as Peru's new president — narrowly elected on 7 June with 50.13% of the vote — seven heads of state who claim or display proximity to Donald Trump have come to power in Latin America since January 2025, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/01/effet-domino-en-amerique-latine-7-presidents-proches-ou-alignes-sur-trump-ont-ete-elus-depuis-2025/). Ecuador, Bolivia, Chile, Costa Rica, Colombia, and Honduras have all elected right or far-right presidents in the past 18 months, reversing the "pink tide" that had crested from Mexico in 2018 to Brazil and Colombia in 2022. Le Grand Continent counts 12 Trump-aligned or Trump-friendly presidents across the region. Campaigns have typically emphasised Trump-style themes: anti-immigration measures and hard-line security policies. Colombia's new president Abelardo de la Espriella, who took Milei and Bukele as models, has given armed groups one month to surrender and ruled out any state negotiations with them.

**China's covert training of Russian forces approved by Putin's defence minister**
China's covert military training of Russian forces last year was personally approved by President Putin's defence minister and directly involved at least four Russian and Chinese generals, according to two European officials and documents seen by [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/), as reported in the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing.
