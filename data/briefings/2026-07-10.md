# Daily Briefing — Friday, 10 July 2026

## EU Affairs


**Von der Leyen to launch EU social media ban for children next week**
European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen will make a public statement on Monday accepting the findings of an expert report she commissioned on the harmful effects of digital platforms on young people, as the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/the-drive-to-get-kids-off-social-media/) reports. The move marks the starting gun for an EU-wide ban that could bar under-13s — or under-16s, with the final age limit still unresolved — from platforms such as Instagram, Facebook and X. Fourteen member states are already on board, with Poland's digital affairs minister Dariusz Standerski telling Playbook there is "a strong majority" and no likely blocking minority. Estonia's justice and digital affairs minister Liisa-Ly Pakosta dissented, calling the process "a little bit biased" and detecting "a kind of parental panic" behind the initiative. Officials are working over the summer ahead of von der Leyen's State of the Union address in September. [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) notes that implementation raises unresolved questions around minimum age-setting, how parental consent would work, and whether restrictions would apply to whole platforms or only certain features.

**EU foreign ministers to discuss Israeli settlement trade restrictions on Monday**
EU foreign ministers are set to discuss options for restricting trade with illegal Israeli settlements in the West Bank at Monday's Foreign Affairs Council, according to [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/the-drive-to-get-kids-off-social-media/) and [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/). A Commission options paper — drafted by von der Leyen's cabinet — was leaked to the press ahead of the meeting, prompting Commission spokesperson Paula Pinho to call the leak "unacceptable" and announce an internal investigation. The paper makes clear the Commission views the move as requiring unanimity among the 27, a threshold "well out of reach." Separately, the European External Action Service is preparing fresh sanctions targeting extremist Israeli settlers and six settlement entities in the West Bank, [Euractiv's Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/) reveals, alongside new measures against Hamas. Belgium's foreign minister Maxime Prévot told Politico that "for once, things are moving in the right direction." The EU will also host the Palestine Donor Group on Monday to press the Palestinian Authority on governance reforms in exchange for financial support.

**Net contributor nations threaten to walk away from €2 trillion MFF talks**
A senior official from a significant net payer country has warned that wealthier EU members would have little to lose by abandoning negotiations on the 2028–2034 Multiannual Financial Framework, as [Politico's Nicholas Vinocur](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/the-drive-to-get-kids-off-social-media/) reports. "For the net contributors, a world without an MFF is by far the least costly option," the official said, estimating that a no-deal outcome would see net payers spend €500–600 billion less than under the Commission's proposal. Under a no-deal scenario, the ceiling for the final year of the current MFF would be rolled over at around €1.4 trillion, without inflation adjustment — and cohesion policies that net recipients depend on would expire, since net contributors hold a blocking minority for any extension. The statement raises the stakes further in what Politico describes as a "more combative phase" of negotiations.

**Russia's 21st sanctions package watered down on soldier visa ban**
France, Italy and Greece successfully pushed to dilute a Baltic-led proposal to bar former Russian soldiers from entering the EU under the bloc's latest sanctions package, [Euractiv's Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports. The original measure would have imposed a broad visa ban on any Russian who had fought in Ukraine; under the amended version now being discussed by ambassadors, the restriction covers only short-term visas for those who actively participated in combat or contributed directly to military operations. Greece also secured a weakening of provisions on Russian LNG transit in exchange for backing a freeze of the oil price cap. France handed out the most visas to Russian nationals in 2025, when the EU issued more than 477,000 Russian visas in total.

**EU enlargement to inch forward before summer recess**
Four candidate countries are expected to make incremental progress in their accession bids on Tuesday, just before Brussels breaks for the summer, [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports. Ukraine and Moldova are set to open their second cluster of reform negotiations, covering external relations and foreign policy — though many EU countries had hoped Kyiv would advance further. Montenegro aims to close three chapters (competition policy, customs union, and transport policy), while Albania targets three (science and research, education, and external relations). Enlargement Commissioner Marta Kos met Montenegrin Prime Minister Milojko Spajić in Brussels on Friday.

**Hungary's €10.4 billion Covid recovery funds to be unfrozen**
EU finance ministers were expected to greenlight Hungary's revised Covid recovery plan on Friday, which would release €10.4 billion in funds previously frozen over rule-of-law concerns, [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports. Since Péter Magyar's government took office, it has rushed through reforms to satisfy the Commission. Budapest must still meet "supermilestones" on anti-corruption and transparency before a 31 August deadline, after which the funds expire. Approval requires backing from all 27 ministers.

## UK Politics

**Burnham all but certain to become prime minister**
Andy Burnham has secured the support of 322 Labour MPs — one short of an outright guarantee of an uncontested succession — and is widely expected to become the UK's next prime minister, as [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk/) and [Thomson Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) report. The former Greater Manchester mayor won the backing of deputy prime minister David Lammy, foreign secretary Yvette Cooper, and health secretary Wes Streeting, among other cabinet ministers. [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk/) notes he could officially become prime minister on 20 July if his campaign remains uncontested by next Friday. Burnham has apologised for the Labour government's response to Israel's actions in Gaza, saying the country needs to "do better," and has pledged to rebuild Britain's "hard power" in his first major foreign policy intervention. [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/10/regno-unito-la-sindrome-del-welfare/) highlights the challenge awaiting him on welfare: disability payments under the Personal Independence Payment scheme have reached 4 million recipients in England and Wales, costing £32 billion this fiscal year and projected to reach £41.5 billion by 2031, with new claims running at 78,000 per month — double the pre-Covid rate and driven heavily by mental health conditions. Previous governments, including Starmer's, failed to push through savings on the programme after backbench rebellions.

**Reform UK faces criminal probe over donation**
The Metropolitan Police have launched a criminal investigation into donations made to Reform UK by the mother of George Cotrell, as [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk/) reports. The inquiry has rocked Nigel Farage's election campaign — he is also facing a by-election contest against the satirical candidate Count Binface, according to the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/the-drive-to-get-kids-off-social-media/).

## Foreign Policy

**Iran: Khamenei buried as ceasefire frays and successor stays hidden**
The slain Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was buried at Iran's holiest shrine on Friday after a massive public funeral, with his son and designated successor Mojtaba Khamenei still absent from public view, as [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) and [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk/) report. US airstrikes on Iranian railways disrupted the burial proceedings. Iranian armed forces subsequently announced attacks on US military infrastructure in Gulf states, further eroding a three-week-old ceasefire. [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk/) describes the war as "close to resuming," and Reuters reports that oil tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has fallen to near a standstill as attacks strain the truce. Oil prices were heading for a weekly gain on the back of heightened supply risks.

**Russia consolidates military and commercial foothold in Syria**
Russia is expected to launch a commercial logistics hub at its naval base in the Syrian port of Tartous within days, handling an initial volume of 250,000 tonnes per month of wheat, vegetable oils, steel, coal and other goods, [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/10/la-russie-consolide-sa-presence-en-syrie/) reports, citing Syrian officials. The development follows Macron's visit to Damascus on 6–7 July — the first by a Western head of state since Ahmed al-Sharaa came to power — and al-Sharaa's subsequent attendance at the NATO summit in Ankara on 8 July. Trump stated his intention to remove Syria from the US list of state sponsors of terrorism, a decision expected to take effect on 22 August. Moscow's position is reinforced by Syria's continued dependence on Russia for 85% of its wheat imports and the bulk of its oil imports; satellite imagery in May showed a Russian cargo vessel carrying military materiel resupplying the Hmeimim air base. Le Grand Continent notes that Tartous and Hmeimim remain essential for Russia's Mediterranean presence and its military operations in Africa.

**NATO summit: Erdoğan distributes vintage revolvers, Turkey and F-35 in focus**
After NATO leaders gathered in Ankara, host president Recep Tayyip Erdoğan gave each departing leader a vintage revolver with live ammunition as a parting gift, [Thomson Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/) reports. Greece's defence minister Nikos Dendias separately said Athens opposes any US move to readmit Turkey to the F-35 programme, after Trump signalled at the summit that Washington was prepared to lift sanctions on Ankara. Dendias said Washington should consider whether supplying advanced fighters to a country that "still threatens an EU member state" serves American interests, [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports.

**Ukraine: exclusive survey reveals public opinion on war and Trump**
