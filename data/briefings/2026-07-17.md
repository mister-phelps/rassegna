# Daily Briefing — Friday, 17 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**Commission unveils contentious ETS overhaul, exposing deep divides**
The European Commission today published a major rewrite of the EU's Emissions Trading System — its flagship carbon market — bowing to pressure from industry and several member states. According to [Politico's Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/green-vs-growth-in-ets-showdown/), the proposed changes would push back the zero-emissions deadline for covered sectors beyond the current 2039 target, relaxing the pace at which free allowances are phased out. The Euractiv Rapporteur newsletter notes that the ETS has roughly halved emissions from covered sectors since 2005 — eliminating around 1 billion tonnes of greenhouse gases a year — but that energy-intensive industries argue the costs are becoming unsustainable. The reform has already exposed fault lines inside the Commission: [Politico reports](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/green-vs-growth-in-ets-showdown/) that at least three cabinets formally objected at a pre-meeting — those of Industry Commissioner Stéphane Séjourné, Transport Commissioner Apostolos Tzitzikostas, and Raffaele Fitto — while the European People's Party has begun a campaign for an "even more moderate" approach and has installed Germany's Peter Liese as the lead MEP on the file. Italy, Poland and Greece are pushing hardest for a relaxed regime; Spain and the Nordic states are holding the line. Sweden's Europe Minister Jessica Rosencrantz told Politico that "the ETS works. Europe should build on its success, not retreat from it." The battle, both sources agree, will define EU politics for the remainder of the year.

**Franco-German summit: defence reset and a fraught transition**
Emmanuel Macron and Friedrich Merz met in Paris on Thursday for a joint Franco-German ministerial council, seeking to turn the page on disagreements over the next-generation fighter jet project and push ahead on defence cooperation, competitiveness and digital rules, the Euractiv Rapporteur reports. The two leaders are expected to advance closer coordination on nuclear deterrence. The meeting marks Macron's final Franco-German ministerial council before he leaves office next year, with Marine Le Pen's Rassemblement National a leading contender for the 2027 presidential election — a fact that gives the partnership's future an air of uncertainty. [Politico's Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/green-vs-growth-in-ets-showdown/) frames the summit as part of a broader effort to "reassure doubters that the EU's traditional power couple can still deliver."

**US launches MAGA grants programme targeting European civil society**
The US State Department's Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights and Labour (DRL) on 13 July launched a call for proposals — worth up to $5 million in grants of $1–3 million each — to develop "civilisational bonds, democratic resilience and the rule of law in Europe," [Le Grand Continent reports](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/17/offensive-maga-contre-leurope-les-etats-unis-lancent-un-programme-de-financement-dorganisations-alignees/). This is the first time since 2025 that the Trump administration has sought to advance its ideological agenda in Europe through direct financial support. Analyst Maya Kandel, cited by LGC, describes the bureau — historically a defender of civil society against authoritarian regimes — as now "officially the instrument and the funder of civil society actors mobilised, inside allied European countries, against their own institutions." Potential recipients could include organisations aligned with the MAGA agenda, including think tanks modelled on Viktor Orbán's Danube Institute network. German Chancellor Merz publicly warned Washington against interfering in German elections following the announcement. On the same day, Elon Musk posted on X that Marine Le Pen was "France's last hope." LGC's analysis characterises the programme alongside Secretary of State Marco Rubio's simultaneous convening of a meeting on "global left-wing terrorism" — in which Europe is described as an "incubator of terrorist threats" — as "two facets of the same enterprise of ideological vassalisation."

**Pentagon's Colby attacks "middle power strategy" — and reveals US anxiety**
Elbridge Colby, the US Under-Secretary for Defence Policy and number three at what the Trump administration has renamed the Department of War, published a widely-discussed X thread on 14 July dismissing the emerging concept of a "collective middle power strategy" as "a distraction" and warning that "access to the American defence industrial base is a privilege, not a right." [Le Grand Continent translated and annotated the text line by line](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/16/le-pentagone-sen-prend-aux-puissances-moyennes/), arguing that it betrays anxiety rather than confidence: Colby's intervention came the day after 10 European countries — Denmark, France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Spain, Sweden, Ukraine and the United Kingdom — announced the launch of an Integrated Coalition Against Ballistic Missiles, and after Macron and Zelensky signed a roadmap envisaging the delivery of 16 Rafale jets to Ukraine between 2027 and 2028. LGC's commentary notes that the US Navy now commissions ships from Korean and Italian shipyards, and that American operations against Iran have depended on European bases, undermining Colby's claim of unrivalled industrial supremacy. David Frum, responding on X, drew a parallel with the Delian League: "one can extort resources from subjects, but subjects will seek a way out."

**EU Rule of Law report takes aim at Spain and Hungary**
The Commission's annual Rule of Law report — grading member states on judicial independence, anti-corruption safeguards, media freedom and institutional checks — lands at today's College of Commissioners meeting, with a particular focus on Spain and Hungary, [Politico's Playbook reports](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/green-vs-growth-in-ets-showdown/). Justice Commissioner Michael McGrath said the report "gives us a clear picture of where we are succeeding and where challenges remain."

**Fitto appointed as Commission's special representative for Cyprus**
Raffaele Fitto has been named the European Commission's special representative for Cyprus, signalling Brussels' intent to play a more political role in resolving Europe's longest-running frozen conflict, the Euractiv Rapporteur reports. Officials see a rare diplomatic opening, with renewed talks expected and Turkey's role in EU defence architecture now formally recognised. One diplomat noted Italy's traditionally strong ties with Ankara as an asset; Turkish Cypriots, however, dismissed the appointment as "unilateral and illegitimate."

**EU home affairs ministers clash over Russian visas; sanctions package stalls**
EU home affairs ministers at an informal meeting in Dublin revisited the divide over Russian citizens still obtaining Schengen visas, after Baltic states urged tighter restrictions. Euractiv first reported that France and Italy issued the highest number of visas to Russian nationals in 2025. EU migration chief Magnus Brunner said "it's still too many," and backed a proposed measure in the bloc's 21st sanctions package to ban entry for anyone who has served in the Russian armed forces since the full-scale invasion. EU capitals failed to approve the package on Wednesday; talks resume next week. Greece's opposition centres on shipping billionaire George Prokopiou's company Dynagas, which has made its fortune from shipping Russian crude and LNG, and for which Athens has demanded an open-ended sanctions derogation.

## UK Politics

**Burnham confirmed as Labour leader; to become Prime Minister Monday**
Andy Burnham was confirmed as Labour leader on Friday, backed by 379 of the party's 403 MPs and unopposed in the contest, and is set to become Prime Minister on Monday following Keir Starmer's resignation, the Euractiv Rapporteur reports. The former Greater Manchester mayor inherits a sluggish economy and mounting pressure from Nigel Farage's Reform UK. He has promised to shift power from Westminster to England's regions. [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/15/il-compagno-trump-e-le-partecipazioni-statali/) notes that Burnham's inheritance includes an out-of-control disability welfare bill — a "bomb in the public finances" — having already weighed on the Starmer government.

**Falklands banner row: UK demands FIFA inquiry**
Argentina players unfurled a banner reading "Las Malvinas son Argentinas" after their World Cup semi-final victory against England, triggering a diplomatic incident. Business Secretary Peter Kyle called on FIFA to launch a "proper inquiry" into the incident, as did Prime Minister Starmer's spokesperson, who said: "The World Cup might not be ours, but the Falkland Islands definitely are," [Politico's Forecast newsletter reports](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/forecast/). The row came days before Burnham takes office, and [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/16/pourquoi-la-guerre-des-malouines-resurgit-dans-le-football-argentin/) published a detailed analysis of why the Falklands conflict has re-emerged so forcefully in Argentine football culture.

## Economy & Markets

**The AI distillation war — and what it means for Europe**
[Phastidio.net published a detailed analysis](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/16/ai-il-contrappasso-distillato/) of the US–China dispute over AI "distillation" — the practice of training a model using outputs from another — arguing that US frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) are conflating a competitive threat with a national security emergency. Anthropic claims Chinese firm Moonshot AI created hundreds of fake accounts to accumulate 3.4 million Claude conversations; Congress is debating sanctions. Phastidio contends the real threat is not distillation but the success of cheap, open-weight Chinese models — and that US government restrictions could perversely damage American developers. The European angle, the analysis argues, is counterintuitive: if AI advantage shifts from capital scale to open, efficient models, Europe's position may be less marginal than commonly assumed. The Euractiv AI newsletter separately warns that Europe "could be double-kneecapped" if China restricts access to its leading models, having already been shut out of Anthropic's most advanced tools after the Trump administration barred non-US citizens.

**US imposes 25% tariff on Brazil; SpaceX shares slip**
The US will impose a 25% tariff on most imports from Brazil starting 22 July — the first action under the Trump administration's new tariff strategy that could eventually affect dozens of countries, Reuters reports. In markets, SpaceX shares have slipped below their IPO price of $135, an ominous sign for the company ahead of a period of heightened volatility in early August.

## Foreign Policy

**US strikes Iran for sixth consecutive night; Tehran warns on Hormuz**
US forces struck Iran for a sixth straight night on Thursday, hitting targets on Qeshm Island and near Bandar Abbas, on the Strait of Hormuz, Reuters and the Politico Brussels Playbook report. Iran responded by declaring the Strait an inviolable "red line," warning that any attack on its broader infrastructure would trigger strikes across Gulf region infrastructure. Reuters reports that recent waves of US strikes also aim to degrade Iranian military capabilities in advance of potentially more complex operations. Some commercial vessels are refusing US military-guided transits through the Strait, according to Reuters sources, clouding energy market outlooks. JD Vance, speaking on a podcast with Joe Rogan, separately claimed that some members of the Israeli government had tried to influence US public opinion against a deal to end the war.

**Trump's Chinese-interference speech: preparation for midterm disruption?**
In an overnight address to the nation, Donald Trump announced that his administration is declassifying documents he claims show Chinese interference in US elections, and threatened to revoke the broadcast licences of television networks that did not carry the speech live, according to the Politico Brussels Playbook and [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/17/donald-trump-prepare-t-il-le-terrain-a-la-disruption-des-elections-de-mi-mandat-texte-integral/), which published a full French translation and commentary. LGC editor Gilles Gressani frames the speech as a possible groundwork-laying for disrupting the upcoming mid-term elections, noting the timing alongside escalations against Iran and the broader domestic political context.

**Zelenskyy fires reforming defence minister; protests erupt in Ukraine**
President Volodymyr Zelenskyy dismissed 35-year-old tech reformer Mykhailo Fedorov as defence minister, citing an irreconcilable breakdown in his relationship with Army Commander General Oleksandr Syrskyi. "Those two could not work together. I had to make a choice," Zelenskyy said at a press conference. SBU chief Yevhenii Khmara has been named acting defence minister. The move surprised Ukraine's Western partners — EU Defence Commissioner Andrius Kubilius said "we were working with him in a very good way" — and sparked protests in Kyiv, with demonstrators carrying cardboard signs demanding Fedorov's reinstatement, according to Politico's Forecast, the Euractiv Rapporteur, and Reuters. Separately, Vsevolod Chentsov, Ukraine's Brussels ambassador since 2021, has been appointed deputy prime minister for European integration.
