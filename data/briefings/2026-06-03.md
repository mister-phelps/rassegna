# Daily Briefing — Wednesday, 3 June 2026

## European Affairs

**EU frames competitiveness as a workforce problem**
The European Commission published its European Semester Spring Package today, reframing the EU's competitiveness challenge as fundamentally a labour-market problem. For the first time, the recommendations include a dedicated focus on human capital, with Commission Executive Vice President Roxana Mînzatu arguing that "European companies need this perspective and investing in people is the best way to make sure Europe is competitive at the global level." According to [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/), the Commission estimates high energy prices could threaten 560,000 jobs this year in construction, chemicals, metals and transport; the automotive sector faces a loss of roughly 600,000 jobs as manufacturers grapple with electrification and Chinese competition; and a further 150,000 jobs tied to battery, solar and steel projects are at risk. Italy, Bulgaria, Greece, Cyprus, Malta and Romania are singled out as facing the toughest challenges on job quality and adult learning.

**EU tech sovereignty package unveiled amid US warnings**
EU tech chief Henna Virkkunen unveiled the long-awaited tech sovereignty package today, aiming to reduce Europe's digital dependence on the US and Asia. As [Euractiv's Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/newsletter/rapporteur/) reports, Brussels wants to attract Taiwanese chipmaker TSMC to expand its European footprint while encouraging domestic companies to reduce reliance on US cloud providers. The European Parliament is separately preparing to replace Google as its primary search engine with French privacy-focused alternative Qwant. US Ambassador to the EU Andrew Puzder has warned the package could be seen as protectionist and further strain transatlantic trade relations; Politico notes the Commission is pressing ahead regardless.

**EPP moves to shield MEP in Huawei lobbying probe**
The European Parliament's legal affairs committee voted today on whether to lift the immunity of MEPs linked to Belgium's criminal investigation into Huawei's lobbying activities. According to [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/newsletter/rapporteur/), the EPP is attempting to shield Salvatore De Meo, one of its top deputies, who denies wrongdoing; the centre-right group is reportedly working to persuade enough MEPs to vote down the committee's recommendation. Other MEPs facing immunity waivers include the EPP's Fulvio Martusciello — considered a key figure in the probe — Renew's Nikola Minchev, and Socialist Daniel Attard, all of whom deny wrongdoing. The committee has not recommended lifting Attard's immunity after Belgian prosecutors' mistaken-identity error was identified. The vote comes as immunity proceedings become increasingly entangled in party politics, following last month's controversial rejection of a request to lift the immunity of EPP member Angelika Niebler.

**Hungary-Ukraine thaw opens path on EU accession**
Hungarian opposition leader Péter Magyar said he is ready to meet Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy early next week to finalise an agreement on the rights of Hungary's minority in Transcarpathia — a key hurdle to advancing Ukraine's EU accession bid. As both [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/) and [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/newsletter/rapporteur/) report, technical negotiations are progressing on linguistic, educational and cultural rights for the minority, with Budapest expecting Kyiv to codify an eleven-point proposal. Unlike his predecessor Viktor Orbán, Magyar has linked support for Ukraine's accession to concrete guarantees on minority rights rather than opposing it outright. The European Commission is expected to propose opening the first negotiating cluster for Ukraine and Moldova on 16 June.

**EU moves to shore up Armenia ahead of elections**
The European Commission is stepping up support for Armenia as the country's pro-EU government approaches parliamentary elections on Sunday. According to [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/), EU Enlargement Commissioner Marta Kos met Armenian Foreign Minister Ararat Mirzoyan on Tuesday and agreed to accelerate trade and energy links; Commission President Ursula von der Leyen is expected to speak with Prime Minister Nikol Pashinyan as soon as Wednesday. Options under discussion include measures to help Armenia reduce its dependency on Russian gas and diversify import and export routes with the EU, as well as supporting negotiations with Turkey to reopen the Armenian-Turkish border — closed for nearly three decades. The Kremlin has been reported to be threatening disruption to Armenia's natural gas supplies and potential interference in the elections.

**Air passenger rights reform stalls in overnight talks**
Co-legislators failed to reach agreement on the air passenger rights (AirPax) reform in overnight negotiations that ran until 5am, as [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/) reports. Representatives from the Council and Parliament agreed to resume talks at 1pm on Wednesday, pushing back Tuesday's deadline.

## Economy & Markets

**OECD warns of recession risk if Iran war continues**
The OECD published its new Economic Outlook today, sharply downgrading the global growth forecast and warning of recession risk if the conflict in Iran prolongs. As [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/03/plusieurs-pays-pourraient-entrer-en-recession-en-2027-si-la-guerre-en-iran-se-prolonge/) reports in detail, the OECD projects global growth of 2.8% in 2026 (versus 3.4% last year) if the conflict ends by mid-2026; if fighting and Strait of Hormuz disruptions persist through 2027, growth could fall to 2.1% this year and 1.8% next year, with some major economies entering recession. The OECD describes a prolonged Hormuz closure as potentially the largest global economic slowdown in 40 years, excluding the pandemic and the 2008 financial crisis. Natural gas prices have already surged 43% in Europe and 81% in Asia since hostilities began on 27 February, while energy-driven inflation has risen in the eurozone and several Latin American economies. OECD Chief Economist Stefano Scarpetta said the war has become "the determining factor in the global economic outlook" in just three months.

**EU solar glut exposes grid and storage gap**
Writing in [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/02/come-evitare-di-scottarsi-col-solare/), economist Mario Seminerio offers a detailed dissection of the structural contradictions in European solar expansion. Europe now has approximately 490 gigawatts of installed solar capacity, but roughly 40 terawatt-hours will be curtailed this summer — equivalent to Greater London's annual electricity consumption and 25% more than in 2025 — because grids cannot absorb the output. Negative wholesale electricity prices have exploded: Spain logged 423 hours below zero in the first months of 2026 alone, more than triple the year-earlier figure. Seminerio argues the core problem is that European policy has accelerated generation for a decade without building the storage and grid infrastructure to receive it. Battery costs have fallen over 90% since 2010 and storage investment is accelerating, but over 1,700 gigawatts of renewable capacity remain stuck in connection queues across 16 EU member states plus the UK. A further complication: over 90% of the global lithium-iron-phosphate battery supply chain is controlled by China, meaning a renewable transition built on batteries could replicate Europe's fossil fuel dependency in a different geography.

**Trump threatens to reimpose 10% tariffs on EU and Canada**
The Trump administration has proposed reimposing a 10% tariff on top trading partners including the European Union and Canada, citing concerns about forced labour, according to [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/). The US Trade Representative's Office included the proposals in a report released late Tuesday. The move adds fresh uncertainty to already fragile EU-US trade relations, particularly as Brussels presses ahead with its tech sovereignty agenda that Washington regards as protectionist.

## Foreign Policy & Geopolitics

**Gulf conflict: missile failures and Zelenskyy at G7**
The Gulf conflict saw further developments overnight. According to [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/), two Iranian missiles aimed at Kuwait fell short or broke apart in flight; several ballistic missiles aimed at other regional targets failed; and three missiles heading for Bahrain were intercepted. Separately, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy is expected to attend the G7 summit in France later this month, where leaders are set to discuss protection against ballistic missile attacks, according to three officials cited by Politico.

**Russian advances in Ukraine near standstill**
[Le Grand Continent's](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/02/la-progression-russe-en-ukraine-est-desormais-quasi-nulle-1/) daily Ukraine battlefield monitor, updated on 2 June, reports that Russian territorial advances have fallen to near zero — a significant shift from earlier in the conflict. The assessment aligns with broader analysis suggesting the Kremlin may be manoeuvring to manage the optics of strategic failure.
