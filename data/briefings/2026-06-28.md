# Daily Briefing — Sunday, 28 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**Frontier AI access becomes a strategic fault line.** Writing in [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/28/lacces-a-lia-de-frontiere-est-devenu-un-privilege-strategique/), Victor Storchan argues that access to frontier artificial intelligence has shifted from a presumed public good to a guarded strategic privilege, pointing to OpenAI's deferral of the public rollout of GPT-5.6 and the United States' tightly gated restoration of Anthropic's most capable models to "trusted" American organisations only. According to Le Grand Continent, Europe now faces three broad trajectories: dependence on a single dominant outside supplier, a negotiated "pax silica" status as a trusted-but-subordinate partner, or a sovereign continental push — on the order of €500 billion, by the piece's estimate — to build genuinely independent frontier capacity. Additional context via Reuters: the Technology Roundup newsletter corroborates the underlying facts, confirming that the US has allowed Anthropic to release its Mythos AI model to "trusted" US organisations, that Washington is restricting OpenAI's GPT-5.6 rollout while seeking early access for itself, and that the US is reportedly close to allowing Anthropic to restore its Fable 5 model — details Le Grand Continent's piece cites as evidence of a hardening two-tier global AI order that currently leaves Europe on the outside.

## UK Politics

**Andy Burnham prepares economic prospectus as succession nears.** Burnham delivers a speech in Manchester tomorrow setting out his economic priorities ahead of an expected confirmation as prime minister within weeks, according to the Politico newsletter. A trail of the speech, reported in the Sunday Mirror and relayed by Politico, has him promising to "rewire Britain" by putting devolution "at the heart of Downing Street" — including letting combined authorities keep all of any uplift in business rates they generate, alongside a significant expansion of mayoral powers. Politico also reports that close Burnham ally Louise Haigh has proposed a "new fiscal framework" for Britain, including bringing capital gains tax rates closer to income tax, while criticising the "imperial" Treasury, as the Sunday Telegraph's splash detailed. Housing Secretary Steve Reed told Sky News the devolution push amounted only to a "change of emphasis" consistent with Labour's 2024 manifesto, though the Financial Times — cited by Politico — reports that both the Conservatives and Reform UK see scope to cast it as an "anti-south" policy. A YouGov poll relayed by Politico found 48 percent of voters want a general election once Burnham takes office, against 35 percent opposed, even as Labour figures publicly play down the prospect.

**Chancellor speculation continues to swirl around Ed Miliband.** Labour deputy leader Lucy Powell told the BBC's Laura Kuenssberg she believes the energy secretary "would" make a good chancellor, as the Politico newsletter reports. The Sunday Times' Josh Glancy, writing in a long-read flagged by both Politico and the Times' own Editor's Choice newsletter, examines deep splits within Burnham's team over a Miliband appointment: supporters point to his experience and ideological alignment with Burnham, while critics cite the political "baggage" he has accumulated over three decades. The Sun on Sunday, via Politico, counters that Work and Pensions Secretary Pat McFadden remains in contention as a "safe pair of hands." Separately, the Times' Ben Taylor notes that foreign secretary Yvette Cooper has given an early signal of what a Burnham-era foreign policy might look like in an interview with Lara Spirit, alongside her account of where Sir Keir Starmer's premiership went wrong.

**A further £1bn has been found for the stalled Defence Investment Plan**, the row that prompted John Healey's resignation as defence secretary, Politico's Sunday Crunch reports. The Sunday Times, cited by Politico, reports the new money will go chiefly towards drones and autonomous vehicles rather than replacing the Royal Navy's ageing fleet. Former defence chief Tony Radakin, writing in the Sunday Times, was sharply critical of the scale of the increase, saying Britain is "bottom of the table" on NATO spending commitments and "propped up only by Iceland."

## Economy & Markets

**Russia's public finances edge towards a breaking point**, according to Phastidio's [Rubli, cannoni e pagherò](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/22/rubli-cannoni-e-paghero/). Mario Seminerio writes that Russia's budget deficit reached 2.6 percent of GDP in 2024, well above the 1.6 percent originally forecast, with debt servicing now consuming around 9 percent of federal spending; the central bank, he notes, is struggling to keep monetary policy tight enough to contain inflation while the state finances an expanding war effort partly through liquidation of its sovereign wealth fund. China now accounts for roughly 35 percent of Russian foreign trade, a dependency Seminerio frames as both a lifeline and a long-term vulnerability for Moscow.

**Germany's pension reform risks repeating Italy's mistakes**, Phastidio argues in [Pensioni tedesche, willkommen in Italien](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/23/pensioni-tedesche-willkommen-in-italien/). The Merz government's proposed reform would raise the retirement age in stages — to 67.5, then 68, then 70 by 2041, 2051 and 2091 respectively — and Seminerio estimates the cost of plugging Germany's 2024 pension deficit alone at roughly €118 billion. He predicts the reform will follow what he calls the "Italian path" of recrimination and political retreat once its costs become apparent to voters.

## Foreign Policy

**The US and Iran continue trading strikes as the ceasefire frays.** Iran launched missiles and drones at US military sites in Kuwait and Bahrain, while the US confirmed it struck ten targets near the Strait of Hormuz in retaliation for an Iranian drone attack on a Panama-flagged tanker, [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/us-carries-out-fresh-strikes-against-iran-after-tanker-struck-hormuz-escalating-2026-06-27/) reports. Iran said it had struck "US-linked targets" in response, and the US carried out a [further, separate round of strikes](https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/us-conducts-further-strikes-iran-2026-06-27/) on Iranian territory, according to Reuters. The UAE held a rare call with Iran to stress the importance of Hormuz security amid the escalation, Reuters adds. Politico's Sunday Crunch corroborates the picture, reporting that the renewed US strikes put the interim ceasefire deal "at risk of collapse."

**Hezbollah has rejected a US-brokered Israel–Lebanon security deal as a "surrender,"** even as Israel and Lebanon separately signed an initial agreement following US-mediated talks, Reuters reports. Israel's military says it killed Hezbollah militants and struck a launcher in the Nabatieh area, while Lebanon's government says Israeli strikes have damaged heritage sites across the south of the country.

**Russian search-engine data point to rising domestic anxiety over drone defence**, according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/27/en-russie-linteret-pour-lachat-de-systemes-anti-drones-sur-internet-est-au-plus-haut-depuis-2023/). The outlet finds that weekly Yandex searches for anti-drone systems have topped 8,000 — a 35 percent increase — with Russian companies estimated to have spent over $1 billion on such systems, including Pantsir SMD-E units installed on rooftops in Moscow's Sokolniki district, which Le Grand Continent frames as a sign of growing unease about the reach of Ukrainian drone strikes.
