# Daily Briefing — Friday, 3 July 2026

## EU Affairs

**NATO summit countdown: defence spending crunch dominates Brussels agenda**

EU leaders and NATO allies are bracing for next week's summit in Ankara, with European defence spending at the top of the agenda amid ongoing uncertainty over the US alliance. According to the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/), German Chancellor Friedrich Merz is meeting today with the leaders of Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania to discuss European security and support for Ukraine. All NATO countries bar Spain have committed to spending 5 per cent of GDP on defence by 2035, though some allies are pressing for a faster timeline. Estonian Prime Minister Kristen Michal told the Playbook: "The question is no longer whether Europe should do more for its own security. The question is how quickly we can deliver." NATO members excluding the US spent an additional €139 billion this year, and officials say "double-digit" billions in new procurement deals are planned for Ankara. The Playbook notes that just a single three-hour session is planned at the summit, partly to keep President Trump engaged — though his overnight social media post lamenting the size of America's NATO spending suggests his attention may not be the problem. The friendship between Washington and Europe has been strained after Europeans hesitated to join US attacks on Iran, with Washington announcing plans to withdraw troops and capabilities from Europe in response. Ottawa hopes to use the Ankara summit to announce a global defence bank with around 10 founding countries, with only Luxembourg publicly on board so far.

**MFF marathon: Ireland's presidency braces for the "five-shirter"**

Negotiations over the EU's proposed €2 trillion long-term budget are shaping up to be among the most gruelling in the bloc's history, according to both the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/rapporteur/) and the Politico Brussels Playbook. Irish Taoiseach Micheál Martin, hosting Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in Cork for the traditional start-of-presidency visit, described the challenge as "reconciling the irreconcilable", recalling that the 2020 pandemic budget negotiations ran to five days and four nights — EU diplomatic shorthand for a "five-shirter". Council President António Costa has privately warned leaders he is prepared to keep them in Brussels for five days this December if necessary, according to two diplomats cited by Euractiv. Budget Commissioner Piotr Serafin cautioned that a leaner budget would not be a more modern one, warning that newer spending priorities — including defence — risk being cut first; a diplomat from a net-contributor country pushed back, dismissing this as "intellectually lazy" and calling for cuts elsewhere to bolster security and competitiveness. The Irish presidency is drawing up a negotiating box for October, when the European Council is expected to hold a two-day summit focused on the defence-MFF dossier — a rare two-day format that signals the scale of the task ahead.

**Liberals gather in Vienna amid Renew fractures and veto reform debate**

Europe's liberals are meeting in Vienna for ALDE's annual congress, where hundreds of delegates will vote on 17 resolutions — including a "pooled veto" proposal that would require a coalition of at least five member states representing 5 per cent of EU population to block foreign policy decisions, according to the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/). The proposal is in part a response to Viktor Orbán's legacy of blocking Ukraine policy, and is also designed to ease concerns about veto powers slowing EU enlargement. The congress arrives at a difficult moment for Renew Europe: divisions over the EU's return regulation saw 37 Renew MEPs vote in favour, 21 against and 12 abstain; and Belgian liberal leader Georges-Louis Bouchez — who has floated the idea of joining the EPP — cancelled his Vienna trip two days before the event. German MEP Svenja Hahn, expected to be re-elected ALDE president unopposed, told the Playbook she understands Bouchez's concerns on economic reform, but the episode illustrates a grouping drifting apart.

**Kallas proposes new Russia sanctions after Kyiv attack**

EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas has proposed a new round of sanctions targeting Russia's military industries following a Russian missile and drone attack on Kyiv that killed at least 27–30 people, according to the Politico Brussels Playbook and the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing. Rescuers were clearing rubble on Friday as flags flew at half-mast across Ukraine. The Playbook further reveals that a hotel where European Parliament staff had been staying earlier in the week was struck, with one missile hitting what had been a staffer's room.

## Italian Politics

**Vannacci challenges Meloni from the nationalist right**

As Giorgia Meloni enters what could be the final stretch of one of Italy's longest-lasting governments, the most potent challenge to her dominance may come not from the fragmented centre-left but from within her own nationalist base. Roberto Vannacci — the anti-EU general-turned-MEP polling at around 5 per cent — has told the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/rapporteur/) that Meloni has "sold out" Italy's nationalist movement. Vannacci sits with the Europe of Sovereign Nations group alongside Germany's AfD, and likens his supporters to The Dirty Dozen: "Meloni will need the Italians who want real change. Today, that space is us." In Italy's coalition arithmetic, Vannacci could become an inconvenient — and potentially indispensable — player after the next election.

## Economy & Markets

**Germany's coalition unveils €10bn fiscal package — critics see small print**

The German coalition government has agreed a broad package of tax, labour and pension reforms, with Chancellor Friedrich Merz presenting the measures as a stimulus to economic growth, according to the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/rapporteur/), the Politico Brussels Playbook, and a detailed analysis by [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/07/02/riforma-fiscale-tedesca-italien-wir-kommen/). The package includes €10 billion in net income tax relief concentrated on lower-to-middle earners; a lowering of the *Reichensteuer* threshold from roughly 280,000 to 250,000 euros with a new 47 per cent rate above 280,000; a reform of sick-note rules requiring in-person certification from day one of absence; reduced corporate reporting requirements; and extended Sunday opening hours for bars and bakeries. Phastidio.net cautions that the €10bn figure is sharply reduced from SPD proposals of 17–27 billion, and that without a constitutional automatic inflation adjustment, much of the tax relief may amount to no more than a partial restitution of fiscal drag rather than a genuine stimulus. Trade unions have criticised the sick-note measure; the German doctors' association warns of "catastrophic" consequences. Opposition parties — from the hard left (Greens) to the far right (AfD) — have attacked the package from opposite directions, the former calling it "inutilely vexatious", the latter dismissing it as a "dead cat" against a backdrop of over €1 trillion in total tax revenue.

**Lagarde signals possible early ECB exit for the French presidential race**

ECB President Christine Lagarde has declined to rule out leaving the central bank before her term ends in late 2027 to weigh in on French politics, according to the [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/newsletter/rapporteur/) and the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing. Speaking at the Rencontres Économiques d'Aix, she told *Les Echos*: "It's possible. I think a European voice should be heard in the French presidential debate." While insisting a presidential bid is "not currently on the agenda", she said she would step in if France's place in Europe came under challenge. The comments came alongside declared or potential 2027 contenders including Gabriel Attal — who held his first major Paris rally on Friday — Édouard Philippe, Dominique de Villepin, Bruno Le Maire, Xavier Bertrand and François Hollande.

**Russian advance slows; Putin faces mobilisation dilemma as economy sours**

Russian forces conquered 770 km² of Ukrainian territory in the first half of 2026 — 2.5 times less than the 1,830 km² taken over the same period in 2025 — according to [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/01/larmee-russe-a-conquis-770-km%c2%b2-de-territoire-ukrainien-au-premier-semestre-soit-25-fois-moins-quen-2025/), citing the Ukrainian analysts Deep State. June saw a partial rebound to 84 km² (up from 14 km² in May), with advances near Sloviansk and Kostiantynivka in Donetsk — where the Kremlin has now missed 15 self-imposed capture deadlines since 2022. Two realities coexist on the front, Le Grand Continent notes: Ukraine is striking deeper into Russia with growing drone mass, yet Russian forces continue to advance slowly at enormous human and material cost; Russian nationalist commentators on Telegram are meanwhile calling on Putin to abandon US-mediated talks and order mass mobilisation. Russian sources cited by Le Grand Continent suggest a new mobilisation wave could occur in October, after September's legislative elections. The home front is also deteriorating: a new [Gallup poll cited by Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/07/02/economie-russe-une-majorite-estime-que-les-conditions-locales-se-deteriorent/) finds 60 per cent of Russians believe local economic conditions are worsening — the highest level of pessimism since 2006, exceeding pandemic highs — while trust in the military fell 13 percentage points this year to 66 per cent, in the government by 14 points to 53 per cent, and confidence in election integrity by 16 points to 40 per cent. Zelensky has separately appealed to the United States to allow Ukraine to produce its own Patriot interceptor missiles, following the missile strike on Kyiv that killed at least 27–30 people, as reported by The Times and the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing.
