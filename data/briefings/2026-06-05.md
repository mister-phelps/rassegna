## EU Affairs

**EU-Western Balkans Summit: Montenegro Positioned as Frontrunner, Franco-German Enlargement Overhaul on the Table**

EU leaders gathered in Tivat, Montenegro for the EU-Western Balkans summit, which was overshadowed by a security incident: Montenegro barred 87 Serbs from entering on a charter flight, citing "national security" risks and alleging a plot to disrupt proceedings, as the [Politico Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/) reported from the scene. Montenegro's Prime Minister Milojko Spajić told Politico his country is the "frontrunner" to become the EU's 28th member by 2028. France and Germany tabled a joint non-paper proposing to overhaul the accession process by offering candidate countries observer status and partial access to the single market before full membership — as [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/newsletter/rapporteur/) reports, fleshing out Chancellor Merz's earlier references to "associate membership." European Council President António Costa told both outlets that "there is a new willingness to simplify and accelerate the process," though the Franco-German paper notably lacks any political commitment to help Montenegro secure unanimous backing from all 27 member states. Serbia's Vučić attended despite warnings from Belgrade's intelligence agency; the EU is also threatening to cut Serbia's access to funds over rule-of-law concerns. Macron was the first French head of state to visit Montenegro since diplomatic relations were established; French construction group Bouygues signed a €300 million contract to build a university hospital in Podgorica on the sidelines.

**EU Countries Race to Identify "Return Hub" Host Nations by Autumn**

What was once a political taboo is rapidly becoming mainstream European policy. A coalition of five EU countries — the Netherlands, Denmark, Germany, Austria, and Greece — has spent months exploring possible models and locations for facilities outside the EU to house rejected asylum seekers awaiting deportation. Dutch Migration Minister Bart van den Brink told [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com) that the group hopes to name prospective host countries by autumn and that reaching "some form of agreement" by year-end is "the ambition." The Nordic countries, led by Sweden, are coordinating a separate effort focused on nationality-specific arrangements. A further group is examining a pilot reception-centre scheme. The Commission has declined to lead the talks. As van den Brink stressed, the facilities would not be detention centres but places where migrants "can stay for a time that they want, and then, they can go." [The Times](https://www.thetimes.co.uk) also noted Germany's role in driving the push, which follows this week's tentative Parliament-Council agreement on new deportation rules.

**Germany Suffers Humiliating Defeat in UN Security Council Ballot**

Germany failed on 3 June to secure one of the two non-permanent seats on the UN Security Council allocated to the Western Europe and Others group — the first time Berlin has lost such a vote. Portugal received 134 votes and Austria 131; Germany secured only 104, short of the 127 needed. As [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/05/pourquoi-lallemagne-a-perdu-le-vote-pour-sieger-au-conseil-de-securite-de-lonu/) reports, observers broadly attribute the result to Berlin's strong support for Israel after October 2023 and its refusal to condemn Israeli military operations in Gaza and the West Bank. The defeat is a blow to Chancellor Merz, who has staked much of his international positioning on Germany's geopolitical return; his foreign minister had personally lobbied some 80 delegations before the vote. The result was also flagged by [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/newsletter/rapporteur/).

**EEAS Faces Simultaneous Departure of Three Top Officials**

The EU diplomatic service posted vacancies for three of its most senior roles simultaneously, [Politico Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/playbook/) reports. Secretary-General Belén Martínez Carbonell is departing after less than two years on the job to lead the EU delegation in Mexico. Deputy Secretary-Generals Charles Fries and Matti Maasikas may also be leaving. The application deadline of 18 June is, an EU official noted, unusually short. The previous secretary-general, Stefano Sannino, was detained by Belgian police last year in an EPPO-led fraud probe.

**Brussels Hints at New Trade Tool Against China**

Denis Redonnet, the EU Commission's chief trade enforcement officer, said on Thursday that the "very, very intense distortions" created by China's economy meant that "traditional trade instruments will inevitably, at some point, reach their limit," as [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/section/politics/newsletter/rapporteur/) reports. The remarks came as Trade Commissioner Maroš Šefčovič met China's vice-minister for commerce in Paris; Šefčovič said his objective was "a practical, result-oriented approach — not escalation. But any path forward needs to be meaningful and sustainable."

## Italian Politics

**Italy's Youth Unemployment "Record" Is a Statistical Illusion**

Writing on [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/05/basta-qualche-ciliegia-e-la-disoccupazione-va-giu/), economist Mario Seminerio demolishes the claim — advanced in a high-profile op-ed in *Il Messaggero* — that Italy's youth unemployment rate has fallen more sharply than anywhere else in the EU. Istat data for April 2025–April 2026 shows that while the number of unemployed 15-to-24-year-olds did fall by 63,000, the number of young people classified as economically inactive — those who have stopped looking for work altogether — rose by 134,000, lifting the inactivity rate by 1.9 percentage points. The number of employed young people actually fell by 40,000. "We can aim for the galactic record for the fewest unemployed," Seminerio writes, "it is enough that they all become inactive."
