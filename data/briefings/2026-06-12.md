# Daily Briefing — Friday, 12 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**MFF Nego Box Triggers Net Contributor Backlash — Leaders Told to "Step Up"**

The Cypriot Council presidency on Thursday released its long-awaited "negotiating box" for the EU's next seven-year budget, proposing a €1.73 trillion Multiannual Financial Framework — down from the €1.76 trillion the European Commission had tabled last July. As [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com) reports, the largest cuts fall on the European Competitiveness Fund and the EU's external action spending pot (roughly €28 billion each), while agriculture and cohesion funding are largely preserved in a structural change that merges traditional policy streams. The proposal immediately drew a broadside from net contributing countries: the Dutch Finance Minister Eelco Heinen and Sweden's EU affairs minister Jessica Rosencrantz both declared it a "no-go box," and one diplomat described it to the Politico Playbook newsletter as looking "more like a crash site" than a landing zone. The Politico Playbook notes the deeper concern: officials have reached the limit of what they can negotiate among themselves, and there is mounting fear that a deal could slip into 2027, when France, Italy, Greece, Poland, and Spain all face election campaigns. "Now it's the leaders' turn — they need to step up," one diplomat said. EU ambassadors will hold a special session on Sunday, ministers will take over at next week's General Affairs Council, and leaders will confront the issue at the European Council summit on 18–19 June. Separately, European Council President António Costa has written to leaders warning that no deal can be reached [without agreement on new EU own resources](https://www.euractiv.com) — effectively new taxes to top up the budget.

**Kallas' "Apartheid" Remarks Deepen EU Foreign Policy Crisis**

In an exclusive, [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com) reveals that EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas compared Israel's treatment of Palestinians to South Africa's apartheid era during high-level talks in Mexico City — a departure from the EU's official foreign policy line. The remarks, which Kallas' office declined to confirm or deny, were relayed to Euractiv by officials and diplomats present. "The EU is critical of Israel and supports a two-state solution. The comparison with apartheid is unacceptable and not EU policy. It is a big problem if she is making these kinds of statements while officially representing the EU on the world stage," one EU diplomat told the outlet. The disclosure comes at a particularly fraught moment for the EEAS: earlier this week, Kallas moved to defend the service after reports emerged that French officials had been exploring ways to restructure it — prompting what Euractiv described as a near institutional "near-death experience" resolved within a single news cycle. Kallas also continues to struggle to secure Council support for further measures against Israel, including trade restrictions on West Bank settlements and sanctions on Israeli National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir.

**ECB Raises Interest Rates for First Time in Nearly Three Years**

The European Central Bank has raised interest rates in a bid to curb inflation driven by the US-Israeli war with Iran, [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com) reports. "The war in the Middle East is generating inflation pressures, and the decision to raise rates is robust across a range of scenarios mapping out how the shock might evolve and affect the medium-term outlook for the euro area," ECB President Christine Lagarde told reporters. The move marks the first rate increase in almost three years and comes against the backdrop of surging fertiliser prices in Europe — a second such shock in five years — as geopolitical disruption hits agricultural supply chains.

**EU Migration Pact Enters Into Force Today**

The EU's flagship migration overhaul formally entered into force on Friday, as [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com) notes, ending years of political wrangling. Several member states are struggling to meet the pact's legal and technical requirements, with Hungary identified as the biggest laggard. Migration Commissioner Magnus Brunner told Euractiv: "There are sticks and carrots in the pact. So, you get funding, you get money, only if you apply the pact."

## Italian Politics

**Italy Plans Major Military Expansion**

Italy is preparing a wide-ranging reform of its armed forces that would introduce a new multi-layered national reserve system and increase military personnel by up to 40,000 by 2033, bringing total numbers to over 200,000, [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com) reports. The plan will be rolled out in stages and forms part of a broader European trend towards increased defence capacity amid growing continental security concerns.

## Economy & Markets

**SpaceX IPO Makes Musk the World's First Trillionaire**

SpaceX debuted on public markets on Friday with a valuation that could exceed $2 trillion, propelling Elon Musk to become the world's first trillionaire and potentially making around 4,400 of the company's Texas-based employees millionaires, as [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/12/au-texas-lintroduction-en-bourse-de-spacex-pourrait-rendre-plusieurs-centaines-demployes-millionnaires/) reports. [Reuters Business](https://www.reuters.com) describes Musk as "dictating terms at every stage" of the IPO process — from allocation to pricing — leaving investors clamouring for stock while concerns about risk were largely set aside. The [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com) notes that SpaceX's record-breaking flotation has already sparked discussion about whether it will fuel IPO ambitions among Chinese space companies, where a significant technological gap remains.

**European Carmakers Urge EU "Made in Europe" Rules**

Volkswagen, Stellantis, and Renault — together accounting for around 60% of Europe's car output — are urging the EU to adopt a simple "Made in Europe" rule and offer stronger incentives to boost local production, the [Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing](https://www.reuters.com) reports. The call reflects escalating concerns among European manufacturers about their competitive position as trade tensions and supply chain disruptions intensify.

## Foreign Policy

**Global Migration Has Tripled Since 2000 — Europe the Top Destination**

A study published Wednesday in *Nature* and reported by [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/12/les-migrations-ont-triple-depuis-le-debut-du-siecle/) finds that annual global migration flows have nearly tripled over the past quarter century, rising from roughly 13 million people per year in 2000 to 35 million in 2023. Europe was the largest destination in the most recent year, receiving more than 7 million arrivals — over half from within Europe itself, primarily from Germany and the United Kingdom. The study attributes the surge in part to a doubling of armed conflicts since the early 2010s (from under 90 to 195 active conflicts last year) and to the accelerating effects of climate change. Flows remain predominantly regional, governed by geographical proximity and family ties rather than long-distance movement.
