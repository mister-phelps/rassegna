# Daily Briefing — Thursday, 11 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**Cyprus presidency tables MFF "nego box" with 2% cut — and a looming clash over the frugal camp**
The Cyprus Council presidency has unveiled the first "negotiating box" for the EU's next seven-year budget (2028–2034), proposing a 2 per cent reduction to the Commission's spending plan, according to five EU diplomats cited by the [Politico Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/). The cuts are expected to fall mainly on the European Competitiveness Fund (a €410 billion pot) and the Global Europe development fund, while farmers' subsidies and regional payments — together accounting for less than half of the near-€2 trillion budget — are expected to be spared, a blow to the northern frugal camp which has demanded cuts of up to 15 per cent. Cyprus is walking a tightrope between 16 countries pushing for more agricultural and regional spending and the rival frugal alliance. European Council President António Costa has added pressure by tying a year-end budget deal to progress on new EU own resources — fresh taxes — calling these "decisive", according to a letter to national governments seen by [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/). Finance ministers gathering in Luxembourg on Thursday are also expected to challenge the Commission's decision to exempt green investments from the bloc's spending rules, Politico reports.

**A "temporary" membership format for enlarged EU gains traction**
France, Germany, the Netherlands, Belgium and Luxembourg are pushing for a transitional accession arrangement that would deny voting rights to newly admitted member states, according to a document seen by [Euractiv](https://www.euractiv.com/). The proposal — designed to allow the EU to absorb strategically important neighbours without exposing its decision-making to new vetoes — is gaining momentum ahead of next week's European Council. Dutch Foreign Minister Tom Berendsen told Euractiv: "Enlargement is a strategic tool, but only if it makes the EU stronger, not weaker." Costa's invitation letter, obtained by [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/), also welcomed a "renewed impetus" on enlargement, with Ukraine and Moldova both expected to advance their accession paths at the summit. The [Le Grand Continent piece](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/09/ukraine-californie/) by economist Yuriy Gorodnichenko argues that Ukraine has become Europe's equivalent of California — the most important investment the EU could make — urging that enlargement be treated as a strategic priority rather than a bureaucratic process.

**Last MEP in Huawei scandal fights to keep his immunity**
Italian Forza Italia MEP and group leader Fulvio Martusciello is the last of four MEPs facing the prospect of losing his parliamentary immunity in the Belgian investigation into alleged bribery involving Huawei lobbyist Valerio Ottati, [Politico's Brussels Playbook](https://www.politico.eu/newsletter/brussels-playbook/) reports. Martusciello has compiled a dossier arguing that prosecutors' case is built on "carelessness" and "superficiality," denying any relationship with Ottati, offering a Ryanair boarding pass and photos on Prague's Charles Bridge as an alibi for one alleged meeting, and rejecting claims he received €15,000 from Huawei. MEPs will vote on his immunity on Tuesday. Broader irritation with Belgian authorities is growing after a string of procedural blunders in the wider probe, including wrongly attributing actions to an MEP with the same name as another.

**Ireland launches its EU presidency pitch**
Taoiseach Micheál Martin set out Ireland's upcoming Council presidency priorities on Wednesday under the motto *Ní neart go cur le chéile* ("there's nothing of strength without unity"), according to [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/). Priorities include competitiveness, values and security. Martin indicated a realistic — rather than definitive — ambition on the MFF, saying Ireland would "seek to secure a solid basis" for negotiations. On enlargement, he said Ireland would aim to get Montenegro "very close to the end line by the end of the year." He also called for greater European engagement on housing, and said Ireland will host a housing summit in November. The presidency is estimated to cost between €165 million and €185 million, excluding security.

**Taliban Brussels visit delayed by missing visa applications**
A Taliban delegation invited to Brussels by the European Commission for "technical-level" talks on deportation of Afghan nationals has yet to apply for Belgian visas, [Euractiv Rapporteur](https://www.euractiv.com/) reports. Belgian foreign minister Maxime Prévot's spokesperson confirmed that no applications have been submitted. The talks were intended to focus on the return of convicted criminals and security threats. Belgium, which controls visa issuance for visitors invited by EU institutions, has already launched security checks on prospective delegation members — but is also keen to stress it is not endorsing those invited, while warning that blocking access would "weaken Brussels' position as an international and diplomatic capital." The visit has triggered backlash from MEPs and civil society groups.

## Economy & Markets

**Trade has become warfare again: Ormuz and the end of free navigation**
Writing in [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/10/commerce-guerre-mer/), maritime risk analyst Stéphane Audrand argues that the conflict around the Strait of Hormuz marks the return of a pre-1856 world in which free navigation was the exception, not the rule. He traces the history from the corsair era through Pax Britannica and the US Navy's post-1945 guarantee of maritime security, noting that both the United States and China have now effectively withdrawn from the role of benevolent guarantor. "When goods stop crossing borders, armies start," Audrand quotes Cordell Hull, arguing that Europe faces questions it has not had to answer in generations: whether to create a pan-European commercial flag, whether to arm merchant vessels, and whether to accept that some maritime risks are now simply uninsurable. The article notes that Iran's current toll on shipping through Ormuz — roughly $1 per barrel, about 1% of crude price — mirrors the historic "Sound Dues" that Denmark charged for Baltic passage, abolished in 1857 as a "simple racket." Audrand concludes that Europeans must relearn to trade "with a rifle on their shoulder," drawing on their unique historical experience of maritime commerce under conditions of insecurity. Le Grand Continent's homepage also flags a separate analysis arguing the Iran war will cause [greater economic losses for Europe and China than for the United States](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/09/la-guerre-en-iran-devrait-provoquer-plus-de-pertes-economiques-pour-leurope-et-la-chine-que-pour-les-etats-unis/).

**Between socialism and cronyism: the US AI ownership debate**
Writing in [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/11/ai-di-stato-tra-socialismo-e-clientelismo/), Mario Seminerio analyses two competing proposals to bring AI companies under state control. Bernie Sanders has introduced the *American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act*, proposing a one-off 50% tax on the *shares* — not profits — of OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI and others, with the proceeds distributed to citizens. Seminerio argues this almost certainly constitutes a "physical taking" under the Fifth Amendment, which would require the government to pay market value for the expropriated equity — potentially over $1 trillion. Trump's rival scheme, a sovereign wealth fund financed by "voluntary" contributions from AI companies, is criticised as institutionalising cronyism: firms that pay gain political protection, those that don't face regulatory risk. Seminerio notes that the government has already begun acquiring stakes in strategic companies by other means — including an $8.9 billion position in Intel via the CHIPS Act — and warns that both proposals risk socialising losses in a sector whose systemic risks are far from hypothetical.
