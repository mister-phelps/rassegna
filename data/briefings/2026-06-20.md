# Daily Briefing — Saturday, 20 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**Germany's economic weight inside the EU is shrinking, and that vacuum isn't being filled by anyone else.** According to Euractiv, the IMF data show Germany's share of EU output has fallen from more than a quarter to just over a fifth since 1980, even as the EU's own share of global output has halved over the same period. Euractiv links the trend to a string of recent defeats for Berlin: it failed to stop EU tariffs on Chinese EVs, saw its push to use €210 billion in frozen Russian assets blocked by Belgium, and watched its objections to looser fiscal rules overridden to accommodate Italy. One analyst quoted by Euractiv argues the cause is less economic than political — "the lack of political leadership and political initiative in Germany" under Chancellor Friedrich Merz — while others see economic stagnation since 2019 as the deeper driver.

**Brussels is racing to lock down the EU's 2028-34 budget before the political map shifts.** Euractiv reports the Commission is pushing for an enlarged budget of around €2 trillion (1.27% of EU gross national income), but the "frugal" member states want tighter, more targeted spending instead, and have already rejected the first numbers tabled by Cyprus's presidency. New EU-wide levies — on tobacco, emissions trading, or e-waste — remain contested, with several capitals preferring to keep the revenue themselves. The Commission has now warned that continued stalling could force an across-the-board cut of up to 40%, a threat Euractiv frames as an attempt to concentrate minds before talks collapse entirely; the urgency is sharpened by the risk that a far-right win in next year's French elections could see a President Bardella seek to slash France's contribution.

## Economy & Markets

**Italy's long-running argument over how to push household savings into "the real economy" flared up again this week, and the numbers behind it don't all hold up.** Writing on Phastidio, Mario Seminerio takes aim at proposals — echoed by Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni and investor Giovanni Tamburi in a recent op-ed — to direct pension funds and savers more forcefully toward Italian equities and IPOs. Tamburi's complaint that only €40 billion of €260 billion raised from Italian workers reaches "the real economy" has been used to back calls for capital-gains exemptions on newly listed shares and the removal of investment caps on tax-advantaged PIR savings plans. Seminerio disputes the frequently cited comparison with Sweden, noting that its ISK savings scheme carries no minimum equity-investment quota — it is the separate, compulsory Premium Pension scheme that allocates 2.5% of wages to investment funds, and even that excludes private-markets funds. He also notes that the Meloni government itself scrapped Italy's ACE business-capitalisation incentive, the same incentive Tamburi is now calling to reinstate.

**New US Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh wasted no time setting a different tone.** His debut policy meeting this week produced what Reuters Business describes as "a return to stripped-down, 1990s-style central banking," a marked departure from the activist, crisis-management role the Fed has played for much of this century. The shift comes as markets head into a long weekend on edge, with US equity fund inflows already surging on the back of the Iran deal and tech stocks drawing record weekly investment, per Reuters.

## Foreign Policy

**A US-Iran memorandum signed with fanfare at the G7's Versailles dinner is already running into trouble on the follow-through.** Mario Seminerio's Phastidio account describes Donald Trump signing the document on 17 June as Emmanuel Macron's stage-managed closing flourish to the summit, with Iranian president Masoud Pezeshkian's side having already signed electronically days earlier. Phastidio is sharply critical of the deal's substance, noting it sets no quantitative cap on uranium enrichment or centrifuges, contains no missile restrictions, and leaves both the timetable for lifting sanctions and a mooted $300 billion reconstruction fund to a further 60 days of negotiation — far less concrete, it argues, than the 2015 JCPOA it replaces. Reuters, in its Iran Briefing and Daily Briefing newsletters, reports the picture more soberly: a planned Switzerland meeting on the follow-up talks was scrapped and Vice President JD Vance cancelled his trip, leaving "uncertainty hangs over timing" of further negotiations, and Reuters notes that some of Trump's own loyal supporters are among the most unsettled by the pact's terms. Where Phastidio frames the memorandum as a chaotic, face-saving retreat dressed up as victory, Reuters sticks to documenting the stalled logistics and the anxiety it has caused within Trump's own coalition.

**Moscow's war financing is becoming structurally expensive, even though its overall debt load looks modest.** Le Grand Continent reports that Russia is set to spend at least 15% of GDP on debt-interest payments over the next decade — equivalent to its entire current public debt stock — as defence spending overshoots budget by as much as 40%. The debt-service cost has tripled since the 2022 invasion, from 1,300 billion to 3,200 billion roubles a year, even though Russia's debt-to-GDP ratio (around 16%) remains far below Italy's, France's or Spain's; the outlet attributes this to central bank rates still double their pre-war level and long-term bond yields near 15%. Oil and gas revenues, meanwhile, have fallen to their lowest share of GDP since 2018 according to economist Janis Kluge cited by Le Grand Continent, partly offset by a VAT rise to 22%.

**Ukraine's drone campaign against targets inside Russia has intensified sharply, Le Grand Continent reports, with strikes up 13-fold since 2023.** Citing ACLED data and Ukrainian military expert Roman Svitan, the outlet counts at least 156 drone strikes on Russian territory so far this year — more than double last year's pace and quadruple 2024's. A 17-18 June barrage was the largest of the war, with Russia's defence ministry claiming 992 drones shot down; some footage suggests an explosion at a Moscow-area refinery was actually caused by an errant Russian air-defence missile rather than a Ukrainian drone. Russian state television gave the strikes little coverage, focusing instead on a Russia-ASEAN summit in Kazan, according to Le Grand Continent.
