# Daily Briefing — Thursday, 18 June 2026

## EU Affairs

**Leaders meet in Brussels as summit splits over Ukraine's path and how to handle China**
EU leaders gathered in Brussels for a summit officially focused on "global microeconomic imbalances" but, per the Politico Brussels Playbook newsletter, really about China, with one senior diplomat telling the newsletter "there's a mood for a much firmer approach". Ukrainian Deputy Prime Minister Taras Kachka pushed for all accession negotiating clusters to be opened before the summer break, and Hungary's new prime minister Péter Magyar — attending his first summit since Viktor Orbán's departure — allowed the bloc to open Ukraine's first cluster and adopt a 20th package of Russia sanctions, with leaders expected to agree summit conclusions unanimously for the first time in years, according to the Euractiv Rapporteur newsletter. Chancellor Friedrich Merz, who arrived late after a Versailles dinner with Macron and Trump, favours opening talks but sees no actual accession before 2035 and is instead proposing an interim "associate membership" — a term Zelenskyy reportedly dislikes for evoking the pre-Maidan Association Agreement — per the Berlin Playbook newsletter; the idea looks likely to be deferred to an October summit. A separate friction point, also flagged by Berlin Playbook, is a push within Council President António Costa's office to open a contact line with the Kremlin, which is unsettling the informal "E3" format of Germany, France and the UK.

**EU edges toward a new trade-defence instrument as the China deficit hits €360bn a year**
Over the summit dinner, leaders debated mandating the Commission to draft a "country-agnostic" trade-protection instrument — in practice, new tariffs — against Chinese industrial overcapacity, a stance that marks a reversal for Chancellor Merz's Germany, which previously opposed EV tariffs under the Scholz government, according to the Berlin Playbook newsletter. The pressure follows mounting alarm over the trade gap: Manfred Weber put the deficit at "€1bn a day, €360bn a year", as reported by the Euractiv newsletter, while Trade Commissioner Maroš Šefčovič called it "unsustainable", per the Euractiv Red Thread newsletter. Brussels' Industrial Accelerator Act has drawn fierce criticism from Beijing, and the dispute comes against a backdrop of China's own economy cooling, with industrial output up 4.5% year-on-year in May but retail sales down 0.6% — the first contraction since December 2022 — even as exports jumped 19.4%, according to the Euractiv Red Thread newsletter.

**China deepens its foothold in EU member states and candidate countries**
While Brussels' attention is fixed on the summit, Beijing is making quiet gains closer to home, the Euractiv Red Thread newsletter reports. Montenegro closed two more accession chapters this week even as China expands its presence in the Western Balkans; Lithuania has allowed Beijing to reopen a chargé d'affaires office in Vilnius, the first thaw since the 2021 Taiwan dispute; and the European Parliament lifted MEP Fulvio Martusciello's immunity over a Brussels-offices corruption probe linked to Huawei, while colleagues De Meo, Minchev and Attard retained theirs. Separately, China's SAIC Motor is reportedly planning its first European factory near the Ferrol naval base in Galicia, Spain, raising security concerns, and Kaja Kallas remains at odds with Beijing over allegations it trained Russian troops, which Beijing calls "a pure smear".

## Italian Politics

**World Cup failure ignites a political fight over football governance**
Italy's third consecutive failure to qualify for the World Cup has triggered a battle over who controls Italian football, Politico's Forecast newsletter reports. Federation president Gabriele Gravina resigned in April after a playoff defeat to Bosnia-Herzegovina, and Giorgia Meloni's Brothers of Italy is now pushing to cancel the federation's 22 June elections and place it under special administration — a move Gravina has called an attempted "occupation". The favourite to succeed him, former Olympic committee president Giovanni Malagò, is disliked by Meloni's party, which prefers rival candidate Giancarlo Abete; the opposition accuses Meloni of extending a pattern of centralising control already seen at state broadcaster RAI, the financial watchdog and the judiciary. Formal authority to impose special administration rests with the Olympic committee CONI rather than the government, whose last resort is reportedly a conflict-of-interest review targeting Malagò.

**Meloni's coalition splits over Italy's €14.9bn share of the EU's SAFE defence loan**
Italy's governing coalition is divided over how much of the EU's SAFE defence-financing scheme to draw on, according to the Euractiv Rapporteur newsletter: Defence Minister Guido Crosetto wants to press ahead with the full €14.9bn investment, Economy Minister Giancarlo Giorgetti is urging caution given the public finances, and Matteo Salvini's League wants the commitment scaled back.

**Italy's "patriot equity" debate exposes the limits of channelling savings into industry**
A sharp commentary on [Phastidio.net](https://phastidio.net/2026/06/17/patriot-equity-e-risparmi-coscritti/) takes aim at the renewed Italian debate over directing household savings into the "real economy". Commentator Mario Seminerio criticises investor Giovanni Tamburi's proposals — reviving the PIR savings scheme, exempting IPO-related capital gains from tax, cutting dividend taxes, and effectively requiring pension funds to direct a set share of assets into listed or soon-to-be-listed Italian companies — arguing these would be costly for the public finances and rest on a flawed comparison with Sweden, since Sweden's mandatory 2.5% pension-fund equity allocation is a separate mechanism from the voluntary ISK savings scheme Tamburi invoked. The piece, responding in part to Prime Minister Meloni's framing of low real-economy investment as a "scandal", concludes that pension savings are deferred wages rather than a disguised sovereign wealth fund, and that — as with any deal — "it takes two", implying Italian firms' reluctance to list, not a shortage of patriotic capital, is the real obstacle.

## Foreign Policy

**US and Iran sign an interim ceasefire, but Trump threatens to resume strikes**
The US and Iran released the text of a 14-point interim agreement their presidents have signed, with Trump warning he could resume attacks and "go back to shooting at them" if unhappy with how the process unfolds, as Reuters and the Gulf Currents newsletter report. The deal, due to be formally signed in Switzerland on Friday, reopens the Strait of Hormuz and lifts the US naval blockade in exchange for a 60-day negotiating window, a $300bn Reconstruction and Development Fund built from private investment (with over $150bn already committed from the US, Gulf states, South Korea and Japan), and the unfreezing of Iranian assets abroad, according to the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing and Gulf Currents newsletters. [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/17/les-14-points-du-protocole-daccord-entre-les-etats-unis-et-liran-texte-integral-dune-capitulation-americaine/) offers a starkly different framing, arguing in a line-by-line analysis of the 14 points that "none... gives an advantage to the United States, [and] nine are net gains for the Iranian regime" — citing the full lifting of sanctions, the $300bn fund, immediate oil-export waivers, and the eventual lock-in of the deal via a UN Security Council resolution. Tom Perry told the Reuters World News podcast, per the Thomson Reuters Daily Briefing, that the deal could also hand Hezbollah "a lifeline" in Lebanon.

**China emerges as the war's quiet beneficiary, Le Grand Continent argues**
An analysis on [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/18/la-chine-a-t-elle-gagne-la-guerre-en-iran/) contends Beijing may be the war's biggest winner. China still takes roughly 80–90% of Iran's oil exports and, despite its overall crude imports falling by a third in May, suffered no major economic damage; meanwhile it exported some €92bn of batteries, electric vehicles, grid equipment and clean-power capacity in the year's first four months, up from €47bn in 2022, while Gulf officials are reportedly exploring ways to reduce reliance on Washington. The piece also notes the US drew down missile-defence systems from South Korea and Japan to fight the war and burned through munitions whose guidance systems depend on rare earths, of which China controls roughly 70% of mining and 90% of processing.

**Analysis: Europe left marginalised as Middle East war redraws the regional order**
A separate [Le Grand Continent](https://legrandcontinent.eu/fr/2026/06/17/leurope-apres-la-guerre-en-iran/) essay by Guillaume Duval argues that "the United States has failed, the [European] Union is marginalised", with the bloc unable to protect Lebanon, aid Iranian civilians, or take part in the negotiations. Duval frames the war as the EU's third major economic shock in a decade, after Covid-19 and the 2022 energy crisis, and argues this strengthens the case for joint EU borrowing, accelerated decarbonisation to cut fossil-fuel dependence, and a European capacity to project military force at chokepoints such as Hormuz, given doubts over continued US commitment to NATO's Article 5.
